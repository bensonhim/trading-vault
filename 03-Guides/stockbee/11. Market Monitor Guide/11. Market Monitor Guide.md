# Market Monitor Guide

> **Speaker:** Pradeep Bonde (StockBee)  
> **Series:** Market Monitor Guide (8 parts)  
> **Transcripts:** [[../../transcripts/11. Market Monitor Guide/|Transcripts]]

## Summary

The Market Monitor is a **market breadth-based timing tool** that answers three critical questions: (1) Is money flowing into or out of the market? (2) Should I trade bullish breakouts or bearish shorts? (3) Is the market nearing a major bottom? At its core is the **primary indicator** — a quarterly breadth measure (stocks up 25% in 65 days) that functions as a traffic light: green = aggressive long, red = short. When the primary indicator drops below 200, it signals **capitulation** — a generational buying opportunity. The monitor is built from daily TC2000 scans (4% breakouts/breakdowns, quarterly/monthly momentum, 50% monthly extremes) that are manually entered into a color-coded spreadsheet. Secondary indicators (5/10-day ratios, monthly 25%/50%, Fibonacci 34×13, T2108) add context but are not core. A unique bubble signal comes from tracking the **total number of stocks trading** — dramatic IPO surges (5,000→7,000) preceded the 2021 bubble and 2022 bear market. Market breadth works superbly for **finding bottoms** but fails for **finding tops** — excessive bullishness can persist for years.

## 1. Architecture: How the Market Monitor Is Built

### 1.1 Data Source — TC2000 Scans

The Market Monitor is not a black-box indicator. It is a **manually maintained spreadsheet** fed by daily TC2000 scan counts. Each evening (~8 PM), Pradeep runs a set of scans, records the counts, and enters them into the spreadsheet.

| Scan                  | What It Measures            | Breadth Category             |
| --------------------- | --------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| 4% breakout           | Stocks up ≥4% today         | Daily intensity              |
| 4% breakdown          | Stocks down ≥4% today       | Daily intensity              |
| 25% in quarter        | Stocks up ≥25% in 65 days   | Quarterly momentum           |
| 25% in quarter (down) | Stocks down ≥25% in 65 days | Quarterly momentum           |
| 25% in month          | Stocks up ≥25% in 22 days   | Monthly momentum             |
| 25% in month (down)   | Stocks down ≥25% in 22 days | Monthly momentum             |
| 50% in month          | Stocks up ≥50% in 22 days   | Monthly extremes             |
| 50% in month (down)   | Stocks down ≥50% in 22 days | Monthly extremes             |
| 13% in 34 days        | Stocks up ≥13% in 34 days   | Faster quarterly (Fibonacci) |

> "All the Market Monitor data comes from this particular scan... the scans for this thing will be added below this video so that you can replicate the scan." — *Part 1*

### 1.2 Why Breadth, Not Price

Market breadth measures **participation** — how many stocks are moving, not just the index price. This is superior for timing because:

- **Market-derived:** The data comes from the market itself, not a secondary derivative
- **Leading indicator:** Breadth often turns before price does
- **Captures underlying pressure:** Reveals whether money is broadly entering or exiting

> "Market breadth is used for market timing, and this is not something which I had discovered. This is a well-known thing in the market that best market timing models are based on market breadth." — *Part 1*

## 2. Daily Intensity: The First Two Columns

### 2.1 What They Measure

The first two columns track the **number of stocks up 4%+** and **down 4%+** in a single day. These are the rawest, most immediate measures of buying and selling pressure.

> "Today, there are 82 stocks up 4% and 351 down. This information will go into this particular spreadsheet." — *Part 2*

### 2.2 Color Coding: The 300 Threshold

The spreadsheet uses color intensity to signal magnitude:

| Condition                      | Color              | Meaning                                |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------ | -------------------------------------- |
| >300 stocks up 4%              | **Dark green**     | Higher-than-normal buying intensity    |
| <300 stocks up 4%              | **Light green**    | Normal buying                          |
| >300 stocks down 4%            | **Dark red**       | Higher-than-normal selling intensity   |
| <300 stocks down 4%            | **Pink/light red** | Normal selling                         |
| More breakouts than breakdowns | **Green column**   | Bullish day                            |
| More breakdowns than breakouts | **Red column**     | Bearish day                            |
| Yellow date                    | **Yellow**         | Half-day (Thanksgiving, Christmas Eve) |

> "Anytime there is larger than 300, then it is shown in dark colors. If it is more than 300 stocks which are up 4% plus, then it is dark green." — *Part 2*

### 2.3 Interpreting Intensity

- **Normal days:** 100–300 stocks moving either direction
- **Intense days:** >300 — signals unusual institutional activity
- **Turn days:** 1,000+ — often appear at market turns (e.g., 1,100, 1,300, 1,500 after March 2020 bottom)

> "What you will see at turns is you'll see abnormally high figures. You'll see typically 1,000, 1,500, 1,700 kind of days. That tells you that a lot of buying has come in." — *Part 2*

## 3. Derived Ratios: 5-Day and 10-Day

### 3.1 How They Are Calculated

The 5-day and 10-day ratios are **derived from the first two columns**:

```
5-Day Ratio = (Sum of 4% breakouts over last 5 days) / (Sum of 4% breakdowns over last 5 days)
10-Day Ratio = (Sum of 4% breakouts over last 10 days) / (Sum of 4% breakdowns over last 10 days)
```

> "This five-day ratio is essentially... you add up the five days of 4% breakout and divide that by the five days cumulative of down breakout." — *Part 3*

### 3.2 Reading the Ratios

| Ratio | Interpretation |
|-------|---------------|
| >1.0 | More buying than selling over the period |
| ~2.0 | Aggressive buying |
| 4–5+ | Super-aggressive buying (often at turns) |
| <0.5 | More selling than buying |
| Very low | Heavy selling/capitulation |

> "When the ratio is two, it tells you there's two times more buying happening than selling... if the ratio is four or five, then it tells you there is super aggressive buying." — *Part 3*

### 3.3 Pradeep's View: Decorative, Not Core

The ratios were added later due to member requests. Pradeep does **not** personally rely on them because the raw numbers already tell the story.

> "Do I really look at that ratio? No. Because that information is also available. If you see something like this, you know the ratio is also going to be low." — *Part 3*

## 4. Primary Indicator: The Traffic Light (Quarterly 25%)

### 4.1 What It Is

The **primary indicator** is the most important signal in the Market Monitor. It measures:
- **Column A:** Number of stocks up ≥25% in the last quarter (65 days)
- **Column B:** Number of stocks down ≥25% in the last quarter (65 days)

When Column A > Column B, the indicator is **green** (bullish). When Column B > Column A, it is **red** (bearish).

> "This is a very important column. This is the primary breadth indicator. Whenever I say primary breadth indicator is negative, that means I'm referring to these two columns." — *Part 4*

### 4.2 Traffic Light Trading Rules

| Signal                 | Action                                                            |
| ---------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Green**              | Trade bullish breakouts aggressively                              |
| **Red**                | Avoid breakouts; look for shorts                                  |
| **Extreme low (<200)** | Prepare for major market bottom — generational buying opportunity |

> "When it is red, you should avoid breakouts and look for shorts. And when it is green, you should be looking at breakouts." — *Part 4*

### 4.3 The Capitulation Signal: <200

When the number of stocks up 25% in a quarter drops **below 200**, it indicates **capitulation** — every stock that needed to be sold has been sold. This is one of the most powerful signals in the entire StockBee methodology.

> "Whenever the reading goes below 200, that's extremely bullish. Whenever this reading is below 200 and there is so much selling, that's extremely bullish because that tells you that there is a capitulation kind of selling." — *Part 4*

**Historical examples:**
- **March 2020 (COVID crash):** Reading hit 180. Within days, massive rally began. Then 1,100, 900, 1,300 days followed.
- **2008–2009 financial crisis:** Similar capitulation pattern

> "Once the capitulation kind of selling happened, the buyers stepped in. And after that, you had 1,100 days, 900, 1,300 — series of positive days." — *Part 4*

### 4.4 401(k) / Long-Term Capital Timing

Pradeep uses this signal for his **401(k)** and long-term position decisions:
- **Entry:** Deploy capital when reading drops below 200–300 (extreme bearishness)
- **Exit:** Move to cash when the primary indicator turns red after a long bull run

> "If this goes below 300, 200, then I'm going to start looking at deploying my capital into 401(k). And similarly, after a bull market has been going on for some time and this signal turns bearish, then I use this to get out of my stocks in 401(k) and sit in cash." — *Part 4*

> "I have been sitting in cash since November [2021], and I have not done anything in my 401(k) because this signal has not really shown extreme bearishness." — *Part 4*

### 4.5 Asymmetry: Works for Bottoms, Not Tops

Market breadth is **asymmetric** — it excels at identifying bottoms but fails at identifying tops.

> "Whenever you're using market breadth, always remember market breadth as a signal works very well at bearish bottoms. It doesn't work to find out a top. If you use that to find a top, you're going to be prematurely getting out." — *Part 4*

> "Excessive bullishness doesn't lead to the market going down. Excessive bullishness will persist for a long period of time." — *Part 8*

**Why?** Tops form by **fewer and fewer stocks participating** in the move (narrowing leadership), not by excessive bullishness. Breadth captures participation, not narrowing.

## 5. Secondary Indicators: Monthly 25% and 50%

### 5.1 Monthly 25%: Context for Selling Intensity

The monthly 25% up/down columns add color to the primary indicator. During extreme selloffs, these numbers spike dramatically.

> "During COVID-19 time in 2020, these figures went extreme on both sides... You had 2,759 stocks which were down 25% in a month, which is a very high figure." — *Part 5*

After the turn, the same columns show explosive buying:

> "Almost 1,000 stocks were up 25%. And that is the strong buying which came in." — *Part 5*

### 5.2 Monthly 50%: The Broken Pullback Predictor (Pre-COVID)

Pre-COVID, the monthly 50% column had a reliable signaling value: **when stocks up 50% in a month exceeded ~20, a market pullback was likely within 2–3 days.**

> "Pre-COVID, if 20 stocks were up 50% in a month, it was very likely that you're going to get some sort of a pullback in the market. Every time these numbers used to go above 20, it is colored red." — *Part 5*

**Post-COVID, this broke** because retail traders flooded the market, creating persistent high readings that didn't lead to pullbacks.

> "Post COVID-19, that equation has broken down. Because there are so much retail traders entered the trading, and stocks started making big moves... Nowadays, it doesn't work." — *Part 5*

Pradeep's advice: Monitor it, but don't trade off it until the retail-driven regime changes.

## 6. 34×13: The Fibonacci-Inspired Faster Signal

A member contributed a faster version of the primary indicator using Fibonacci numbers: **13% move in 34 days** (half the magnitude, roughly half the duration of the quarterly 25%).

> "He was a Fibonacci fan, so he made it into a Fibonacci sequence: 13 and 34. And that is how it is done." — *Part 6*

**Verdict:** Can give earlier signals but also **false signals** — it may turn green briefly while the primary indicator remains bearish.

> "It can give you a false signal also because here, it started going green, but the primary indicator never went green. Do I pay much attention to it? No." — *Part 6*

Pradeep's advice: All secondary indicators add color but can be ignored. The primary indicator is what matters.

## 7. Number of Stocks in Universe: The Bubble Indicator

### 7.1 The Data Point

The Market Monitor tracks the **total number of US common stocks trading** (e.g., 6,974). This figure is remarkably stable over time — historically around 5,000–5,700.

> "For many years, this figure has remained more or less constant... around 5,000 very consistently. Companies come in, they IPO, some companies die. 5,000 is the number." — *Part 7*

### 7.2 The Bubble Signal: Dramatic Surge

In 2021, the number surged from ~5,200 to ~7,000 — a **2,000-stock jump**. This indicated a flood of IPOs, many low-quality, pushed into a market of willing buyers.

> "Whenever a lot of IPOs come in and dramatic increase... you suddenly have 7,000, which is a big jump. Whenever that has happened, that indicates that there has been a bubble in the market because IPOs are pushed into the market, and lower quality IPOs are pushed when there is lot of willing buyers." — *Part 7*

### 7.3 Historical Pattern: Bubble → Duration Bear Market

Every time the number of stocks has surged dramatically, a **duration bear market** followed:
- **1999–2000 (.com bubble):** Number spiked → 2+ year bear market
- **2021:** Number spiked to 7,000 → 2022 bear market (ongoing at time of video)

> "Whenever there has been high numbers, there has been very high number in 2000, 1999–2000 .com bubble. And after that, the market dropped. When it became a bubble kind of an environment, whenever there is a bubble, when the bubble burst, you get a duration bearish market of 18, 19, 20, 24 months." — *Part 7*

### 7.4 Low Numbers: Slow Recovery

When the number of stocks drops very low, it takes **many years** for the market to reach previous highs. Low supply + high demand = sustained bull markets.

## 8. T2108: The Ward Indicator (% Above 40-Day MA)

### 8.1 What It Measures

T2108 calculates the **percentage of stocks trading above their 40-day moving average**. It was added later to the Market Monitor as a confirmation tool.

> "T2108 basically calculates every day percentage of stocks above 40-day price moving average. At this stage, 21.85% of the stocks are trading above their 40-day moving average." — *Part 8*

### 8.2 Thresholds

| Reading | Interpretation |
|---------|---------------|
| >50% | Normal to bullish |
| 20–50% | Moderate |
| <20% | Bear market |
| **Single digits** | **Capitulation — major bottom forming** |

> "Whenever this figure goes below 20, that means you are in a bearish market, solid bearish market. But typically, market bottoms below 20 when that figure reaches very low." — *Part 8*

### 8.3 Historical Bottoms

- **March 2020:** T2108 reached **single digits**
- **2008–2009:** T2108 hit **1.6%** near the bottom

> "In 2008–2009, it reached single digit. 1.6% of the stocks were above their moving average near the bottom. Capitulation kind of selling is required for the market to bottom." — *Part 8*

### 8.4 Conjunction with Primary Indicator

T2108 is **not used standalone**. It is used alongside the primary indicator for extreme signals:

- **Primary indicator <200** → T2108 almost certainly in **single digits**
- **Both extreme** → "Powerful signal and bottoms which are formed at that stage tend to last for many years"

> "When T2108 is going low, and it is really going very low, below 20. And also, the primary indicator is going extreme. If this goes below 200, you're 100% likely to have T2108 in single digit. Those two then give you a powerful signal and bottoms which are formed at that stage tend to last for many years." — *Part 8*

### 8.5 Excessive Bearishness Is Bullish

The counterintuitive core of the Market Monitor: **extreme bearishness is bullish**, but extreme bullishness is **not** bearish.

> "When it becomes excessively bearish, then you have to be careful and not become excessively bearish because that is what leads to a turn in the market. Excessive bearishness is bullish." — *Part 8*

> "It's not that T2108 goes above 90 and then the market has topped. No. That tells you that there has been just a start of a new bull market. A lot of money has come in, and that bull market is going to last for two to three years." — *Part 8*

## 9. Practical Trading Application

### 9.1 Day-to-Day Calibration

| Market Monitor Signal             | Action                                              |
| --------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Primary indicator **green**       | Trade bullish breakouts aggressively                |
| Primary indicator **red**         | Trade shorts; avoid breakouts                       |
| 4% columns >300 dark green        | Intense buying — breakouts likely to follow through |
| 4% columns >300 dark red          | Intense selling — shorts likely to follow through   |
| Primary <200 + T2108 single digit | **Capitulation — deploy long-term capital**         |

### 9.2 Reducing Drawdown

The single biggest benefit of the Market Monitor is **avoiding trades when they are unlikely to work**:

> "If you just trade breakouts when the primary indicator is green, you will find most of the time, most of the breakouts work during that time. If you short only when the Market Monitor is red, you'll find that the shorts work very well. And accordingly, you can drop your drawdown substantially by using Market Monitor." — *Part 8*

### 9.3 What NOT to Do

1. **Don't use it to find tops** — It will get you out prematurely
2. **Don't trade secondary indicators in isolation** — They are for color, not core decisions
3. **Don't ignore the 300 threshold** — Half-day yellow dates distort the numbers
4. **Don't expect daily precision** — It's a **regime** indicator, not a day-trading trigger

## Key Takeaways

1. **Market Monitor is a breadth-based timing tool** — not a price indicator. It measures how many stocks are moving, revealing underlying money flow.
2. **The primary indicator (quarterly 25% up/down) is the core signal** — green = trade breakouts, red = trade shorts. All other columns are secondary.
3. **The 300 threshold separates normal from intense days** — >300 in 4% columns = unusual institutional activity, often at turns.
4. **Capitulation signal (<200 quarterly up) is generational** — March 2020 hit 180; massive rally followed. Used for 401(k) deployment.
5. **Market breadth works for bottoms, not tops** — Excessive bullishness can persist for years. Tops form by narrowing participation, not breadth extremes.
6. **Post-COVID broke some secondary signals** — The monthly 50% >20 pullback predictor no longer works due to retail influx.
7. **IPO surge = bubble signal** — When the number of stocks trading jumps dramatically (5,000→7,000), a duration bear market follows.
8. **T2108 confirms capitulation** — Single digits + primary <200 = powerful multi-year bottom signal.
9. **Excessive bearishness is bullish; excessive bullishness is NOT bearish** — The asymmetry is the hardest lesson to internalize.
10. **Manual process, not automation** — Scans are run daily, counts entered manually, colors applied by spreadsheet rules. The discipline of doing it nightly is part of the edge.
