# Guide 23 - Bearish Trading

## Overview

Bearish trading completes a trader's skill set, but it is **not a mirror image of the long side**. Pradeep emphasizes that markets structurally favor the long side (typically a 2:1 or 3:1 ratio of stocks up vs. down over time), yet the short side offers something powerful: **speed**. A stock that takes six months to make a 100% move can give back 50–98% in a matter of days when it unwinds.

This guide covers both the **tactical short side** (how to find shorts in a neutral-to-bearish market) and the **strategic bear-market framework** (what happens when a recession-level catalyst drives sectors to zero, why sectors matter, and how traders can make "life-changing money" by treating the short side without emotional attachment).

## Core Concepts

### The Short Side Is Faster

> "It might take a stock six months to make a 100% move, and it might take three days for that 100% move to be over."

Declines are violent and non-linear because they are driven by **unwinding of leveraged positions, margin calls, and panic**. The same structural forces that lift a stock slowly can crush it overnight.

### Best Shorts: True Market Leaders After a Big Run

Pradeep is unequivocal: the best shorting targets are **the stocks everyone loved at the top** — momentum leaders and "true market leaders" that are up 200–300%+ in a quarter or less. When these break, they break hard.

| Criterion | Why It Matters |
|-----------|--------------|
| **Up 300%+ in a quarter** | Parabolic moves retrace violently; "whatever goes up with rapid speed goes down even faster" |
| **Small / nano caps (<$1B or <$10B)** | Many have no real business; weak business models exposed under selling pressure |
| **"Sugar babies" / story stocks** | Hot retail story stocks (e.g., quantum computing, AI, clean energy) have asymmetric downside when the story breaks |
| **New IPOs** | Often have questionable business models and no earnings floor; no real support |
| **9,000,000+ volume** | Ensures liquidity so you can get filled without a borrow crisis |

> "Any stock which is up 300% plus and has questionable fundamentals, when it breaks down, it's going to be brutal."

### Sector Analysis Is Non-Negotiable

In a bear market, **weak sectors get weaker**. During the 2008–2009 recession:
- Consumer discretionary was taken to the woodshed (-75% to -90%)
- Casinos, retail, restaurants, auto companies collapsed
- Banks imploded; mortgage companies vaporized
- Citibank went from "$10 to $1" 
- Goldman Sachs fell 81%

In the 2025 tariff scenario Pradeep analyzes:
- **Technology** is the #1 most exposed sector (supply chains dependent on Vietnam, India, China)
- **Consumer electronics, semiconductors, scientific instruments** all face tariff-driven disruption
- **Retail / furniture / home goods** (Wayfair, Restoration Hardware) are especially vulnerable because import costs spike

> "You want to short something institutions want to get rid of."  
> "Weak sectors are going to get even weaker. That's where the spectacular failures happen."

### Two Legs, Not One

If you miss the **first leg down**, don't chase it. Wait for a dead-cat bounce and short the **second leg** — Pradeep calls second legs "so easy" because the hope trade has been exhausted and the trend is established.

> "Second legs become so easy to do it. Rather than trying to find the falling knife, wait for the bounce and then short the second leg."

## Key Principles

1. **Short side is not the mirror image of long side.** Intuition says it should be — it isn't. Short setups require different timing, different sizing, and different sector focus.

2. **Focus on small caps and nano caps.** The sub-$1B market cap universe is where the most fraudulent, story-driven, and unsustainable business models live. When they break, they break hardest.

3. **Use the afternoon and last hour for shorting.** Pradeep's morning is reserved for EPs, SIPs, and breakouts. Short execution is a second-half-of-day activity, often between 2 PM and 3:45 PM.

> "Don't short anything early in the day unless there is news. I always short in the second half of the day or in the last half an hour."

4. **The #1 short-side setup is Delayed Reaction EP (9,000,000).** Wait for a stock to sell off after a negative catalyst (e.g., missed earnings) with heavy volume, then short the start-of-a-swing (SOS) continuation move.

> "The number one setup you should focus on during this particular time period is EP 9,000,000, delayed reaction."

5. **Always prioritize sectors under liquidation.** When a sector is being sold by institutions, the weakest stocks in that sector will fall the hardest and never recover.

6. **Second leg is easier than first leg.** The first leg may be noisy; the second leg is the clean trend.

7. **Triple and double ETFs are highly liquid short vehicles.** Pradeep prefers shorting the **downward leveraged ETF** (e.g., SOXL, TQQQ on their way down) because they offer liquid, high-volume, large-$-price shares with rapid moves.

8. **Put a tight stop; never take more than 6% open risk overnight.** Pradeep caps his maximum aggregate overnight stop-loss exposure at **6% of total capital** across all positions.

9. **Sell into strength on the short side.** Don't sit in a +40% gain and hope it goes to -90%. Rumors and counter-trend bounces can erase profits quickly.

10. **Don't listen to billionaires calling bottoms.** In 2008, Kirk Kerkorian bought Ford at $10 and watched it drop to ~$1.50. Billionaires can afford to be wrong; traders cannot.

> "Don't listen to billionaires. By the time I learned it, I had already made some stupid mistakes going long because I listened to some idiot billionaire."

11. **Wait for a bounce before shorting an oversold market.** Never short into a waterfall. Wait for a counter-trend bounce, then short, essentially using the same anticipation logic as reversal bullish, but inverted.

> "In the short run, don't short anything. You don't short anything when the market is oversold. You wait for a counter trend bounce now and then short that counter trend bounce."

12. **Think worst-case scenario.** Stocks that look cheap at $10 can go to $5, then to $1. Some will never come back.

## Scan Formulas

### Core Scan: 9,000,000 EP (Delayed Reaction) Watch List

Pradeep explicitly states: **the #1 short-side setup in a bear market is a delayed reaction EP.**

Steps:
- Run the **delayed reaction EP scan** with **minimum volume 9,000,000 shares**
- Look for a **negative catalyst** (earnings miss, analyst downgrade, sector headwinds)
- Wait for the stock to react (gap down or big range-expansion down day)
- Then wait for a **follow-through SOS (start of a swing)** bar on the next 2–5 days
- Short on the second leg / continuation move, **never the first panic drop**

### 50% Bearish Study Filter

For a broader market view of what's broken, apply a **bearish 15% filter** across the market and extend the lookback window:
- Look for stocks down **>15% in the last 25 days**
- Filter by **capitalization <$10B**, ideally <$1B
- Study these charts daily — this is how you build pattern recognition

### Momentum Rank Inversion

- Sort your momentum ranking list (e.g., 25-day momentum) by **lowest momentum**
- The stocks that were top momentum 25 days ago are now the biggest losers
- These are your best short candidates

### Sugar Baby Tracking

- Maintain a watch list of all current "sugar babies" (hot story stocks)
- When the sector sours, these will be the most liquid and most accelerated shorts
- **Most sugar babies are currently down 33–50% from highs when the correction hits**

## Entry / Stop / Sizing Rules

| Parameter | Rule |
|-----------|------|
| **Primary setup** | Bearish Delayed Reaction EP (9,000,000+ share criteria) |
| **Entry timing** | Afternoon to last hour (2 PM – 3:45 PM); never short first hour unless news-driven |
| **Entry trigger** | Start-of-a-swing (SOS) after a negative catalyst has occurred; avoid first-leg panic |
| **Second-leg preference** | Wait for bounce, then short renewed breakdown |
| **Stop** | Tight stop inside the entry bar or breakeven; max 2.5–5% risk per position |
| **Overnight risk cap** | **Never more than 6% open risk across entire portfolio** |
| **Profit management** | Sell into strength; don't hold open profits too long |
| **Position sizing** | Fixed share sizing adjusted by liquidity; size smaller for wider stops |
| **Liquidity minimum** | Ideally 3,000,000+ shares/day; at least 1,000,000+ |

## Bottom Line

Bearish trading is a **complementary, not supplementary**, skill. The opportunities come in bursts — during corrections, bear markets, and sector-specific collapses. The edge lies in:

1. **Speed**: Moves down are faster than moves up.
2. **Selection**: Momentum leaders and sugar babies that are up 300%+ offer the most asymmetric downside.
3. **Sector sensitivity**: Go where institutions are selling — technology, consumer discretionary, and emerging story sectors are the first liquidated.
4. **Tactical discipline**: Use the afternoon for shorts, cap overnight risk at 6%, and always take profit into weakness before a rumor-driven bounce.

Pradeep's final message is blunt: **ignore politics, ignore billionaires, ignore narratives.** Build your own process, create your short playbook, and ask only one question daily: **"Where is the big money trade here?"**

> "Focus on perfecting your setup and fine tuning and fine tuning. Don't worry about others. What is the big money trade here? That is the only single-minded question which I'm asking."
