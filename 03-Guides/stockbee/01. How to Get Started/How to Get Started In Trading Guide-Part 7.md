## Summary

Part 7 answers what a working person should do **if they cannot actively trade** — be it day trading or swing trading. Pradeep's answer is deliberately cold: investing is **the most difficult skill in this entire business**, possibly even harder than beating the market as a swing or day trader. The core message is that **if you cannot sit through volatility without panicking, you cannot do any kind of longer-term trading or investing.** Period. Volatility handling is the single non-negotiable requirement. If that eliminates you, alternatives exist: real estate, or accepting lower-return approaches like asset allocation / ETFs. For those pursuing growth investing, he maps out the real requirements: finding fixed stars (not meteors), managing position sizing for volatility, understanding business model and management (not just valuation), subscribing to expensive tools/services, and putting in enormous research time — with no guarantee of success.

### 1. Why Swing/Day Trading Require Active Lifestyle

Pradeep opens Part 7 by referencing Part 2 (time frame) and Part 6 (day trading). Both require active monitoring.

If you're a working person: can you day trade? Technically yes ("anything is possible"), but practically unlikely. He points out that the ex-CEO of Goldman Sachs now trades several times a day — proving even the highest-status people can end up day trading after retirement if they want market action. But for most working people, the lifestyle doesn't fit.

**The question then becomes: if active trading is out, what choices do you have?**

---

### 2. Working People Option 1 — Longer-Duration Swings

If active trading is out but you still want market exposure, one approach is longer-duration swings (weeks to months).

**The catch:** This requires something most working people lack:

> "Your ability to handle volatility is important because, if you cannot handle volatility, like, lot of these members themselves, they say I'm so busy."

**The "Picks for Working People" (PIX) story:** Pradeep gives picks specifically designed for working people who cannot actively trade. What happens?

> "They cannot handle volatility."

> "Every five minutes, every time the stock goes up or down, they just freak out. They get their panty twisted. They email me or they just close the position, panic, close the position, panic, close the position, then say, can I get back in? Can I get back in? If it has gone up, then they regret."

**The core insight:** People who do swing trading and day trading do it to *avoid* volatility. They trade so they don't have to sit through drawdowns. If you cannot handle volatility, you cannot do any kind of longer-term holding.

---

### 3. Volatility + Position Size — The Trade-Off

If you're doing longer-duration trades:
- **Smaller position sizes** → less volatility per position → but also less return
- **Larger position sizes** → more volatility → potential for bigger returns AND bigger drawdowns

> "If you can handle volatility risk and if you can do large sizes, then that is when you can make a lot of money. But the problem with that is going to be you will have large drawdowns."

**The two failure modes of longer-duration investing:**
1. **Small positions, many positions** → 40–50 ideas, each too small to matter → boring returns
2. **Fewer positions, larger drawdowns** → emotionally unbearable, most people panic out

> "There is no escaping this."

---

### 4. Working People Option 2 — Growth Investing / Fixed Stars

If you want to become an investor rather than a trader, Pradeep's advice:

**Focus on growth stocks, specifically NEW GROWTH.**

> "I would always say if you want to become an investor, become a growth investor."

> "Within growth, I would do a combination of... new growth."

**The "Fixed Stars vs Meteors" framework** (from a book he recommends):

| Type | What It Is | Example | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Meteor** | Grows fast, then burns out | UPST, many tech stocks in 2021 | Tremendous — can go to zero or down 80%+ |
| **Fixed Star** | Grows steadily, year after year | TJX (+26,000%), AutoZone, VNMA | Lower — but finding them early is extremely hard |

**Key characteristics of a fixed star:**
- **Consistency** — not flashy growth, but steady growth year after year
- TJX: 26,000% move since IPO
- AutoZone: profit growing from ~30 to 98 over many years steadily
- VNMA, some others
- Not dramatic, just consistent

> "Consistency is important. That's what makes a fixed star. These are companies which are growing, not necessarily at rapid rate. But they are so consistent."

**Pradeep's assessment:** It's incredibly difficult.

> "If you do successfully longer term, you should think kind of a successfully, easily raise anywhere between $200 to $500,000,000. There is that much demand."

If someone could reliably find 10 fixed stars, they'd have hedge fund managers beating down their door. But almost nobody can do it consistently year after year.

---

### 5. The Novel Analogy — Why Prediction Is So Hard

> "As that guy, Renaissance Capital, owner said, if there is a thousand-page novel and the first word is 'New York,' can you likely figure out what is likely to be the ending?"

> "Most of these people, their success ratio is very low. Like, they will say this stock is going to become a 100-bagger. And then after two years, three years after holding it, once it is down 70%, then they'll close the position saying, yeah, it wasn't really a 100-bagger."

Pradeep's conclusion: **finding the next 100-bagger consistently is a fundamentally hard problem.** Even the best quantitative hedge funds (Renaissance Capital) don't actually do traditional long-term investing — they trade on 3-year timeframes, not 10 or 20 years.

---

### 6. Active Trading Is Relatively Easier

> "Can you learn swing trading in six weeks? The answer is reasonably yes if you have a template. Is it easy to become a swing trader and be profitable consistently? Relatively, it's much easier than becoming an investor and be consistently profitable."

> "For me personally, it's so easy to make money swing trading. I don't even break a sweat."

The hierarchy of difficulty:
1. Swing trading (easiest, with a template)
2. Day trading (harder, 3–5 years, requires size)
3. Being a consistently profitable long-term investor (possibly hardest)

### 7. Investment Products & Research Pradeep Actually Uses

His conclusion on these services: **"I'm not impressed with anybody who's consistent."**

> "I subscribe to 20 services because I can pay $10,000 to these services or I can give it to the IRS. That's why. But I'm not impressed with anybody."

**Tools he actually uses:**
- MarketSmith (fundamental screening)
- TC2000 (charting)
- Fidelity research on mutual fund holdings
- Multiple data sources
- Books he reads and re-reads

**Why so much effort is required:**
- Finding the next Tesla, next Amazon is ridiculously hard
- Most people (even professional money managers) simply buy the CURRENT winners (Facebook at $300, Tesla at $1000), not the next ones
- The next Amazon is going to come from "bucket zero" (pradeep's early-stage stock watchlist), not from S&P 500

### 8. Chris Camillo — A Good Free Example

Pradeep cites **Chris Camillo** (on Twitter) as a practitioner of this new-growth approach:

> "He'll have forty, fifty stocks, which are like growth stocks. He's not looking at old stocks. He buys them near to two hundred-day moving average or when their correction happened, and then he keeps adding, and he will hold a portfolio of forty, fifty positions."

He holds 40–50 positions, diversified, buying dips, keeping adding on conviction. Not easy, but a documented example of someone doing it.

---

### 9. Business Model + Management > Valuation Alone

For investing, you need more than valuation:

**Three things you need to know:**
1. **Valuation** — the numbers
2. **Business model** — how the company actually makes money
3. **Management** — because "the companies which are managed well are the one which can do well"

> "You can't just go by the numbers. There's a lot of digging which you need to do." > "It's not only valuation which comes into consideration. You need to know business model and management."

Management is particularly important for long-term holds because a good company under bad management still fails.

---

### 10. OPM — Other People's Money Is the Easiest Path

> "If you want to become a billionaire, OPM is the easiest way."

If you can successfully do growth investing, you have enormous OPM opportunity. Family offices, relatives, friends all want this. You can raise $200M+ if you prove you can consistently find 10-year compounders. The issue: almost nobody can.

---

### 11. If You Can't Do It — Real Estate Is the Actual Alternative

For people who want to build wealth but can't or won't trade:

> "Real estate is much easier than this. That's why I'm also buying real estate."

**His family friend story:**
- Bought first house by owner (older person), fixed it up, rented it
- Bought second (flooded basement), learned from mistakes, fixed it
- Bought third (two-unit), became more ambitious
- Bought fourth (needed $100K in fixes), moved in himself
- Ended up with **six rental properties now making more than his salary**

> "He learned in six months of doing that, what mistakes he's made and all. And then he started looking at it."

**Real estate advantages over investing in stocks:**
- Easier to understand and manage
- Depreciation provides tax advantages (shows losses even when profitable)
- Lower overall risk
- Does not require sitting through -70% drawdowns like stocks

**Options for working people who want to build wealth:**
1. **Real estate** — buy, fix, rent, compound
2. **ETFs / asset allocation** — boring, lower return, passive
3. **Swing trading with small time** (if you have any time at all)
4. **Don't try growth investing** unless you have skills, tools, inclination, and time

---

### 12. The Hard Reality — Self-Assessment Is Required

Pradeep repeatedly emphasizes: **take your own inventory honestly.**

- Do you even have time to research stocks for 1–2 weeks per idea?
- Do you have the tools? (MarketSmith, data sources, etc.)
- Can you afford the service subscriptions?
- Do you have skills to identify the NEXT Tesla, not the current one?
- Can you handle drawdowns without panicking?
- Do you have an employee or system to do this full-time?

> "If you are coming at that stage, find a job which will make you a thousand dollars a day. That's the simplest answer."

## Key Takeaways

1. **If you cannot handle volatility, you cannot hold anything long term.** The #1 requirement for any investing or longer-term trading is your personality must tolerate drawdowns without panic. Otherwise, you buy high, sell low, and regret.

2. **Investing is harder than swing trading.** Swing trading with a proven template can be learned in 6 weeks. Day trading takes 3–5 years. Being a consistently profitable long-term growth investor may be even harder.

3. **Fixed stars vs meteors is the core investment skill.** Meteors like UPST burn out. Fixed stars like TJX grow steadily year after year. Finding fixed stars early is >> valuation alone.

4. **Most investment products are unreliable.** Pradeep subscribes to ~20 services (Motley Fool, Beth Kindig, Briefing, etc.). None are consistently accurate. "Timing is atrocious." None give you a reliable answer.

5. **Chris Camillo** gives a useful model: 40–50 growth positions, buying at or near 200-day MA corrections, adding on conviction.

6. **Business model + management > valuation.** Numbers alone aren't enough. Management quality determines which growth companies survive.

7. **Real estate is the realistic alternative** for people who can't trade. Easier, less stressful, tax advantages, and compounding rental income. The family friend example: started with one, now has six.

8. **OPM exists.** If you CAN find fixed stars, OPM will find you. But proving that skill is the hard part.

9. **Don't buy current winners.** Facebook at $300 or Tesla at $1000 is not the game. The game is finding THE NEXT Facebook/Tesla at IPO price.

10. **Honest self-assessment:** If you can't do good swing trading, you definitely can't do growth investing. Consider real estate or ETFs.
