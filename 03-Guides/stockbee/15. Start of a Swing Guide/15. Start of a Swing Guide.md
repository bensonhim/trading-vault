# Start of a Swing (SOS) Guide

> **Speaker:** Pradeep Bonde (StockBee)  
> **Series:** Start of a Swing / SOS Guide (11 parts)  
> **Transcripts:** [[../../transcripts/15. Start of a Swing Guide/|Transcripts]]

## Summary

**Start of a Swing (SOS)** is the foundational entry concept for all swing trading on StockBee. The core thesis: **you make money in swing trading by buying at the start of a swing, not the second or third day.** Every explosive move (20%+ in a week, 50%+ in two months) almost invariably begins with a **range expansion or momentum burst on day one** — this is the "grammar of the market." SOS is not a single setup but a family of setups defined by **where** in a stock's trajectory the swing begins. The three primary types are: **(1) Ground Zero** (before any leg is established — highest risk/reward), **(2) Continuation** (first leg → 3-5 day consolidation/pullback → second leg — most common and tradable), and **(3) Consolidation** (extended sideways base → breakout). The two core scans are the **4% breakout** (C/C1 ≥ 1.04) and the **$ breakout / FHP** (Close − Open ≥ $0.90, for high-priced stocks). Critical distinction: **not every SOS is tradable** — you must qualify each setup by the quality of its pre-breakout action, the stock's context, and market conditions.

## 1. What Is Start of a Swing?

### 1.1 The Fundamental Rule

> "You make money in swing trading by buying at the start of a swing. If you cannot define what is the start of a swing, then do not trade swing trading setups. It's just a matter of time before you'll blow up." — *Part 1*

**SOS = the first day of a potential swing move.** Before that day, the stock was not going up. On that day, it begins.

### 1.2 The Grammar of the Market

Pradeep studied **1,000 explosive moves** (20%+ in a week, 50%+ in two months) and found:

> "Whenever a move starts, you're going to see the same phenomena again and again. A swing starts with a range expansion or a momentum burst. This is the simplest concept which anybody who wants to do day trading or swing trading should know about." — *Part 2*

| Move Magnitude | Typical Day-1 Trigger |
|---------------|---------------------|
| 20% in 3–5 days | 4%+ range expansion |
| 50% in 2 months | 4%+ breakout + volume surge |
| 100%+ parabolic | 4%+ + 9M volume + catalyst |

## 2. The Four Types of SOS

### 2.1 Overview Table

| Type | Description | Risk/Reward | Tradability |
|------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| **Ground Zero** | No first leg established; stock breaks from flat/base | Highest risk, highest reward | Requires 2-day tight action |
| **Continuation** | First leg → 3–5 day consolidation/pullback → second leg | Moderate risk, consistent reward | **Most tradable; start here** |
| **Consolidation** | Extended sideways base (10+ days) → breakout | Moderate risk | Requires clean, tight base |
| **Exhaustion / Third Leg** | After two completed legs, attempted third leg | High failure probability | **Avoid** |

### 2.2 Ground Zero SOS

The stock has **never had a first leg** yet. You are buying the very first attempt.

> "You need to see some sort of a two-day tight action to get interested in something which is start of a swing like this." — *Part 5*

- **Requirement**: Pre-breakout tightness (2+ days of small range)
- **Risk**: False starts common; stock may fail immediately
- **Reward**: If it works, you capture the entire first leg

### 2.3 Continuation SOS (The Core Setup)

The **most important and tradable** SOS type.

> "In a continuation setup, there is a first leg already established, and then there is a pullback or a flat consolidation less than ten days, and then there is a second leg." — *Part 5*

**Three elements:**
1. **First leg** — established upward move (3–10 days)
2. **Consolidation/pullback** — flat or shallow pullback, **3–5 days ideal**, never more than 10 days
3. **Breakout day** — range expansion, volume confirmation

> "Each one of them — the quality of each one of them tells you the probability increases if you qualify each one of these." — *Part 10*

### 2.4 Consolidation SOS

Extended sideways base (10+ days of tight action) followed by breakout.

- **Best when**: Base is tight, low volatility, no distribution
- **Risk**: Longer consolidation = more overhead supply
- **Scan**: TI65 or M20 + tight range

### 2.5 Exhaustion / Third Leg SOS

> "When you go into a third leg kind of a move, you should be very clear that the probability of it failing is very high." — *Part 5*

After two completed legs, the market is exhausted. The third leg attempt fails more often than not. **Skip these.**

## 3. Tradable vs. Non-Tradable SOS

### 3.1 The Critical Distinction

> "There are start of a swing on every stock. But not every start of a swing is tradable, and that distinction is very important because that is how we select setups." — *Part 6*

**Tradable SOS checklist:**
- [ ] Preceded by clear consolidation or tight action
- [ ] Volume expansion on breakout day (preferably 9M+)
- [ ] Stock is in a Sugar Baby or EP-quality candidate (optional but ideal)
- [ ] Market Monitor is bullish (primary > 200)
- [ ] Not a third-leg exhaustion attempt

### 3.2 Examples of Non-Tradable SOS

- Breakout from messy, choppy base
- No volume expansion
- Third leg attempt
- Stock with persistent failed breakouts ("broken stock")
- Market Monitor in bearish territory

## 4. The Two Core Scans

### 4.1 4% Breakout

Pradeep's **40-year study** on all stocks that made 25%+ moves in a month:

> "I did a deep dive on all stocks which made at least a 25% move anytime during a month for the last forty years. And then looked at what was the common characteristic of their beginning day. Majority of the cases will be a 4% plus move." — *Part 8*

**Scan formula:**
```
C / C1 >= 1.04  (4% breakout)
AND V > V1     (volume expansion)
AND V >= 100000 (100K+ volume)
```

**Why it works:**
- 4% = meaningful institutional interest
- Volume expansion = conviction
- Simple, universal, no curve-fitting

> "If you want to simplify your trading and remove all other things like 9M breakouts and dollar breakouts, all you need is 4% breakout." — *Part 8*

### 4.2 $ Breakout / FHP (Focus on High Priced)

For **high-priced stocks ($60+)** with different behavior:

> "Dollar breakout is basically a stock which is up 90¢ from the open. So C − O >= 0.90 and V >= 100,000." — *Part 9*

**Scan formula:**
```
C - O >= 0.90   (90¢ move from open)
AND V >= 100000 (100K+ volume)
```

**Characteristics of FHP stocks:**
- Low volatility, linear moves
- Institutional-dominated (mutual funds, pension funds)
- Longer, more persistent trends
- Less chop, cleaner risk management

> "The advantage of high-priced stocks is very clear: moves persist, low risk, likelihood of getting shaken out is lower. Most people don't trade them because retail traders are not wired to think high-priced stocks." — *Part 9*

**FHP as stock selection:**
- Sort dollar breakout scan by **percentage change ascending**
- Stocks with smallest % gain but $0.90+ absolute move = lowest risk
- Think in **dollars, not percentages** — "I'm risking $2 to make $10"

## 5. Qualification and Context

### 5.1 Qualification Is the Skill

> "Just because it's a start of a swing doesn't mean you buy every start of a swing. You have to qualify any setup — whether it is a Sugar Baby, 9M breakout, or VCP pattern. Without qualification, if you just buy a breakout, you will lose money. That's the skill." — *Part 10*

**Qualification layers:**
1. **Setup quality** — clean base? volume? tight pre-action?
2. **Stock quality** — Sugar Baby? EP candidate? trending?
3. **Market context** — bullish primary? breadth expanding?
4. **Sector/theme** — hot sector? thematic tailwind?

### 5.2 Context Checklist

| Factor | Ideal | Avoid |
|--------|-------|-------|
| Pre-breakout action | 2–5 day tight consolidation | Choppy, wide-range, messy |
| Volume on SOS day | 2× average, ideally 9M+ | Below average |
| Market Monitor | Primary > 300 | Primary < 200 |
| Stock type | Sugar Baby, EP, trending | Broken stock, serial failure |
| Leg count | First or second leg | Third leg (exhaustion) |
| Sector | Hot/theme in play | Dead/ignored sector |

## 6. Training Your Eye

### 6.1 The 25-Stock Exercise

> "The simplest exercise which can allow you to overcome [buying late] is to just mark start of a swing on 25 stocks." — *Part 2*

1. Open any chart
2. Mark every SOS you see
3. Check: Was it tradable? Did it work?
4. After 25 stocks, you'll start seeing the pattern

### 6.2 Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Fix |
|---------|---------------|-----|
| Buying on day 2 or 3 | FOMO, no concept of SOS | Study 25 SOS examples |
| Buying every breakout | No qualification | Apply 4-layer qualification |
| Ignoring volume | Focus only on price | Volume = conviction |
| Trading third legs | Not counting legs | "Two legs max" rule |
| No market context | Stock-only focus | Check Market Monitor first |

## Key Takeaways

1. **SOS is the first day of a swing.** Buying at the start captures the full move. Buying on day 2 or 3 = leaving money on the table + higher risk.
2. **Every explosive move starts with range expansion.** 4% breakout + volume surge is the "grammar of the market." This is not opinion — it's 40 years of data.
3. **Four SOS types: Ground Zero, Continuation, Consolidation, Exhaustion.** Start with Continuation (most tradable). Avoid Exhaustion (third leg).
4. **Not every SOS is tradable.** Qualification is the skill. Pre-breakout tightness, volume, stock quality, and market context all matter.
5. **Continuation SOS = first leg → 3-5 day consolidation → second leg.** The consolidation should be tight and short (<10 days). Quality of all three phases determines probability.
6. **4% breakout is the universal scan.** Simplifies everything. If you only use one scan, use 4% breakout with volume expansion.
7. **$ Breakout / FHP is for high-priced stocks ($60+).** Institutional-dominated, linear moves, lower volatility. Sort by % change ascending to find lowest-risk entries.
8. **Think in dollars, not percentages, for FHP.** "Risking $2 to make $10" is clearer than "risking 2% to make 10%."
9. **Train your eye with 25 examples.** Mark SOS on 25 charts. After that, you'll see it everywhere.
10. **Qualification > entry.** A great setup on a broken stock in a bear market will fail. Context is everything.
11. **Start simple: SOS Continuation with 5 examples.** Master one type before adding others.
12. **The 4% breakout and 9M volume are the two pillars.** 4% = price conviction. 9M = institutional participation. Together = high-probability SOS.
