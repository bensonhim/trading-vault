## Summary

Anticipation (ANTS) is a **momentum-pause entry strategy** that buys stocks *before* the formal breakout occurs, achieving tighter stops (often under 2.5%, ideally ~1%) and closer entries than waiting for a 4% or dollar breakout confirmation. It is not a standalone methodology — it requires prior mastery of the momentum-ranking frameworks (TI65, M20, MDT, DT) and Pradeep insists you should **only try anticipation after you have perfected breakout trading**. The guide presents two versions: a labor-intensive "grunt work" or "elbow grease" approach (manual chart review of hundreds of stocks nightly, 2–3 hours) and a "smart" approach (scans that reduce the universe to 20–40 stocks, 10–20 minutes). Pradeep's personal edge comes from a **three-pillar smarter method**: (1) extremely tight setup definition (±0.4%), (2) scans to reduce the universe, (3) running scans at **3:30 PM and entering at 3:58–3:59 PM** on the same day, front-running the majority of traders who do their anticipation work at night.

## 1. Core Philosophy: Momentum Pause

> "In order to understand this, what you need to do is you need to first understand the guide section in which we talked about TI65, we talked about MDT, we talked about DT, all those kind of, or M20, all those kind of momentum based methodology to rank stocks or to determine whether stock has a momentum." — Part 1

Anticipation is **not** about predicting breakouts in random stocks. It is about finding stocks that:
1. **Already have proven momentum** (via M20, TI65, MDT, or DT)
2. **Have paused** in a tight, orderly consolidation or minor pullback
3. **Are likely to resume** the trend with the next range-expansion move

The core thesis: if you can identify the pause correctly and enter before the crowd, your stop is tighter and your risk/reward is superior to a formal breakout entry.

**Why early momentum identification matters:** The sooner you catch a stock in its momentum cycle (via TI65 or M20), the easier anticipation is — because you trade the first or second leg, not an exhausted third or fifth. Pradeep explicitly connects this: "This is why on something like TI65 or M20 where we are able to identify momentum early, it is much easier to make anticipation work." — Part 6

## 2. The Grunt-Work Approach (Parts 1–3)

Pradeep calls this the **"elbow grease" or "dumb way"** to do anticipation — going through hundreds or thousands of charts manually.

### 2.1 Step 1: Build the Universe (Part 1)
Every evening after market close, run your momentum scans (M20, TI65, MDT, DT). In a bear market this might be 80 stocks; in a strong bull market it can be 1,800–2,000 stocks. Go through **every chart manually** and flag stocks that are:
- Holding up well after a momentum move
- Showing sideways consolidation or minor pullback
- Setting up for a possible breakout

Create an "Anticipation" watchlist in TC2000 (or your platform) and copy all flagged tickers into it. **Do this on all four momentum methods** — not just M20. Run the same manual review on TI65, MDT, and DT as well.

**Stocks flagged in Pradeep's Part 1 walkthrough (M20 scan):** AMLX, XMTR, AMPS, ZeroFOX, DCPH, AKRO, Veridian Therapeutics, Revance Therapeutics, CRCD, BSC, ACCD.

### 2.2 Step 2: Filter for Quality (Part 2)
Go through the watchlist with a "fine-tooth comb" and **remove setups that look attractive but have high failure probability**:

| Remove | Keep |
|--------|------|
| Stocks with recent 4%+ breakdown days (distribution) | Stocks with orderly pullback after aggressive buying |
| Choppy, long sideways moves without momentum | First or second leg after clear breakout |
| High volume during selling | High volume during first leg, low volume during consolidation |
| Extended third/fourth/fifth legs | Stocks where the momentum pause is recent and shallow |
| Dull, low-volatility drift | Tight, compressing price action |
| Stocks with known catalysts absent | Stocks with identifiable catalysts (drug approval, trial results, etc.) |

**Catalysts as a quality signal:** When Pradeep reviews stocks, he specifically flags those with known catalysts — e.g., a wrinkle-removing drug (Botox competitor) approved, or a NASDAQ trial result. A catalyst means the buying has a structural reason behind it, not just retail momentum-chasing.

> "Whenever you see a stock which is going sideways for a long period of time, it looks very attractive. But, these are the stocks where you're going to have highest failure in anticipation. Because, whenever a stock goes sideways for such a long period of time, the momentum is not there." — Part 2

**Long sideways consolidations are the #1 trap.** They look "ready" but lack the aggressive buying that makes anticipation work. If you see a long sideways move, do **not** buy it in anticipation — wait for a proper high-volume breakout instead.

### 2.3 Step 3: Entry Execution (Part 3)
Once filtered to 3–5 high-quality ideas, execute in one of two ways:

**Method A — Active Morning Monitoring (Aggressive, Recommended)**
- Load the 3–5 stocks into your brokerage platform (Interactive Brokers) before market open
- Watch them like a hawk in the first 10–15 minutes
- Enter on any **1% positive move** (even 1% is a range expansion)
- Immediately place a stop below the consolidation low
- Why: "every single one of the momentum trader who follows momentum trading is going to have a similar list. So as soon as they start breaking out, other people get into them. So earlier you can enter, the better it is."

**Method B — Buy Stop Limit Order (BSLO)**
- Calculate your trigger (e.g., yesterday's close + 1%)
- Set a BSLO: trigger activates the order; a limit cap prevents filling on a violent gap-up
- Example: stock at $26.00 → trigger at $26.25, limit at $26.50, stop at $25.80
- Place **before** market open; if the stock hits $26.25, you're in automatically
- If the stock gaps up above your limit ($26.50), the order is **not** activated — protecting you from a bad fill
- If the stock gaps down, the order is also not activated — natural protection on the downside

> "If you wait for a stock to breakout like this 4% or $1 and then buy, you wasted all your effort because then why did you create this watch list?" — Part 3

## 3. Trade Management: Why Anticipation Fails (Part 4)

Anticipation setups have a **higher failure rate than formal breakouts** for a structural reason: there is no volume confirmation at entry. You are buying on the *hope* that follow-through buying will arrive.

**Master breakouts first.** Pradeep is explicit: "I have recommended anticipation more to people who are good at breakouts. And, once you perfect breakout is when you should be looking at anticipation." — Part 4

### 3.1 The Crowded-Trade Problem
Because momentum trading is widely taught, many traders build similar watchlists. Some use M20 or TI65; others use IBD Relative Strength (similar to MDT) or Double Trouble (similar to DT). The point: **a lot of eyes are on the same stocks.** When a stock breaks out in the first 20 minutes:
- Anticipation traders enter early
- Breakout traders enter on the move
- Newsletter followers pile in
If genuine institutional follow-through does **not** arrive, the stock fades and gives back the morning spike.

> "Every single trader you know of, name a good trader and their trading anticipation set up. And many of these traders have a lot more followers. So they are sending out watch list. They are sending out newsletter with the same set of stock. As a result of that, these stocks do initially in the first half an hour breakout. And many of them after a breakout in the first half an hour tend to give up all the gains or fail." — Part 4

### 3.2 The Golden Rule: Move Stop to Breakeven Fast
- **First priority:** If the stock moves 4–5% in the first 30 minutes, move your stop to **protect that profit** (not just breakeven)
- **If the setup works** and follow-through arrives, manage it like a normal 3–5 day momentum burst (see [[03. Bullish Momentum Burst Guide/Bullish Momentum Burst Guide-Section 4|Momentum Burst Guide — Trade Management]])
- **If it fails**, your breakeven stop ensures you lose nothing or minimal
- Pradeep's personal behavior: "a lot of time, I get into some of these setups and if they move four or 5%, I immediately move my stop to protect that much amount of profit because I've seen failure rate is very high in this particular setup."

> "The first thing which you need to do once you are in this kind of a setup and it starts going up, is to try and move your stop to at least to break even. And if it makes four to 5% move immediately in the first half an hour, to at least move your stop to protect some of the profit." — Part 4

## 4. The Single Most Important Filter: Market Condition (Part 5)

> "The only time you should be trading anticipation setup is when the market is in a very confirmed and in a very wild bullish conditions. If you trade this setup in a choppy market conditions, you are going to have setups after setups trying to have a minor breakout and failing and you are going to get frustrated. You will have death by thousand cuts." — Part 5

| Market Condition | Action |
|-----------------|--------|
| **Confirmed bull** — Market Monitor green (stocks up 25%+ in a quarter > stocks down 25%+ in a quarter) | Trade anticipation aggressively |
| **Choppy / uncertain** | Stop; expect minor breakouts followed by immediate failure |
| **Bearish** | Do not trade anticipation; **guaranteed losses** on failed breakouts |

This one filter alone will **dramatically improve win rate**. Anticipation requires a continuous inflow of new buyers — "what is required is more buyer needs to show up." Only a confirmed bull market provides that. The other critical nuance: the bull market should be **established**, past the first phase of buying, with money continuously coming in.

## 5. The Leg-Count Rule (Part 6)

Not all momentum pauses are equal. The highest-probability anticipation setups form after the **first leg** of a move. Second-leg setups are still viable. Third+ leg setups fail increasingly often.

| Leg | Probability | Rationale |
|-----|------------|-----------|
| **First leg** | Highest | Fresh momentum, early adopters still accumulating |
| **Second leg** | Good | Momentum confirmed, not yet over-owned |
| **Third+ leg** | Declining | "Who is left to buy?" — exhausted move, buyer pool saturated |

> "Ask yourself a question: Who is left to buy that particular stock? And that is where you're gonna get high failure rates." — Part 6

> "First leg, the first consolidation after the first leg is best. The consolidation after the second leg is still good, but once third, fourth, fifth leg starts happening, the failure rate is going to increase and accordingly plan your trade." — Part 6

This is why early momentum identification (via TI65, M20) is critical: the sooner you catch a stock in its momentum cycle, the better your anticipation entries will perform.

## 6. The "Elbow Grease" Approach Summarized (Part 7)

Part 7 recaps the grunt-work method and explicitly frames it as **the slow, labor-intensive path**:

1. Start with a momentum scan (DT, TI65, MDT, M20 — any/all of them)
2. Go through every stock in the results
3. Look for ones setting up nicely
4. Make a watchlist
5. Clean up the watchlist (remove bad setups)
6. Put them in your brokerage platform
7. Watch them and enter (or use BSLO for aggressive entry)

**Limitations of the grunt-work approach:**
- All work happens at **night** → you enter the **next day**
- Stocks you identified may already break out or gap up overnight → you miss them
- In the morning, stocks break out **very fast and very early** → even with a watchlist, if you're not quick, the breakout has already happened
- In a bullish market, 1,800–2,000 stocks show up in momentum scans — you cannot manually review all of them

> "This kind of an approach which I have explained till now is more like what we called on the side and plus plus which is like elbow grease or a dumb way to do anticipation or a way to do anticipation putting lot of effort. There is a smarter way to do anticipation." — Part 7

## 7. The Smart Approach: Scans (Parts 8–9)

Instead of manually reviewing 1,500 charts nightly, use scans to pre-filter the universe.

### 7.1 Scan 1: Tight Day Filter (Part 8)
Build a scan called **"Anticipation Smart"** with:
1. **Liquidity** condition (standard)
2. **Price > $10** (Pradeep: "for this kind of methods, it's much better to focus on stocks which are priced about $10")
3. **TI65 > 1.05** (or M20, MDT, DT condition — can combine with OR)
4. **Price % change today between −1% and +1%** (narrow further to −0.5% to +0.5% for more restrictive)

**No ETFs** for this method: "I don't recommend ETFs for this kind of a method. Because inherently they have higher volatility than stocks." — Part 8

Result: in a bear market, 415 TI65 stocks → 32 stocks with ±0.5% move. In a bull market, 1,800+ stocks → ~100 stocks. Review time drops from 2–3 hours to **10 minutes**.

**Buyout detection:** When running this scan, you'll see many stocks that are just flat black lines — these are **buyouts**. Mark them with **"XXX"** so you don't waste time on them. Buyouts go sideways forever at the acquisition price and are useless for anticipation.

> "If you look at every stock, which meets TI 65 bullish criteria, you'll be looking at 415 stocks versus if you use the smart approach, you're only looking at stocks which are going to be anticipations smart. Only 32 stocks." — Part 8

### 7.2 Scan 2: Tight Days in a Row (Part 9)
Further tighten by requiring **multiple consecutive tight days**:
- Change the daily-move filter to **"X bars in a row"** in TC2000
- Set 3 bars in a row with price % change between −1% and +1% → **"3T"** (three tight days)
- Or 2 bars in a row → **"2T"** (two tight days)

Result: 400 stocks → 19 stocks (3T). This finds the most compressed, explosive setups — the very tightest formations.

**Combining momentum conditions:** Instead of running four separate scans (TI65, M20, MDT, DT), add OR conditions to a single scan. Example: `TI65 > 1.05 OR M20 condition = true`. This gives you one unified anticipation smart scan.

> "So, if you want to find like two tight days, all that you need to do is to change this particular condition to two tight days." — Part 9

### 7.3 Pradeep's Personal Settings
For his own trading, Pradeep uses **±0.4%** as the daily-move threshold — far tighter than the ±1% or ±2% examples given for beginners. At this setting, ~25 stocks appear. This finds only the most explosive, lowest-risk compressions.

## 8. Pradeep's Personal Edge: The 3:30 PM Tactic (Part 10)

This is the **advanced technique** that gives Pradeep an edge over traders who do anticipation work at night.

| Step | Time | Action |
|------|------|--------|
| 1 | 3:30 PM | Run the anticipation smart scan during market hours |
| 2 | 3:30–3:55 PM | Review the 20–30 stocks, identify 1–2 best setups |
| 3 | 3:58–3:59 PM | Enter the setup in anticipation of next-day breakout |
| 4 | Next morning | Stop already in; move to breakeven quickly if it breaks out |

**Why this works:**
- All other anticipation traders do their work at night and place BSLO orders for the next morning
- By entering at 3:58–3:59 PM, Pradeep is **already in the stock** before they even start their analysis
- If the stock gaps up and breaks out at the open, he captures the full move while others are scrambling to enter
- He does **not** enter immediately at 3:30 PM — he waits because "between 3:30 and close, there can be a lot of things which can happen. The setup can deteriorate or sometimes it can break out also."

**Risk:** The stock can gap down overnight. Pradeep accepts this risk because in a confirmed bull market, well-setup stocks rarely gap down dramatically: "Tell me how many times in a bullish market a stock which is setting up nicely gaps down. It happens all the time in the bearish market. It doesn't happen in the bullish market as often as people think."

### 8.1 The Three-Pillar Smarter Approach (Pradeep's Summary)
Pradeep explicitly summarizes his personal smarter method in three points:

1. **Tighter setup definition** — ±0.4%, not ±1% or ±2%
2. **Scan to reduce universe** — no grunt work through 1,500 stocks; smart scan brings it to ~25
3. **Different timing** — scan at 3:30 PM, enter at 3:58–3:59 PM, front-run the night analysis traders

> "There is a generic edge in the stock market of being first. If you can enter any move before other people enter you are always going to be in a better position." — Part 10

## 9. Stringent Visual Criteria for Ideal Setups (Part 11)

When reviewing charts, Pradeep applies a strict checklist. This is what he looks for when actually selecting from the scan results — the scan filters at ±0.4%, but his real selection is even tighter.

### 9.1 Price and Volatility
- **Higher-price stocks preferred** ($10+; ideally $30–$300): tighter absolute-dollar stops — "a $300 stock up or down less than 30¢, that is the kind of situation where you get the most bang for your buck"
- **Daily move ≤ 0.4%** scan filter, but **actual selection even tighter**: 0.05%, 0.10%, less than 0.20% — "even though I scan for 0.4%, what I really in reality, when I select my stock is to look for something which is even 0.05%, 0.10%, less than 0.20%"
- **Stop target: ~1% or less** on ideal setups (2.5% is the upper bound, but the point is to find situations allowing a 1% stop)
- **Lower-price stocks (<$10)** require wider stops because 0.4% on an $8 stock is just 1–2 cents — for these, adjust the filter to ±1% or ±1.5%

### 9.2 First Leg Characteristics
- **Unidirectional candles**: clear one-directional buying, candles closing near the high — "clearly you see only buyers in control"
- **No gap-up heavy legs**: continuous buying preferred over gap-and-go — "I don't really like this kind of gap up kind of setups. Better setups are one where you have continuously one directional candles"
- **Aggressive volume on the leg**: high volume during the up-move

### 9.3 Consolidation Characteristics
- **Volatility compression**: consolidation candles must be **smaller-bodied** than first-leg candles — "the body of the candle should be very small"
- **Body matters, wicks don't**: "I do not care about the wick. Somehow people are fascinated by wicks. I only look at the body. The bodies of the candle should be very narrow compared to what was happening in the up move on the first leg."
- **Staged compression**: "if this was a 2% move, you want this to be 1.5, 1.5 and the last stage should be the smallest stage" — triangle or flag formations where the last day before breakout is the tightest
- **Direction of consolidation**: slightly down or flat (not rising — a **rising wedge is not a good anticipation setup**: "I would not take an anticipation setup where this is going up and then there is a narrow candle. That is like a rising wedge. And it is not a good anticipation set up.")
- **Duration**: 3–4 days ideal; < 7 days best; > 10–12 days = not a momentum pause, just stalemate — "longer the consolidation, then it indicates that it is not a momentum pause. It just indicates that buying and selling has come to a standstill and you need a breakout for it to work."
- **Volume dries up** during consolidation: lower volume than first leg
- **Short-term trader = small pause**: "if you're a short term trader, what you're looking for is a small pause. If it goes into too long a pause in terms of consolidation, becomes a very long consolidation, that it is not a good thing."

### 9.4 Momentum Strength
- Prioritize stocks with **highest TI65** (or highest momentum rank): "always prioritize the one which has the highest TI65. The higher the TI65, the more is the momentum"

> "I want the body of the candles to be smaller, during this consolidation. I want the volatility to decrease in the direction of the breakout." — Part 11

## 10. Position Trading Variant: Weekly Charts (Part 12)

Anticipation is not limited to 3–5 day swing trades. It can also find **multi-month position trades** using longer-term momentum and weekly charts.

### 10.1 Methodology
1. Use **MDT, MDT25, or MDT50** (or DT) to build the momentum universe
   - MDT scan: `liquidity = true`, `price > $3`, `C / AVGC126 > 1.18`
   - Or rank top 300–400 stocks by MDT25/MDT50 (displaced momentum ranking)
2. Switch to **weekly charts** (Pradeep: "I wouldn't suggest monthly. Weekly is much better if you're going to find longer term positions")
3. Look for stocks that have:
   - Established a clear first leg with **strong buying volume**
   - Formed a **4–10+ week sideways consolidation** — not just 1–2 weeks
   - Volume drying up during consolidation
4. **Verify on the daily chart** — if weekly looks good but daily doesn't, remove it
5. Create a watchlist with price alerts; wait for the breakout
6. **Always check for buyouts** before adding to the watchlist

### 10.2 The Darvas Parallel
Pradeep draws an explicit parallel to Nicolas Darvas: "Darvas used to have a watch list of only three to five stocks. And from that, he used to operate." Position-trading anticipation works the same way — a tight watchlist of the best setups, held for months when they break out.

### 10.3 Key Differences from Swing Trading

| Aspect | Swing ANTS | Position ANTS |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Timeframe | Daily | Weekly |
| Consolidation | 3–7 days | 4–10+ weeks |
| Hold duration | 3–5 days | 2–4+ months |
| Momentum tool | TI65, M20 | MDT, MDT25, MDT50, DT |
| Entry style | 3:58 PM or BSLO | Formal breakout with volume (watchlist + price alerts) |
| Goal | 8–20% move (or $10–$20 in high-price stocks) | 40–100%+ move (move lasts for months or weeks) |
| Volume requirement | High volume on first leg, low on consolidation | Both equally critical: strong buying on first leg **and** volume drying up on consolidation |

> "If you are using DT or MDT underlying assumption is that you are using it to find longer term hold positions because if it is a base of two to three months or four months then you are going to be holding it for three to four months." — Part 12

**Critical for position trades:** You want strong buying in the first leg *before* the consolidation forms. "If there is a very weak buying, that tells you that it's not a very attractive candidate for this." — Part 12

## 11. Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

| Pitfall | Why It Happens | Fix |
|---------|--------------|-----|
| **Trading anticipation before mastering breakouts** | Anticipation is "cooler" and seems like an edge | Master 4%/dollar breakout trading first; anticipation has higher failure rate |
| **Trading in wrong market** | FOMO; desire to trade every day | Only trade when Market Monitor is green; confirmed bull only |
| **Buying extended third+ legs** | Momentum scans show strong stocks late in cycle | Always ask: "Who is left to buy?" |
| **Long sideways traps** | Looks "ready" but lacks momentum | Remove if consolidation > 12 days or choppy; wait for high-volume breakout instead |
| **Low-price stocks with wide stops** | Penny stocks have high volatility | Focus on $10+ stocks; adjust filters to ±1–1.5% for sub-$10 |
| **Buyouts showing in scans** | Acquired stocks go sideways forever | Mark with "XXX"; skip all buyouts |
| **ETFs in anticipation** | ETFs show up in universe scans | Do not use ETFs for anticipation — inherently higher volatility than individual stocks |
| **Not moving stop to breakeven** | Hope that breakout will continue | Move stop aggressively in first 30 min; 4–5% move = protect profit immediately |
| **Entering on gap-up days** | BSLO triggers on gap-up, bad fill | Use limit cap on BSLO; if stock opens above limit, order not activated |
| **Rising-wedge consolidations** | Narrow candles look tight | Avoid setups where consolidation is rising; want flat or slightly declining |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Anticipation is a momentum-pause strategy, not a standalone system.** Master TI65, M20, MDT, and DT first — and master breakout trading before attempting anticipation.
2. **Confirmed bull market only.** This one filter eliminates most anticipation losses. Choppy market = death by thousand cuts; bearish market = guaranteed losses.
3. **First and second leg only.** Third+ leg setups fail because the buying pool is exhausted. If it looks "obvious," ask: "Who is left to buy?"
4. **Two paths:** grunt-work (manual review, 2–3 hours) or smart (scans, 10–20 minutes). Both valid — choose based on how much time you want to invest.
5. **Pradeep's three-pillar edge:** (1) ±0.4% tight setup, (2) scan to reduce universe, (3) 3:30 PM scan + 3:58–3:59 PM entry to front-run night traders.
6. **Scan → real selection is even tighter.** Scan at ±0.4%, but actual buy candidates should show 0.05–0.20% daily move.
7. **Stringent visual criteria:** unidirectional first leg, body compression (not wicks), slightly down or flat consolidation, 3–7 day duration, volume drying up, highest TI65 priority.
8. **Move stop to breakeven fast.** Anticipation setups fade quickly if genuine buying doesn't follow. 4–5% move in first 30 min = protect profit immediately.
9. **Position-trading variant:** Weekly charts + MDT/MDT25 for multi-month holds; like Darvas, keep a tight 3–5 stock watchlist.
10. **No ETFs, no buyouts, no low-price stocks.** These are structural headwinds for anticipation.
