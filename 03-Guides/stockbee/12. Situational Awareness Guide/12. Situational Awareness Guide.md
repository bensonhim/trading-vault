# Situational Awareness (SA) Guide

> **Speaker:** Pradeep Bonde (StockBee)  
> **Series:** Situational Awareness Guide (13 parts)  
> **Transcripts:** [[../../transcripts/12. Situational Awareness Guide/|Transcripts]]

## Summary

Situational Awareness (SA) is a **filter placed on top of trading setups** to reduce drawdowns, increase win rates, and calibrate risk. The concept originates from aviation — before every flight, pilots hold a "situational awareness meeting" to review turbulence, storms, and landing conditions, preparing contingencies before takeoff. Similarly, traders must assess market conditions before deploying capital: Is this a bullish or bearish regime? Is breadth expanding or contracting? Is the Fed stimulating or tightening? Are we near capitulation or euphoria?

This guide provides a multi-layered framework. **Foundationally**, the US stock market has an upward bias — it has been up in 80 of the last 96 years, and multi-year bear markets are extremely rare post-1941. **Tactically**, market breadth (via the Market Monitor) tells you whether to trade long or short on any given day. **Structurally**, bull markets last 3–10+ years because the Fed stimulates for extended periods, companies restructure, consumers rebuild balance sheets, and policy responses address underlying problems. **Operationally**, the "20% study" (stocks up/down 20% in a week) reveals mini-cycles of exhaustion, while capitulation signals (T2108 single digits, primary indicator <200) mark generational bottoms. **Thematically**, every bull market has a dominant theme (work-from-home, emerging markets, turnarounds), and the biggest moves come not from the first-breakout stocks but from successive waves of new breakouts. The single hardest skill: **remaining bullish throughout a bull market** without premature exit.

## 1. The Origin and Purpose of Situational Awareness

Situational awareness is an **aviation industry concept** pioneered by the US Air Force after the Vietnam War. Pilots were losing focus on surrounding planes, separating from formations, and getting shot down because they were not aware of their tactical environment.

> "In the airline industry, before a flight takes off, there is a situational awareness meeting where the situation during the flight is discussed. If the flight is going to go through turbulence or the landing conditions are going to be foggy, the pilot and crew need to be aware of those things." — *Part 1*

**The analogy for trading:** before you deploy capital each day, week, or month, you must assess:
- What is the market situation?
- Are breakouts likely to work?
- Is it time to use margin aggressively or conserve capital?
- Which setups will work best?

> "Setups work in a particular situation. Setups do not work in vacuum." — *Part 1*

**Benefits of SA:**
- Lower drawdowns (trade only when conditions favor your setup)
- Higher win rate (avoid trades that are structurally unlikely to work)
- Better mental capital (less frustration, fewer forced exits)
- Correct risk calibration (aggressive when favorable, defensive when hostile)

## 2. Foundational Principle: The Market Has a Bullish Bias

### 2.1 Historical Evidence

The first and most critical SA lesson: the US stock market has an **inherent bullish bias**.

> "Out of the 96 years which you're looking at, there are very few bearish years. Out of the total years, if you look at negative years, you're looking at a total of 11 years which was down 10% plus, and 5 years which was down. 80 years, the market has been up." — *Part 2*

| Statistic | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| Years analyzed | 96 (since 1926) |
| Up years | ~80 |
| Down 10%+ years | 11 |
| Multi-year down streaks since 1942 | 2–3 times |
| Average annual return | ~11.94% |
| Years with 30%+ returns | Rare (mean reversion follows) |

### 2.2 Why the Bias Exists

**Post-1941:** The Federal Reserve learned how to stabilize economies. Bear markets became shorter and shallower.

> "Since 1941, you will see that there are periods of five, six, seven, eight years of bullish market, and once in a while, you get a bearish market." — *Part 2*

**Pre-1941:** Bear markets were longer because there were no economic stabilization tools (no Fed, no QE, no fiscal stimulus).

### 2.3 The Ideological Trap

Most people become bearish not because of market data but because of **ideology**:
- Deficit fears
- Taxation concerns
- Political agenda
- Doomsday bias

> "People become bearish, not because they are looking at the market. They become bearish because, ideologically, they have a bearish bias... The reality is this database. The reality is this database, that in 96 years, there has been 5 bearish markets." — *Part 2*

> "Anybody who says the market is going to be down for 3 years, 5 years — ask him to get a mental health checkup done. The market has a bullish bias." — *Part 2*

### 2.4 Practical Implication

**90% of the time, your default SA posture should be bullish.** The market goes up, corrections are shallow, and buying dips works.

> "Keep buying the dips. Keep buying the dips. That should be your situational awareness generically." — *Part 2*

## 3. Market Breadth: The Daily Compass

### 3.1 Why Breadth Matters

Market breadth tells you **how many stocks are participating** in a move. It is the most reliable indicator of underlying money flow.

> "Breadth tells you how many stocks are participating in a move. When the market is going up, the breadth will be positive. When the market is going down, you'll see the breadth will be negative." — *Part 3*

### 3.2 The Market Monitor Primary Indicator

The primary indicator (stocks up 25% in a quarter vs. down 25% in a quarter) is the core SA signal:

| Signal | Situational Awareness Posture |
|--------|------------------------------|
| **Green** (more up than down) | Bullish — trade breakouts, avoid shorts |
| **Red** (more down than up) | Bearish — trade shorts, avoid breakouts |
| **Extreme low (<200)** | Capitulation — prepare for major bottom |

> "Whenever the primary indicator is bullish, you are going to be bullish. Whenever it is bearish, it is going to be bearish... Anticipation kind of setups work only when the primary indicator is bullish. Anticipation setups are not going to work in a bearish market." — *Part 3*

**Historical example:** The primary indicator turned bearish in November 2021 and never recovered — signaling the start of the 2022 bear market.

### 3.3 Tactical Breadth Windows

Even within a bear market, there are **short windows of positive breadth** where bullish setups briefly work:

> "During that period, if you are trading a breakout, it worked. And if you are trading the short, it will not work... Similarly, the market breadth went negative during that period. And if you were trading short side, you would have a higher win rate." — *Part 3*

**Rule:** Always trade in the direction of market breadth.

## 4. The 20% Study: Mini-Cycle Exhaustion Detector

### 4.1 What It Measures

Every day, Pradeep studies the **number of stocks up or down 20%+ in a single week**. This reveals mini-cycles within larger trends.

### 4.2 Excessive Numbers = Pullback Coming

When **150–300+ stocks are up 20% in a week**, it signals **exhaustion**:
- Stocks have run too far too fast
- Buyers are exhausted
- A pullback or consolidation is imminent
- The pullback will hit the stocks you are most heavily invested in

> "Whenever it becomes extremely bullish or bearish, you know that not next day, but immediately in some time period within next two to three days, there's gonna be a pullback." — *Part 4*

> "They're up 40%, and you're already thinking about which island you're going to buy, where you're going to park your Ferrari... And what happens is then there is an exhaustion of buying, and there's a pullback. And by the time you realize that, you end up giving up a lot of gains." — *Part 4*

### 4.3 Tactical Response to Excess

| 20% Study Reading | Action |
|-------------------|--------|
| **Very high** (150–300+ up) | Sell/reduce exposure — pullback imminent |
| **Moderate** (normal range) | Continue trading normally |
| **Very low** (both sides quiet) | Prepare for next move — bullish when in bull market |
| **Very high down** (150–300+ down) | Shorts working well — but bounce may follow |

> "You have to be constantly thinking what the 20% study is showing me, and use that for creating situational awareness." — *Part 4*

### 4.4 The Psychology Trap

Traders get hurt because they increase risk when things are working (high 20% readings) instead of decreasing it:

> "When things are working for you, you have 90% win rate, you think that you are god's gift to trading. And then that's when you get hit." — *Part 4*

## 5. The Federal Reserve: The Invisible Hand

### 5.1 The Fed's Mandate

The Fed's role is to **stabilize the economy** and ensure it spends as little time as possible in contraction. Independent of political ideology, this is the structural reality.

> "Their role is to make sure that the economy spends as little time as possible in a downtrend... Their role is to make sure that the economy spends as little time as possible in a downtrend, or if there is a contraction in the economy, to make sure that they stimulate the economy." — *Part 5*

### 5.2 Stimulus Mode: Every Dip Is a Buy

When the Fed is stimulating (rate cuts, QE, liquidity injection):
- **They don't stop early.** Stimulus lasts 3–5+ years until the economy is robust.
- **Every dip is a buy.**
- **Premature bearishness is fatal.**

> "When the Fed decides to stimulate the economy, they're not just going to do it for one day or one week. They will be at it till the economy has recovered to its peak capacity... BTFD or buy the fucking dip is the best strategy." — *Part 5*

> "In 2009, finally, the Federal Reserve decided to stimulate the economy and they started quantitative easing, they kept at it for fourteen, fifteen, like six, seven years." — *Part 5*

### 5.3 Tightening Mode: The Fed Wants to Cool the Economy

When inflation is high, the Fed does the **opposite** — it wants to crash the economy to reduce demand:
- Rate hikes continue until inflation is broken
- Every rally is suspect during tightening
- Stocks and the economy move together to the downside

> "Currently, when I did this video, inflation is a problem. So the Fed is continuing to increase the rate till they can break the back of inflation. Every rally has been suspect because the Fed is trying to reduce the demand." — *Part 5*

### 5.4 Rare Policy Errors

Only 2–3 times in history has the Fed mistakenly tightened into weakness (Volcker era 1980s being the main example). Most of the time, they stabilize.

## 6. Bear Market Tendencies

### 6.1 Tendency 1: Everything Moves Together

In bear markets, **correlation goes to 1.0**. Individual stocks lose independence and move in lockstep with the indexes.

> "In a bearish market, everything moves together. Especially once a bearish market is established, what you'll see is all stocks will move in tandem." — *Part 6*

**Implication:** Short setups only work when the **index itself is breaking down**. Shorting during a bounce = getting stopped out.

> "If you short when the market itself is breaking down, your short is going to work. If you short when the market is having a bounce, it is not going to work." — *Part 6*

### 6.2 SPY Breakdown Sync Rule

Synchronize shorts with days when **SPY is down 1.5%+**. On those days, most stocks break down simultaneously.

> "Whenever the S&P is down 1.5% plus, you will see this. That day, if you take shorts, they tend to work for three to four days." — *Part 6*

### 6.3 Tendency 2: Vicious Counter-Trend Rallies

Bear markets produce **explosive, multi-week counter-trend rallies** that can be tradable:
- 14% rallies in a bear market are common
- They last weeks or even months
- They trap shorts and tempt bulls prematurely

> "Bearish markets have very vicious counter trend rallies. In bullish market, there are not many dips. In bearish market, all of a sudden there will be V-shaped rallies which can be very tradable." — *Part 7*

> "This was a rally which happened, and it was almost like 14%. And it was almost like a full bull market, 19% the market went up." — *Part 7*

**Historical examples:** 2000–2002 and 2008–2009 both had multiple vicious counter-trend rallies before the final bottom.

## 7. Capitulation: The Bear Market End

### 7.1 What Capitulation Looks Like

Bear markets end in **capitulation** — indiscriminate selling where everything is sold regardless of quality.

> "There is a saying on Wall Street: when they raid the whorehouse, they don't only take the whores, they take the johns and they also take the piano player. So at some stage, in a longer duration bearish market, there is a capitulation kind of selling." — *Part 8*

### 7.2 Capitulation Signals

| Signal | Threshold | Meaning |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **T2108** | Single digits (<10%) | Only 1–9% of stocks above 40-day MA |
| **Primary indicator** | <200 | Capitulation-level breadth |
| **VIX** | Extreme spike | Fear at maximum |

> "Capitulation kind of selling is a very common characteristic of bearish bottoms... T2108, if you see capitulation, you will see 1.1 reading or three or four readings, so very low reading." — *Part 8*

### 7.3 What Happens After Capitulation

Once capitulation occurs, the market bottoms and begins a **multi-year bull market**:

> "When capitulation kind of selling happens, you should start changing your situational awareness to likely there is a bottom in the market. And then look for the birth of a new bull market, which can last for multiple years." — *Part 8*

## 8. Policy Response at the Bottom

Major bear markets end not with technicals alone but with **policy response**:
- **2002:** Government stimulus and rate cuts
- **2009:** TARP (Troubled Asset Relief Program) passed by Congress
- **2020:** $3 trillion stimulus package

> "Every single duration bearish market... ends when the pain becomes too much or the economy is in real trouble, and there is a policy response." — *Part 9*

> "At the bottom, you have capitulation. The Federal Reserve trying to stimulate the economy. Some sort of a signaling is always there or anticipation of the signaling and the congressional response. Combination of these factors create a bottom." — *Part 9*

**The market anticipates policy.** It often bottoms **before** the bill is passed.

## 9. Bull Market Structure: Why They Last Years

### 9.1 Why Bull Markets Persist

When a bull market starts, it lasts **3–10+ years** because:
1. **Fed stimulus** continues for years
2. **Companies restructure** — change management, cost structure, buybacks
3. **Consumers rebuild** — fix balance sheets after bear market wipeouts
4. **Policy fixes** address the root cause of the crisis
5. **Weak competitors** go out of business, leaving survivors stronger

> "When the bull market starts, it is going to last long. Consumer taking action. Companies taking action. Management gets changed. CEOs get changed. Company change cost structure. Marginal players go out of business. As a result, the surviving players are in a very good situation." — *Part 10*

### 9.2 The Skepticism Phase

Early bull markets are met with skepticism because the **recent bear market trauma** is still fresh:

> "When the bull market starts, people are skeptical. And there is a reason why they are skeptical because there is so much background as to why the bearish market happened." — *Part 10*

### 9.3 Pradeep's 2023 Prediction

At the start of 2023, Pradeep predicted: "Sometime during this year, we are going to get a bottom in the market. When the rally starts, it is going to be a rally which will last for years."

## 10. Cascading Breakouts: The First-Leg Trap

### 10.1 The Pattern

At the start of a bull market, **first-leg breakout stocks** often make 500–1,000%+ moves and then go sideways for years. They do **not** necessarily have a second leg.

> "The first set of stocks which break out after a bearish market have some reason why they're breaking out. They are growing during the bearish market or they are a turnaround kind of situation... But nothing happened after that. There is a separately different group of stocks which had a breakout after that, and they went on to make a big move." — *Part 11*

**Examples:**
- **Wayfair:** 1,200% in one year, then sideways
- **Zoom:** Explosive first leg, then nothing

### 10.2 Relative Strength Trap

Traders focused on "relative strength" (stocks that held up best) often get trapped in stocks that had their one move and are now dead money:

> "If your basic strategy is to do relative strength, you'll find that these stocks can be a trap. They just make the first leg. And many times, the stocks which break out first leg, they make it, and then they just keep going sideways for a long period of time." — *Part 11*

### 10.3 The Relay Race

Bull markets work like a **relay race** — fresh stocks take over while earlier leaders rest:

> "It's like a relay race. Fresh set of stocks come into the market during bull market. They make big moves. Another set of stocks make big moves. Another set of stocks make big moves." — *Part 13*

## 11. Theme Identification: The Engine of Bull Markets

Every bull market has a **dominant theme**. Identifying it early provides a massive edge.

| Bull Market | Theme | Big Movers |
|-------------|-------|------------|
| **2020–2021** | Work from home | Wayfair, Shopify, Zoom, Overstock |
| **2003–2007** | Emerging markets | NetEase, Sina, Chinese stocks, commodities |
| **2009–2014** | Turnarounds / Internet & tech | Banks post-crisis, fintech |
| **Future (2023+)** | Turnarounds? | Tech stocks beaten down in 2022 |

> "If you can understand what is the theme which is driving a bull market, then it is easier to essentially ride a train because that theme is going to run significantly more than you can imagine." — *Part 12*

> "The theme becomes apparent in the first two or three months of a bull market. And once the theme becomes apparent, it's just riding that theme and looking at any company in that particular industry." — *Part 12*

**How to find themes:** Your scans will show you — thematic stocks consistently show up in breakout scans.

## 12. The Hardest Skill: Remain Bullish

### 12.1 The Premature Bearishness Trap

The single most common and costly mistake in bull markets: **becoming bearish too early**.

> "The most important thing which you can do in a bull market, the single most important thing, is to remain bullish. Prematurely becoming bearish is a big problem in a bull market." — *Part 13*

> "You need to be bullish and need to remain bullish for months, years till there is real deterioration. Till you get tired, you have to remember that it is a bull market." — *Part 13*

### 12.2 The Livermore "Old Turkey" Reference

Pradeep references Jesse Livermore's "Reminiscences of a Stock Operator" — the character "Old Turkey" who simply repeats: "It's a bull market." This captures the discipline required.

> "If you read Livermore's book, there is that character, the old turkey, and he keeps saying, 'Look at it. It's a bull market. It's a bull market. It's a bull market.' Till you get tired, you have to remember that it is a bull market." — *Part 13*

### 12.3 Bubbles vs. Normal Bull Markets

A 40% move in one year does **not** make a bubble. Bubbles form after **10–15 years** of sustained bull market when retail traders flood in.

> "Just because the market has gone up big in one year doesn't make it a bubble. Bubble happens after ten years, fifteen years. It's when a lot of new people have come into the market. Retail traders have taken over the market." — *Part 13*

### 12.4 The Opportunity Cost

Traders who became bearish in 2009, 2010, 2011, 2013, 2016, 2017, 2019 missed **years** of gains:

> "A lot of people started shorting the market because they were still conditioned to think... when it started rallying, big losses people made because they're conditioned to short. And they never believed in the rally, and the rally kept on going up." — *Part 13*

## Key Takeaways

1. **Situational awareness is a filter, not a setup.** It tells you when your setups are likely to work and when they are not. Setups do not work in a vacuum.
2. **The market has a bullish bias.** Up 80/96 years. Multi-year bear markets are extremely rare post-1941. Your default posture should be bullish.
3. **Ideology kills traders.** Bearishness driven by politics, deficits, or doomsday narratives is not situational awareness — it's bias.
4. **Trade in the direction of market breadth.** When the Market Monitor primary indicator is green, trade breakouts; when red, trade shorts; when extreme (<200), prepare for capitulation.
5. **The 20% study reveals mini-cycles.** When 150–300+ stocks are up 20% in a week, a pullback is imminent. Sell into strength. When readings are low, prepare for the next move.
6. **Fed stimulus = multi-year buy signal.** The Fed doesn't stimulate for weeks — they do it for years. BTFD is the correct strategy during stimulus phases.
7. **In bear markets, everything moves together.** Shorts only work when the index is breaking down (SPY down 1.5%+). Timing shorts with index breakdowns is critical.
8. **Bear markets have vicious counter-trend rallies.** Don't mistake a 14% rally for a new bull market. Bear market rallies can last weeks and trap shorts.
9. **Capitulation ends bear markets.** Look for T2108 single digits + primary indicator <200. After capitulation, a multi-year bull market begins.
10. **Policy response creates bottoms.** TARP, stimulus packages, Fed pivots — major bear markets end with government/Fed action. The market often bottoms before the bill passes.
11. **Bull markets last years, not months.** Fed stimulus, corporate restructuring, consumer rebuilding, and policy fixes combine to produce sustained uptrends.
12. **First-leg breakout stocks often don't have a second leg.** Wayfair, Zoom — explosive first moves, then years of sideways action. Don't get trapped in relative strength plays.
13. **Every bull market has a theme.** Identify it in the first 2–3 months (scans will show you), then ride it. Themes run further than logic suggests.
14. **The hardest skill is remaining bullish.** Prematurely exiting a bull market because stocks "feel extended" is the greatest error. The market goes up for years.
15. **Situational awareness is developed, not innate.** Study historical market data, track breadth daily, monitor the Fed, and review the 20% study. These are skills built through repetition.
