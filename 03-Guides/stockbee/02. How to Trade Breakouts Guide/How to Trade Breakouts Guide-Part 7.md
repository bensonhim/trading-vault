## 1. The Theme: Stock Selection by Youth

The breakout setup is fixed (anticipation, reversal, or breakout). What varies is **the stock list** you apply it to.

Part 7 introduces a third vehicle-selection criterion:

> **Youth — IPO age within the last 5 years.**

---

## 2. The Core Thesis: Big Moves Happen Early

> "Maybe the stock might never again make the move again."

Historical evidence:
- **Amazon** — biggest move in first 4–5 years after IPO
- **Qualcomm** — monster move within first 5–6 years. "On the eighth or ninth year... this is where it made the move."
- **Shopify** — moved 4,300% in first 5 years; after that, sideways. "All the move happened in the first five years."
- **Upstart (UPST)** — 1,400% out of the gate; now likely never repeats. "In the first two years. Is there any guarantee that this stock will ever come back and recover? There is no guarantee."
- **MongoDB** — massive run from $34 to $500+ in first few years
- **Shockwave** — 400–500% in first few years

The logic:

> "Once a company becomes older, it's difficult for them to make big moves."

Young companies have **vitality** — new products, rapid growth, undiscovered stories. Old companies are "done, married, had kids, and out of the market."

## 3. The IPO Youth Screen

### 3.1 MarketSmith Version

| Filter | Value |
|--------|-------|
| IPO Date | 2018 → 2023 (5-year window) |
| Exclude | ETFs/CLOs |
| Liquidity | Volume > 300,000 |
| Price | Any (most will be < $50 naturally) |

**Result:** ~1,017 stocks → after liquidity filters: **only ~307 stocks**

> "Is it very difficult to keep an eye on 307 stocks? Answer is no."

### 3.2 TC2000 Version (No MarketSmith Needed)

For those without MarketSmith:

Add a column called **Price Growth Rate 5 Years**:
- Stocks with **no value** = IPO within last 5 years
- Sort by this column; the blanks at bottom are your young stocks
- Apply standard liquidity filter to eliminate thin names

> "You don't need MarketSmith. Yes... You go to US common stocks and put a column called price growth rate five years. When you put a column called price growth rate five years and sort by it, at some stage you'll have stocks which do not have price growth rate. If they don't have a value associated with them, they have IPO in the last five years."

Pradeep warns: TC2000 will include some ETFs/closed-end funds in the blank list, but "once you put the liquidity condition, those closed end funds are going to get eliminated anyway."

## 4. How to Use the Youth List

### 4.1 Run Standard Breakout Scan on It

Apply the same 3 setups:
- Bottom bounce
- Continuation setup
- Consolidation breakout

> "Only 300 stocks. Any one of those sectors, whether there is a bottom bounce or a continuation setup or consolidation breakout."

### 4.2 Build an Anticipation List

Not every youth stock is breaking out every day. Create anticipation lists for those setting up:

- Sort by YTD % positive — identify which young stocks are already leading
- These are the ones with real momentum

### 4.3 Research the Story (Once)

When a young stock makes an abnormal move, **research it once**:
- What is the business?
- Is it a real product/service or a fad?
- Is it in a growth sector (e.g., energy storage, SaaS, fintech)?
- Does it have the potential for a multi-year move?

> "Once you research, do you need to research it again? Answer is no."

**Example — Fluent Energy:**
- Provides energy storage for renewables
- Real business, real sales (~$400M)
- Growth sector
- Worth keeping an eye on for an EP (earnings-driven) breakout

**Counter-example — Gato Silver:**
- A metal/mining stock
- "Which one of these is likely to make a multiyear move?"
- "This is a metal stock, which doesn't necessarily have explosive capacity to make a big move."
- Not a priority

### 4.4 Set "Sugar Babies"

Young stocks that pass research become your **sugar babies** — long-term watchlist items you monitor for repeated entries:

> "Can we find one of them early in its move and ride that trend?"

Pradeep's process: "Every day I spend half an hour trying to look at the new stocks... the stocks which already have made a move, you don't need to research them. They're done. They already married, had kids, and they're out of the market. What you want is the new new kid on the block."

## 5. Pradeep's Current Priority Framework

This is the synthesis of all 7 parts:

| Priority | List | Purpose |
|----------|------|---------|
| **#1** | 5,000+ funds (9 stocks) | **Size** — large position, low anxiety |
| **#2** | Youth IPO < 5 years (~307 stocks) | **New discoveries** — explosive potential, story stocks |
| **#3** | Bucket zero / Bucket one | **Real growth** — sales + momentum validated companies |

These three lists cover different risk profiles:
- **Fund ownership** list = stability, weighted by institutional consensus
- **Youth list** = vitality, story-driven, higher explosive potential
- **Bucket list** = revenue momentum, real business growth

> "If you do this every day, is there any probability of me missing something new like MongoDB? Like, the next time, is there a probability that I will miss a Shopify? Is there a probability that I'm going to miss one of the newer ideas? The answer is no."

---

## 6. The Key Distinction: Youth ≠ Bucket Zero

| | Youth | Bucket Zero |
|---|---|---|
| **Filter** | IPO < 5 years | Sales growth > 39% |
| **Includes** | IPOs with no sales yet | Companies with proven revenue |
| **Best for** | Story stocks, early-stage discovery | Real business momentum |
| **Risk** | Higher (many fail) | Lower (have revenue track record) |

> "Here the factor is youth. Here the factor is sales. These are two separate factors."

**Recommendation:** Keep both. Youth finds MongoDB before it has revenue. Bucket zero confirms it after sales appear.

---

## 7. Reducing Workload

> "There is no need to jump on every single breakout."

If you operate only from the **youth list** (~307) instead of all stocks (~6,000):

- **Anticipation lists** become smaller and higher-quality
- **Daily review** goes from thousands → hundreds
- **Research** stays fresh because young stocks are constantly entering
- The 9 stocks in the fund-ownership list are trivial to monitor

> "If you do anticipation on all 6,000 stocks, is it better? Or if you do just anticipation on this young list or if you do just anticipation on bucket zero, is it better?"

**Working person shortcut:** "If you're a working person and you really want to do trading and not really worry about this thing, then this might be the better option for you."

---

## 8. Research Once

> "If I research these stocks every week, then over a period of time, you know every single stock."

- Young companies that have already moved = known, done research, no need to repeat
- New names that just IPO'd or just broke out = research immediately
- Focus energy on the **new new kid on the block**, not yesterday's story

**Example — Cardlytics:**
"Cardlytics already had its time in the sun. It made 600% move and then made another 400% move. So this is like an AI to me. Cardlytics is like an AI. Can it come back ever to its old violation? I do not think so. This is a done story."

But Fluent Energy? "Is it worth keeping an eye and understanding fluence and as and when it has an EP then load up on it because it is real business?"

## 10. The Conclusion: Two Things That Matter

> "The two things which are important, very important to think: always think in terms of setup selection. And stock selection."

1. **Setup selection** — anticipation, reversal, or breakout. "Setup equation is cracked. I know what to look for in a good setup."
2. **Stock selection** — which list to apply the setup to.

> "A combination of a good list or a good stock selection... is what is going to determine whether you make 25% or whether you make triple digit."

The entire 7-part series builds to this:
- **Parts 1-2**: Master the setup (breakout definition, entry, exit, stops)
- **Parts 3**: Build the process (watchlists, scanning, execution)
- **Parts 4-7**: Master vehicle selection (volume, revenue momentum, fund ownership, youth)

Together: "If you cover [sales + youth], then you're covered."

## Key Takeaways

1. **Big moves happen early.** Shopify 4,300% in first 5 years. Amazon, Qualcomm, Upstart, MongoDB — all life-changing moves within first decade. After that, probability of repeat is near zero.

2. **Youth list = IPO < 5 years + liquidity > 300K = ~307 stocks.** Manageable. Focused. "Is it very difficult to keep an eye on 307 stocks? Answer is no."

3. **TC2000 alternative exists.** Add "Price Growth Rate 5 Years" column. Blanks = IPO < 5 years. No MarketSmith required.

4. **Research once, know forever.** "Once you research, do you need to research it again? Answer is no." Cardlytics is a "done story." Fluent Energy might be the next one.

5. **"Sugar babies" = young stocks that pass research.** Monitor them for repeated entries. "Can we find one of them early in its move and ride that trend?"

6. **Priority framework: Fund ownership (#1 for size) → Youth (#2 for discovery) → Bucket Zero/One (#3 for validated growth).** Three lists, three purposes.

7. **Youth ≠ Bucket Zero.** Youth finds companies before they have revenue. Bucket Zero confirms them after sales appear. Keep both.

8. **Working people: operate from youth list only.** Reduces daily review from thousands → hundreds. "If you're a working person... this might be the better option for you."

9. **No need to jump on every breakout.** "There is no need to jump on every single breakout. There is absolutely no need."

10. **The entire series conclusion:** Setup selection (cracked in Parts 1-3) + Stock selection (mastered in Parts 4-7) = the difference between 25% returns and triple digits.
