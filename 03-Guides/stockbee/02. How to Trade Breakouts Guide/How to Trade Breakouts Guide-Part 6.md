## Summary

Part 6 introduces **five fund-ownership buckets** as a vehicle-selection framework. The core thesis: **the more funds own a stock, the more stable it is** — but the fewer funds own it, the more explosive the potential when discovery happens. Pradeep created this for his own scaling problem: as his account grew, he couldn't put large size on micro-caps without moving the market. The session covers Bucket A (5,000+ funds, only 9 stocks — includes a market health indicator), Bucket B (2,500+ funds, ~125 stocks), Bucket C (Kevin Marder's "Liquid Glamour" + IPO < 5 years, 29 stocks), Bucket D (1,000+ funds + ownership up 9%, 24 stocks), and Bucket E (<100 funds + 39% sales growth, ~50 stocks). Each bucket serves a different purpose: stability, conviction, discovery, or explosiveness. The breakout setup itself never changes — what changes is position size, hold time, and sleep quality.

## 1. Setup vs. Vehicle: Fund Ownership as a Filter

The breakout setup (price action + volume) tells you **when** to buy. **Fund ownership** tells you **what** to buy — and how much conviction to have.

> "You want to select vehicles where there is a very high probability that the move will be explosive."

Pradeep introduces **five buckets** based on mutual fund ownership, each with different risk/reward profiles and suitable for different account sizes.

---

## 2. Five Fund-Ownership Buckets

### 2.1 Bucket A: 5,000+ Funds (The "Sleep Well at Night" List)

| Trait | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Count | Only **9 stocks** |
| Character | Mega-cap, ultra-stable, liquid |
| Purpose | Large position sizing, low overnight anxiety |
| Examples | Microsoft (9,213), Nvidia, Google, Apple, Amazon, UnitedHealth, JNJ, Visa, Mastercard |

**Why this works:**
- More funds = more stability — "if more funds own a stock, it's more stable"
- Breakouts here are backed by institutional conviction, not speculative noise
- Can hold through volatility without panic
- Also acts as a **market health indicator** — when all 9 correct simultaneously, the market itself is correcting

> "Can you put a bigger position in this kind of a stock and hold it and not worry too much about it? The answer is yes."

**Pradeep's surprise finding:** He thought Apple would be #1 by fund count. Actually **Microsoft** is the most widely held (9,213 funds), followed by Google, then Apple. JNJ was also a surprise — "I never thought JNJ would be so widely held."

**Trade-off:** Lower explosive potential. These are not 10-baggers. But with size, a 10% move on a $1M position = $100K.

### 2.2 Bucket B: 2,500+ Funds (The Widely Held Expansion)

| Trait | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Count | **~125 stocks** (includes the 9 above) |
| Character | Large-cap, institutional favorites |
| Purpose | More opportunities while retaining stability |
| Examples | Adobe, Cisco, JPMorgan, Meta, Pfizer, Merck |

**Key insight:** Only 125 out of ~500 S&P 500 stocks are widely held by 2,500+ funds. This itself is a filter — if funds consider these the "most important," they deserve attention.

This list changes slowly — weekly/monthly updates are sufficient.

### 2.3 Bucket C: 1,000+ Funds + IPO < 5 Years (Kevin Marder's "Liquid Glamour" Twist)

**Inspired by Kevin Marder** (recently deceased), who found that stocks with 1,000+ fund ownership make persistent, multi-year moves.

Pradeep adds a **youth filter**:

| Trait | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Count | Only **29 stocks** |
| Filters | IPO < 5 years AND 1,000+ funds |
| Character | Young companies validated by institutional due diligence |
| Examples | Uber, Airbnb, DoorDash, Moderna |

**Why add the IPO filter?**
- A young stock that already has 1,000+ funds must have something special
- Funds don't pile into young stocks by accident — "they would have done some due diligence"
- These are growth ideas with institutional backing
- Allows **larger position sizing** with higher confidence: "I know I'm not alone. The funds are also liking this."

### 2.4 Bucket D: 1,000+ Funds + Ownership Up 9%+ (The Aggressive Accumulation List)

| Trait | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Count | Only **24 stocks** |
| Filter | 1,000+ funds AND fund count increased by 9%+ since last report |
| Character | Institutions are actively buying, not just holding |
| Examples | First Solar, Schlumberger, Enphase, Airbnb, Booking |

This captures the **inflection point**: when funds already holding a stock start adding more.

> "When this happens and if they break down, then you know a lot of these funds are going to get out of it."

**Trade example:** First Solar appeared on this list before its major move — "one of the reasons why I recommended First Solar for the working people."

Pradeep's note: "This list is dynamic. It keeps changing." He initially labeled it "5%" but corrected to **9%** to keep the list under 25 stocks.

### 2.5 Bucket E: < 100 Funds + 39% Sales Growth (The Explosive Neglect List)

For smaller accounts seeking maximum explosive potential.

| Trait | Value |
|-------|-------|
| Count | ~50 stocks (after filters) |
| Filters | Funds < 100 + sales growth ≥ 39% + annual sales > $25M + liquidity > 100K volume + price > $3 |
| Character | Neglected, under-owned, high-growth |
| Example | AEHR: 55 funds → 114 funds after catalyst → explosive move |

**Why this works:**
- Big moves happen when **neglected stocks** get discovered
- Low fund ownership = room for 100→1,000 funds to pile in
- The shift from 55 → 114 funds is what drives the explosive move
- Combines with Bucket Zero/One sales-growth criteria from Part 5

> "These explosive moves are always going to be on stocks which are very low fund ownership... Once something comes in, those stocks make a big move."

**Trade example:** TSCS — found via this bucket, bought because it's a neglected sales-star with a bottom-bounce setup. "Can I really put large size on this? No. But you might be [able to]."

## 3. The Spectrum: Stability vs. Explosiveness

| Bucket | Funds | Count | Stability | Explosive Potential | Best For |
|--------|-------|-------|-----------|---------------------|----------|
| A | 5,000+ | 9 | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | ⭐ | Large accounts, low anxiety |
| B | 2,500+ | ~125 | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | ⭐⭐ | Balanced |
| C | 1,000+ + <5yr IPO | 29 | ⭐⭐⭐ | ⭐⭐⭐ | Growth + stability |
| D | 1,000+ + ↑9% | 24 | ⭐⭐⭐ | ⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Active accumulation plays |
| E | <100 + sales growth | ~50 | ⭐⭐ | ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ | Small accounts, max upside |

**The rule:**
- **More funds owned** = can put larger size, sleep better, lower explosive potential
- **Fewer funds owned** = smaller size only, higher anxiety, higher explosive potential

## 4. Implementation: Same Setup, Different Conviction

> "What setup do you trade on this? Same setup. I trade breakouts and pullbacks."

The breakout setup (bottom bounce / continuation / consolidation) is **unchanged**. What changes is:
1. **Position size** — larger on widely held, smaller on neglected
2. **Hold time** — longer on widely held, shorter on neglected
3. **Conviction level** — higher when funds are actively accumulating

### 4.1 Daily Workflow
1. Import bucket lists (outsourced — sister-in-law generates them)
2. Apply standard breakout scan (combination bullish)
3. When a stock shows up, check which bucket it's in
4. Size the position based on bucket stability
5. Mark charts with fund count (e.g., "5000", "2500", "<100") so you know instantly

### 4.2 Pradeep's Real Trades from These Buckets

- **MongoDB**: Big consolidation breakout from Bucket C (young + 1,000+ funds). "That's why I got in big on MongoDB because it looked to me as a good, nice consolidation breakout on that day. Obviously I got rolled, rolled, rolled. That is a different story, but the original idea was the right idea."
- **MELI (MercadoLibre)**: Found via the 9% accumulation list. "I bought MELI and then held it and got some money out of it. I've been trading around MELI couple of times since then."
- **First Solar**: Working people recommendation from the accumulation list.
- **TSCS**: Bottom bounce from Bucket E (<100 funds + sales growth). "Can I really put large size on this? No."

### 4.3 Pradeep's Chart Labeling System

To avoid checking multiple lists during trading hours, Pradeep manually writes fund counts on TC2000 charts:

> "I had to sit for one hour and write 2,500 or 5,000 on those stocks." And: "If meta was 2,500, then you have to write 5,000. So you have to manually sit and write 125,000 or 125 times."

This is painful but one-time work. Once done, when a stock shows up in the combo scan, he instantly sees "5000" or "2500" on the chart and knows which bucket it's from.

## 5. Kevin Marder and "Liquid Glamour"

Pradeep pays tribute to **Kevin Marder** (deceased), who attended StockBee boot camps and ran a newsletter:

> "He had this thing called what he used to call liquid glamour. And he had done some study in which he had found that the stocks really make a persistent and consistent big move for multiple year or something if they have a thousand plus mutual fund."

Marder's insight: once a stock crosses 1,000+ fund ownership, it becomes "more steady and it makes more linear move."

Pradeep's twist: add the IPO < 5 years filter. "If a stock which has IPO'd in the last five years and gets thousand plus funds, that's like there is something in that stock."

## 6. Fund Ownership as a Sentiment Filter

> "I have a higher confidence level because I know I'm not alone. The funds are also liking this particular thing."

Fund ownership is not just a stock selection filter — it's a **conviction amplifier**:
- A breakout on a stock with 5,000+ funds is backed by massive institutional consensus
- A breakout on a stock with <100 funds is a speculative bet with higher payoff
- A breakout on a stock where funds just increased by 9% is backed by **momentum in institutional sentiment**

The exact number thresholds (5,000 / 2,500 / 1,000 / <100) are arbitrary — what matters is the principle: **use fund concentration as a proxy for stability and institutional validation**.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Fund ownership = stability proxy.** More funds = more deliberate decisions = less overnight panic.

2. **Bucket A (5,000+ funds, 9 stocks) is a market health indicator.** When all 9 correct together, the market itself is correcting.

3. **Bucket C (Kevin Marder's "Liquid Glamour" + youth) = 29 stocks.** Young + 1,000+ funds = validated growth ideas. "Funds don't pile into young stocks by accident."

4. **Bucket D (1,000+ funds + 9% increase) = 24 stocks.** Captures the inflection point where institutions are actively adding, not just holding. First Solar was on this list.

5. **Bucket E (<100 funds + sales growth) = ~50 stocks.** Where the explosive moves happen. AEHR went from 55→114 funds = explosive price move.

6. **The breakout setup never changes.** Same anticipation/reversal/breakout. What changes: position size, hold time, and conviction level.

7. **Mark fund counts on charts.** Painful one-time work ("sat for one hour writing 2,500 and 5,000"), but eliminates list-checking during trading hours.

8. **Larger accounts need this.** "If you have a small account, I don't think any of these ideas make much sense to you." Pradeep created this framework because he can no longer put size on micro-caps.

9. **Working people benefit from widely-held lists.** 9 stocks = trivial to monitor. 125 stocks = manageable. Both provide lower-anxiety entries.

10. **Dynamic lists need periodic refresh.** The 9 and 125 lists change slowly. The 9% accumulation list changes more frequently. Pradeep's sister-in-law updates them daily.
