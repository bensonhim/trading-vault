# Weak Structure Short (WSS) Guide

> **Speaker:** Pradeep Bonde (StockBee)  
> **Series:** Weak Structure Short Guide (4 parts)  
> **Transcripts:** [[../../transcripts/10. Weak Structure Short Guide/|Transcripts]]

## Summary

The Weak Structure Short (WSS) is Pradeep Bonde's **favorite shorting setup** because it works in **all market conditions** — bullish or bearish. The setup is captured by a simple acronym: **"Niagara 3×10, Weak 3×2.5"** — three days of waterfall decline, down 10% or $10, followed by a weak bounce of 1–3 days with each day up less than 2.5%, then shorted on the next negative day near the end of the session. The setup exploits institutional liquidation: large funds continuously selling a position, pausing briefly, then resuming. Stop management is aggressive — half the entry day's range — because the setup either works immediately (next day gap-down and continued selling) or it doesn't. Three sources feed the watchlist: the combination bearish scan, stocks down 20%+ in five days, and the M20/TI65 bearish scans filtered for minor bounces.

## 1. The Setup: Niagara 3×10, Weak 3×2.5

The entire setup is encoded in a single acronym that Pradeep repeats throughout the guide:

| Acronym Component | Meaning | Requirement |
|-------------------|---------|-------------|
| **Niagara** | Waterfall decline | 3+ consecutive **red/selling days** with **no positive day** in between |
| **3** | Three or more days | Minimum duration of the waterfall decline |
| **10** | 10% or $10 decline | Stock must be down **at least 10%** or **at least $10** during the decline |
| **Weak** | Weak bounce | A pause/consolidation after the waterfall |
| **3** | Three or fewer days | Bounce must last **no more than 3 days** |
| **2.5** | 2.5% or less | Each bounce day must be up **less than 2.5%** |

> "Niagara 3×10, Weak 3×2.5. What it means is there should be a waterfall decline of three days plus in a row... Down 10% or $10 during the decline... weak bounce of less than three days... and each of those days should be less than 2.5%." — *Part 1*

### 1.1 Waterfall Decline (Niagara)

The first leg is a **waterfall decline** — 3 or more consecutive days of selling with no green/positive bar interrupting the sequence. The decline must be substantial: **at least 10% or $10** in total.

> "You want continuous selling days... there's no positive bar." — *Part 1*

> "The stock is down 36%... you want a continuous selling days." — *Part 1*

This magnitude matters because it signals **institutional-scale liquidation** — not just routine profit-taking but a large holder (mutual fund, hedge fund) actively exiting a position.

### 1.2 Weak Bounce (Weak 3×2.5)

After the waterfall, the stock attempts a bounce. The bounce must be:
- **Short:** 1–3 days maximum
- **Weak:** Each day up less than 2.5%
- The **weaker** the bounce, the **better** the setup

> "A weak bounce of one or two or three days, not more than three days. And in each of those three days, the stock should not be up more than 2.5%." — *Part 1*

> "Ideal situations are where you just get one or two days bounce, and those are so weak... their stock was not even up more than 0.5% during those things. The weaker the move during this bounce, better it is." — *Part 1*

A strong bounce (>2.5% per day or >3 days) means buyers are stepping in, which reduces the probability of continued selling.

### 1.3 Entry: Next Negative Day, Near End of Day

The short is initiated on the **first negative day after the bounce**.

> "You short on the next negative day and short near end of the day." — *Part 1*

**Timing is critical:** Pradeep explicitly recommends entering **near the end of the trading day** (after ~3:00 PM, or the last 30–60 minutes), not at the open. Early entries often reverse because the stock may have an intraday bounce that fails by the close.

> "You should short this near the end of the day because if you short it early, many times, it will reverse. So in order to ensure that your win rate is higher, you short it near the end of the day." — *Part 1*

### 1.4 Second-Leg Magnitude

When the setup works, the second leg of the decline is typically **similar in magnitude to the first leg**:

> "Most of the time, the second leg of the move will be similar to the first leg... if there was a $10 down, the second leg will be also $10... or around 30% down or 20% down, and you'll see the second leg is also 20% down." — *Part 1*

This symmetry is not guaranteed but is the **expected outcome**, providing a rough profit target framework.

## 2. Finding the Setup: The Combination Scan

### 2.1 No Dedicated Scan Exists

Unlike other StockBee methodologies, there is **no separate scan** for weak structure shorts. The setup is found by **visually filtering** the output of existing bearish scans.

> "There is no separate scan for weak structure short. In order to find weak structure short, you just need to run your combination bearish scan, which is a scan for momentum burst." — *Part 2*

### 2.2 Primary Method: Combination Bearish Scan

The **combination bearish scan** (the standard momentum burst bearish scan) generates a list of stocks showing bearish momentum. From that list, visually identify candidates with:
- 3+ consecutive red days (waterfall decline)
- A minor pause/bounce of 1–3 days

**Example:** CLVT would appear in the combination bearish scan on the exact day it qualifies as a WSS entry — waterfall decline done, weak bounce occurring, next negative day is the short signal.

> "The easiest way to find these stocks is to just run your combination scan and visually identify a stock which has got three down days." — *Part 2*

### 2.3 Secondary Method: 20%+ Decline in 5 Days

A supplementary approach is to scan for stocks that are **down 20% or more in the last 5 days**. Most of these will also show a waterfall decline pattern, and a subset will have the weak bounce required for WSS.

> "Most of the time, these are stocks which will also be down five days, like 20% plus, and then they will have a consolidation. I find almost all of my weak structure short by looking at either combination scan or looking at 20% plus down in five days." — *Part 2*

## 3. Stop Management: Aggressive and Immediate

### 3.1 Stop Placement: Half the Entry Day's Range

The stop is placed at **half the entry day's range** above the entry price.

> "I typically like to put my stops at half of this day's range, half of this day's range... whatever was the range here, $3, you can put a stop after your entry, $1.5 above your entry, and it works." — *Part 3*

**Why so tight?** Because by definition, if the setup is valid, the stock should **immediately** resume selling. A stock that bounces back is a failed setup.

> "By very definition, this setup, if it is going to work, it immediately works... Very close stops is what I would always recommend on weak structure short because the ones which work, the next day immediately start going down. If they're not going to work, you can get stopped out for a very small loss." — *Part 3*

### 3.2 The Next-Day Test

The setup's validity is tested on **Day 1** (the day after entry):

| Day 1 Behavior | Interpretation | Action |
|----------------|----------------|--------|
| Opens negative and continues down | Setup is working | Move stop to protect profit |
| Bounces back / doesn't open negative | Setup is failing | Close with small profit or small loss, move to next setup |

> "Always remember, in this setup, if it is going to work, next day it will open negative and it will continue to go down. If it is not, it's much better to just close the trade with a small profit or whatever small loss and go to the next setup." — *Part 3*

### 3.3 Progressive Stop Movement

As the trade moves in your favor, **move the stop aggressively every day** to lock in profit:

1. **Day 1:** Stop at half the entry day's range
2. **Day 2 (if down):** Move stop to protect Day 1's profit — half of Day 2's range
3. **Day 3:** Reassess — many setups work for 3 days, some for 5

> "After you put in a stop, after that next day if it goes down, then make sure that you are moving your stop to protect that day's profit... I look at moving my stop very close. Half of the day's, the second day's range kind of a thing." — *Part 3*

### 3.4 Gap-Down = Take Profit

If the stock **gaps down big** on Day 2 or Day 3, take profit immediately rather than holding for the full second leg.

> "If it gaps down, it's much better to, like, again, move your stop to protect your profit... Especially if they gap down big, it's best to just take your profit and run." — *Part 3*

### 3.5 Partial Profit Strategy

Pradeep suggests a **partial profit + let partial run** approach:

> "You can take partial profit and let partial run. But close stops and moving your stops aggressively every day to start locking in some of the profit is the key to being profitable in this particular setup." — *Part 3*

## 4. Watchlist Creation: Three Sources

For traders who prefer to operate from a pre-built watchlist rather than scanning daily, Pradeep identifies **three scan outputs** that can be systematically filtered for WSS candidates:

### 4.1 Source 1: Stocks Down 20%+ in Last 5 Days

Scan for stocks with a **20% or greater decline over the last 5 trading days**. From that list, look for:
- Waterfall decline pattern (consecutive red days)
- Stock still near its lows (not recovered)
- A minor pause/bounce of 1–3 days, up less than 2.5% per day

> "Stocks which are down 20% plus, and it still is near low, and it has a waterfall decline is what you're looking for." — *Part 4*

### 4.2 Source 2: M20 Bearish Scan

The **M20 bearish scan** finds stocks that are down 20%+ from their 30-day high. From the M20 bearish output:
- Look for stocks that have bounced slightly (up, but **not up too much** — less than 2.5%)
- Verify the waterfall decline + weak bounce pattern

> "The other place where you can find weak structure short is M20 bearish... they're looking for stocks which are down 20% plus... you start looking at stocks which are down less than 2.5% [bounce] and see if they are weak structure short." — *Part 4*

*Note:* The transcript contains some ASR artefacts in this section ("stocks up 98%" is likely a misrecognition of a phrase like "stocks up too much" or "stocks up big"). The key instruction is to filter for minor bounces from the M20 bearish scan results.

### 4.3 Source 3: TI65 Bearish Scan

The **TI65 bearish scan** identifies stocks with negative momentum relative to their 65-day average. From the TI65 bearish output:
- Again, filter for stocks with a minor bounce (not up more than ~2.5%)
- Look for the Niagara 3×10, Weak 3×2.5 pattern

> "The third thing where you can create a watch list from is TI65 bearish... Again, look for something which is not up 2.5% plus, and then keep looking for stocks which meet your definition of weak structure short, which is Niagara: three days down continuously, then they have less than two to three days of bounce." — *Part 4*

### 4.4 Watchlist Maintenance

Once a candidate is identified, it goes on the watchlist. The watchlist is reviewed periodically (e.g., weekly) to monitor which candidates develop the full WSS pattern and are ready for entry.

> "You can create a watch list and operate from watch list per week." — *Part 4*

## 5. Why WSS Works Universally

### 5.1 The Institutional Liquidation Mechanism

The WSS setup captures a specific market microstructure event: **large institutional holders aggressively liquidating a position**.

> "Whoever is holding the stock starts to liquidate the stock, or big mutual funds or big hedge funds are getting out of that stock. And every day, they come and keep selling. And then there is this small pause in momentum of less than three days. And then, there are no buyers. There are only sellers. And again, selling comes in." — *Part 5 (Bearish Guide)*

This mechanism is **stock-specific**, not market-dependent. Whether the S&P 500 is at all-time highs or in a bear market, individual stocks can experience institutional liquidation. This is why WSS works in **all market conditions**.

### 5.2 The Bounce Quality Filter

The 2.5% bounce ceiling serves as a **demand test**. If buyers were genuinely interested in catching the falling stock, the bounce would be larger (>5–10%). A bounce under 2.5% means:
- No institutional accumulation
- Retail dip-buyers are insufficient to absorb supply
- The liquidation is incomplete

## Key Takeaways

1. **The WSS is Pradeep's only consistently traded short setup** because it works in bull and bear markets alike.
2. **The acronym "Niagara 3×10, Weak 3×2.5" encapsulates the entire setup:** 3+ day waterfall decline, down 10% or $10, followed by 1–3 day bounce under 2.5% per day, then short on the next negative day near end of day.
3. **Entry timing matters:** Enter near the end of the day, not at the open, to avoid intraday reversals.
4. **Stops must be tight:** Half the entry day's range. If the setup is valid, the stock goes down immediately. If it doesn't, it's a failed setup — cut it quickly.
5. **Day 1 is the test:** A valid WSS opens negative and continues down on Day 1. If it bounces, close with a small profit or loss and move on.
6. **Move stops aggressively:** Protect Day 1 profit on Day 2, Day 2 profit on Day 3. Gap-down = take profit.
7. **No dedicated scan exists:** Use the combination bearish scan and visually filter for the pattern. Supplement with stocks down 20%+ in 5 days, M20 bearish, or TI65 bearish.
8. **Second leg ≈ first leg:** Expect a decline of similar magnitude to the initial waterfall.
9. **Watchlist construction:** Filter the three scan sources (20% in 5 days, M20 bearish, TI65 bearish) for minor bounces, then monitor weekly.
10. **The setup reflects institutional liquidation:** The waterfall is a large seller exiting; the weak bounce proves no buyers exist to absorb the supply.
