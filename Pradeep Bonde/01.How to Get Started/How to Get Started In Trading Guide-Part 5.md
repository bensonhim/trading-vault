# How to Get Started In Trading Guide – Part 5

**Source:** https://stockbee-videos.b-cdn.net/2021-10-18-how-to-get-started-in-trading/2021-10-18-how-to-get-started-in-trading.mp4

## Summary

Part 5 is the most radical session in the entire series. Pradeep strips swing trading down to its skeleton: **there are only three setups — breakout, anticipation, and pullback — and 90% of profitable traders use breakout.** Everything else is a repackaged version of these three. Mean reversion is just pullback. Fibonacci levels are just pullback. Flags are just anticipation. Elliott Wave — run away. His challenge: study 1,000, 10,000, or 100,000 profitable swing traders, and "if you find something different than what I'm telling you, I'll change my name." The session is a call to stop overthinking and commit: pick breakout, find a template, shamelessly copy, and spend six weeks going deep.

---

### 1. The Only Three Setups in Swing Trading

Pradeep opens by connecting back to Part 2 (time frame decision). Once you've decided to become a swing trader, the next question is: **what setup are you going to trade?** His answer is deliberately reductive:

> "If you survey 100,000 swing traders or study the methods of 100,000 swing traders, the number one setup which will show up in most people's thing, vast majority, I would say like 90% of them will be breakout."

**The three setups that 95% of money-making swing traders use:**

| Setup | What It Looks Like | Who Trades It |
|---|---|---|
| **Breakout** | High volume → stock makes new high → buy the move | ~90% of profitable traders |
| **Anticipation** | Build a watchlist → watch for signals → enter when breakout conditions emerge | Some, but they still end up buying breakouts |
| **Pullback** | Wait for an established trend → buy the dip when price retraces to support | The rest; "mean reversion" = pullback with a fancy label |

> "There is nothing else in swing trading. All the other exotic techniques, all those — mean reversion is just a fancy word for a pullback."

> "If you do a six months, six years, ten years, twenty years study of swing traders, and if you find something different than what I'm telling you, I'll change my name from my current name to James Wan."

**What falls under pullback, repackaged:** Fibonacci retracements, mean reversion, "levels"-based methods, any system where you buy after a pullback against a defined level. They are all pullback. The name and packaging differ, the core mechanic doesn't.

> "Even those people who keep talking about Fibonacci — what's that Fibonacci level all about? That's a pullback method."

**What falls under anticipation, repackaged:** Flags, "watchlist then execute" methods. Pradeep's own anticipation setups are essentially watching for conditions and then buying the breakout signal — not predicting before it happens.

> "Flag is just another name for anticipation."

---

### 2. Breakout Is #1 — By a Massive Margin

> "High volume breakout is the most widely used setup in the trading world. There is no other setup which is going to be as widely used as a high volume breakout."

The basic high volume breakout is simple:
- Stock shows a **big volume candle** (1.5× or more its 50-day average volume)
- Some **consolidation** follows
- You **buy the breakout** and **put a stop below the consolidation**
- Let it run

> "Just make breakouts work. Make high volume breakout work. That's as simple as that."

Breakouts don't need to be 100% accurate. Pradeep is explicit: **if 50% of your breakouts work and you put proper stops, you still make money.** There is nothing in trading that works 100% of the time. You don't need perfection. You need to be good enough at one thing.

**Key nuance — breakout doesn't require an uptrend or resistance:**

> "Momentum burst doesn't need to be about resistance. It doesn't have to be about resistance for it to work."

He shows a chart where the stock was breaking out despite having 20% resistance above. The point: a breakout setup can work even when it's not breaking through a textbook resistance level. Don't disqualify breakouts because they don't look "perfect."

---

### 3. Model T Ford vs. Lincoln Navigator — Simple vs. Refined

> "What's the difference between Model T and Lincoln Navigator? It's a hundred years of Ford experience distilled into making a car which is the number one ranked luxury car in the world."

| Level | Setup | What You Get |
|---|---|---|
| **Model T** | Basic 1.5× volume breakout | You're on your own for scans, criteria, selection. Higher risk, but it works. |
| **Lincoln Navigator** | Momentum Burst | Every luxury thought through. Scans, entries, stops — one button, you're in business. |

Pradeep isn't saying you must use Momentum Burst. He's saying: **if you want to shortcut the learning curve, the refined system exists.** But even the basic Model T version — 1.5× volume, buy breakout, put stop below — works. The only trade-off is risk: with a simple breakout, your risk per trade is wider.

> "If you are just going to be doing high volume breakout, you'll still have to figure out all the thing. How am I going to scan for it? What am I going to look at? What is my criteria going to be for selecting? That everything you are on your own."

### 4. The Borat Cheese Scene — All Variations Are Breakout

Pradeep recites the entire Borat supermarket scene ("What is this?" "Cheese." "And this?" "Cheese." "And the veal?" "Cheese.") to make his point:

**Every breakout variation you will ever see is still just a breakout. Different packaging, same core mechanic.**

| "Flavor" of Breakout | Who Trades It | What They Add |
|---|---|---|
| IBD-style breakouts | William O'Neil followers | Fundamental filter: 85+ EPS rating, 85+ RS rating |
| Minervini-style breakouts | Mark Minervini | Momentum filter: stock within 15% of 52-week high |
| Zanger-style breakouts | Dan Zanger | Price filter: only $40+ stocks |
| Sector breakouts | Various | Only buy breakouts in trending sectors |
| Low-float breakouts | Various | Small float as a catalyst filter |
| Darvas breakouts | Nicolas Darvas | All-time high breakouts only |
| Simple 1.5× volume | Beginners | Volume threshold only |

> "Some cheese look blue. Some cheese look shredded. Some cheese come in a big block. But they are breakouts. Just pick one which you like."

Pradeep's point: don't get paralyzed by choice. Don't spend years exploring every variation. Pick one flavor, go deep for six weeks, and make it work. The choice of which "cheese" is less important than the commitment to mastering one.

---

### 5. Breakout Is Easier for Beginners

> "In the beginning, I think breakout is an easier setup to start with. Just high volume comes in, and the stock moves in the direction of high volume buying. Sometimes it doesn't, but most of the time, it does move in the direction of high volume."

Why breakout is the beginner's best starting point:
- **Mechanically simple:** Volume spike → price movement → entry → stop → let it run
- **Directionally clear:** High volume buying pushes the stock in a visible direction
- **Less thinking required:** You don't need to predict — you react to a signal
- **Most documented:** The most traders, books, and templates exist for breakout methods

Once you've decided your time frame (Part 2) and chosen breakout (Part 5), you have clarity:

> "Once you decided your time frame, then you decided swing trading, and then you decided to break out — you're going somewhere. You're going somewhere. And once you do that, you'll make money."

---

### 6. Copy a Template — Not Just the Idea

Pradeep returns to the "shameless copy" theme (Part 4) but now applies it specifically to setups. The key distinction: **find a trader with a complete, documented system, not just an idea.**

**His own example — Mark Minervini / "Mark Puccher" book:**
- "There was a ready made system, completely fleshed out system for buying breakouts."
- "The good thing about this book was that it had the absolute nitty gritties."
- He took that complete template, made it work, then refined it over years.
- "It's no more even closely resembling what he was doing. But he was very successful."

**Why most gurus fail you:** They give stock picks, not process. They don't share their scans, their tactics, the small things that make the system work.

> "Most people will not give you the exact rules of what they're doing because they're superstitious or they think that their edge will go away."

**The 6-week timeline:** If you have a complete template from a proven trader, and you're committed, "probably six weeks to three months." Six weeks is the optimistic target.

**He cites multiple examples of traders you could copy:**
- Oliver Kane — buy his book for $50
- Morris, LB, PCB, Gallic, SDKV2009 — all profitable traders on Twitter
- The key: find someone with documented, recent performance, and **copy their complete approach**

**The corporate parallel (again):** Pradeep found someone at a level below CEO who was very successful at a young age. He "shamelessly copied her approach." Within a few years, he was one of the youngest heads of marketing at that same level. Trading is no different.

### 7. Personality Matches the Setup

Not everyone can buy breakouts. Pradeep acknowledges this:

- **Can't stomach buying something already moving?** → Pullback trading may suit you
- **Need control, want to plan entries carefully?** → Anticipation may suit you
- **Comfortable acting on a signal?** → Breakout is natural

But he immediately qualifies: **anticipation traders still end up buying breakouts.**

> "Anticipation traders also at the end of the day, they end up buying a breakout only. They just put enormous amount of effort in creating a watchlist, and they're watching the watchlist. And then when the stocks start breaking out, they enter into it."

> "It's the same wine. The bottle color is different, labels are different, and the corks are different. Everything is the same thing."

---

### 8. The Natural Progression: Breakout → Anticipation

> "Once you start making breakouts work, automatically, once you become good at buying breakouts, you realize some of these can be anticipated."

The progression is strictly sequential:
1. **Master breakouts first** — this is your foundation
2. After you're consistently profitable, you'll notice: **~25% of breakouts can be anticipated**
3. Start studying those 25% — that's where anticipation begins
4. Anticipation becomes your **next logical step**, never your first step

He also drops a specific number: **the 4% threshold** for Momentum Burst entries. "I found 4% as the optimum percentage." This is a concrete scan parameter he uses for anticipating which breakouts are about to fire.

---

### 9. Commit — Marry Your Setup

The dating analogy:

> "You date a lot, and then you find one partner for three to six months. Don't move in. After six months, you move in, and then things start happening."

> "Breakout trader — you marry breakout. Things will happen."

**The career-ending mistake:** Most beginners never reach the commitment stage. They bounce from day trading to swing trading, from gold stocks to uranium stocks, from one guru to another. Their trading career gets **aborted before they even decide which setup to trade.**

> "They never reach this stage. Their career gets aborted in trading before they even reach this stage of deciding which setup to trade."

---

### 10. The Exotic Methods Warning — Run Away

> "If you see all these exotic methods — run as fast as you can away from them."

Elliott Wave, Fibonacci, and other exotic methods are all pullback in disguise. The people selling them are selling packaging, not a different core mechanic. Pradeep is dismissive because:
1. They waste your time exploring instead of committing
2. They're not used by the people actually making millions
3. The same core mechanic (pullback) is available in simpler, more documented forms

> "There is absolutely nothing new in this business. Everybody thinks that somebody has a bigger organ. And they all have micro organs."

---

### 11. Day Trading — Same Three Setups

In response to a question about day trading, Pradeep is clear:

> "Do day traders buy breakouts? Do they do anticipation? Do they do pullback? Yeah. It's just different timeframe."

There might be a few additional intraday setups possible due to the structure of day trading, but the three core setups remain the same.

> "Swing trading is a 100-year-old art, and it hasn't changed. Only the participants keep changing."

---

### 12. Momentum Burst Doesn't Need Resistance

A specific chart example Pradeep shows: a stock breaking out that had **20% overhead resistance** — a situation where many traders would say "don't buy, there's resistance above." His point:

> "Does stock need to be about resistance for it to work? No. This was a good buy point for this stock."

The breakouts that work don't always look textbook-perfect. Don't disqualify setups because they don't meet every textbook criterion. The high volume is the signal, not the absence of resistance.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Three setups only.** Breakout, anticipation, pullback. Everything else is these three repackaged. Mean reversion = pullback. Fibonacci = pullback. Flag = anticipation.
2. **90% of profitable traders use breakout.** If you study 100,000 swing traders, breakout dominates overwhelmingly.
3. **Start with breakout.** It's mechanically simpler and most documented. Make it work for 6 weeks before adding anything.
4. **All breakout variations are cheese.** IBD, Minervini, Zanger, Darvas — different packaging, same core mechanic. Pick the flavor that fits your thinking.
5. **Copy a complete template.** Find a profitable trader's fully-documented system (not just picks). Shamelessly copy it. Refine only after it works.
6. **Momentum Burst is the refined version.** Like a Lincoln Navigator vs. Model T. Same vehicle, 100 years of improvement. One button vs. figuring out everything yourself.
7. **Breakout doesn't need resistance or uptrend.** High volume is the signal. Don't overcomplicate.
8. **Anticipation comes naturally after mastery.** ~25% of breakouts are anticipatable. Study those only after you've mastered breakout.
9. **Personality affects entry method, not destination.** Pullback traders, anticipation traders — they all converge on the same three setups differently.
10. **Commit or your career dies.** Most beginners bounce between setups and never commit. They lose money, get disillusioned, quit. Decide. Commit. Go deep.
11. **Exotic methods are poison.** Elliott Wave, Fibonacci — repackaged pullbacks that waste your time and money. Run away.
