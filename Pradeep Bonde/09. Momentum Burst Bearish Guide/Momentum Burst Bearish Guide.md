# Momentum Burst Bearish Guide

> **Speaker:** Pradeep Bonde (StockBee)  
> **Series:** Momentum Burst Bearish Guide (5 parts)  
> **Transcripts:** [[../../transcripts/09. Momentum Burst Bearish Guide/|Transcripts]]

## Summary

This guide outlines five bearish short setups for profiting from downward momentum bursts. Unlike the bullish side, bearish opportunities are rarer because markets have an inherent upward bias. However, Pradeep identifies five distinct setups: (1) parabolic shorts on stocks up 300%+ that reverse, (2) weak structure shorts after waterfall declines with minor pauses, (3) momentum burst shorts synchronized with SPY down 1.5%+, (4) consolidation breakdowns when the Market Monitor primary indicator is bearish, and (5) catalyst-driven shorts on high-volume selloffs. Of these five, Pradeep trades only **weak structure shorts** and **SPY-synchronized momentum bursts** — the others require specialized brokers, high borrow costs, or wider stops that he avoids.

## 1. Overview of Five Bearish Setups

The market spends most of its time going up. This inherent bullish tendency means short setups are less frequent, but five distinct patterns exist:

### 1.1 Parabolic Shorts

A parabolic short occurs when a stock has risen 300%+ in a very short period (days to weeks) and then reverses because that momentum is unsustainable. These setups work even in bullish markets because speculative stocks with questionable stories routinely make 500–800% moves and then collapse 80–90%.

> "Some of the stocks like GME, like AMC... made a very big move, and then it collapsed." — *Part 1*

**Why Pradeep avoids them:**
- **Borrow cost:** Hard-to-borrow stocks can cost 70%+ in daily borrow fees
- **Halts:** Multiple intraday trading halts create gap-risk on reopens
- **Wide stops:** Stops need to be 10–20% because first pullback often fails
- **Specialized broker required:** Need a broker that specializes in locating shorts for low-float parabolic stocks

> "Parabolic short... requires your ability to pay for borrow cost. And many times when you're paying high borrow cost, your ability to hold these charts is going to be questionable because the cost of keep on holding the position is going to be much higher." — *Part 2*

### 1.2 Weak Structure Short

The stock is sold continuously for 3+ days (waterfall decline), then has a very weak bounce (pause), and then continues selling. This works in **all market conditions** — bullish or bearish — and is Pradeep's **favorite short setup**.

### 1.3 SPY-Breakdown Sync

On days when SPY drops 1.5% or more, most individual stocks move in lockstep with the index. Taking a short on stocks that are breaking down on the **same day** SPY breaks down is a high-probability setup.

### 1.4 Consolidation Breakdown with Bearish Breadth

A regular breakdown from consolidation only works when the Market Monitor primary indicator is bearish (red). During bullish phases, the same consolidation breakdown fails.

### 1.5 Catalyst-Driven Short

Occasionally, a stock with 100M+ shares traded (e.g., Netflix post-earnings) will have a massive selloff. These are rare 9-figure volume events.

> "Five types of setups. But primarily out of this, I only trade weak structure shot." — *Part 1*

## 2. Parabolic Shorts in Detail — Why Avoid Them

Parabolic shorts are theoretically "extremely profitable" — stocks that have gone up 800% in eight days inevitably collapse 90%. However, Pradeep identifies **four practical barriers**:

### 2.1 Borrow Cost Destroys Profitability

Many parabolic stocks are low-float or heavily shorted already. Borrow costs on hard-to-locate shares can be **70% per day**, meaning a position held for a week costs more than the potential gain.

> "Whenever there is something which is going parabolic, in order to short a stock like this, you will need a specialized broker... those charges for just shorting will be 70%, as a charge per day kind of a thing." — *Part 2*

### 2.2 Trading Halts Create Uncontrollable Gap Risk

Parabolic stocks get halted multiple times intraday. When they reopen, they can gap up 30–50%, instantly forcing a loss far wider than any planned stop.

> "A lot of time, these stocks will have halts during the day... when the stock opens from a halt, it can open at a very high price." — *Part 2*

### 2.3 Wide Stops Required

The first negative bar on a parabolic stock is not necessarily the reversal. The stock can bounce +37% the next day before collapsing. Stops of 10–20% are required, which conflicts with Pradeep's capital-preservation discipline.

> "The first sign, like in a year, this stock went up like 500%, but you try to short. And if you didn't have really tight stop, you can get really stopped out... next day, the stock went up another 37%." — *Part 2*

### 2.4 Specialized Infrastructure

Requires: a locate broker (e.g., CenterPoint, Cobra), willingness to pay high borrow costs, and the ability to monitor halts intraday.

> "Go to the old guide section... Christian Kulamaghi [Kahl] has written a guide for parabolic charts." — *Part 2*

**Verdict:** Extremely profitable on paper, operationally impractical for most traders.

## 3. SPY-Breakdown Synchronized Shorts

### 3.1 Core Principle: Correlation Spikes in Bear Markets

In bearish markets, individual stocks move in near-perfect correlation with SPY. When SPY drops 1.5%+ in a day, almost all stocks — AMD, NVDA, AMZN, GOOGL — break down simultaneously.

> "The market in a bearish market... when the SPY goes down, or SPY has a momentum burst, every stock or most stocks are going to move along with their SPY." — *Part 3*

> "If you see AMD... it's the same thing. Or if we look at NVIDIA... It looks exactly similar. This is the SPY chart and this is the NVIDIA chart." — *Part 3*

### 3.2 Setup Criteria

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| SPY move | Down 1.5% or more in a single day |
| Stock | Any liquid stock (large/mid-cap preferred) |
| Entry | Short the breakdown on the same day |
| Hold period | 3–5 days |
| Expected profit | 10–20% |
| Stock selection | First day of momentum burst (not 3rd or 4th day of decline) |

### 3.3 Why First Day Matters

The first day of SPY's breakdown is when the correlation is strongest and the follow-through is longest. Shorting stocks already down 3–4 days is too late — they may bounce soon.

> "You want to find on that day something which is having the first day. You don't want to be shorting something which is down three or four days in a row." — *Part 3*

### 3.4 Market Context

This setup works **only during bearish markets** — SPY down 1.5% days are rare in bull markets. In confirmed downtrends, these days cluster together.

> "This setup works very well during the bearish market." — *Part 3*

## 4. Consolidation Breakdowns with Bearish Breadth

### 4.1 The Context Rule

A straight breakdown from consolidation works **only when** the Market Monitor primary indicator is bearish. During bullish phases, the identical pattern fails.

> "During that particular time period, if you take a straight breakdown from a consolidation, then it is likely to work... the same consolidation breakdown, if you try and do a short in a bullish market, it does not work." — *Part 4*

### 4.2 Why Breadth Matters

When the Market Monitor primary indicator is red, there is selling pressure across the market. Breakdowns have follow-through because the broad weakness means no buyers step in to catch falling stocks.

> "Periods when you have this extended period of bearishness, that time, if you take a short, it is likely to work... because the market itself, you know, there is selling coming into the market." — *Part 4*

### 4.3 What to Avoid

Pradeep explicitly warns against taking this setup when Market Monitor is bullish:

> "Avoid this trade during bullish market when the momentum, the Market Monitor, is bullish. If you do the same trade when the Market Monitor is bullish, you're not likely to make money." — *Part 4*

## 5. Weak Structure Short — Pradeep's Favorite Setup

### 5.1 The Core Pattern

The weak structure short is the **only setup Pradeep trades consistently** because it works in **all market conditions** (bullish and bearish).

**Three elements:**
1. **Waterfall decline:** 3+ consecutive days of selling (no green/positive days)
2. **Pause:** A minor bounce lasting 1–3 days, typically < 2.5%
3. **Continued selling:** The stock resumes its decline after the pause

> "You get a waterfall decline, you get just a small pause, and then selling continues." — *Part 5*

### 5.2 The Mechanism: Institutional Liquidation

This pattern occurs when large institutional holders (mutual funds, hedge funds) are aggressively liquidating a position. The continuous decline represents sustained selling. The pause represents a temporary exhaustion of sellers or minor dip-buying. The resumption of selling confirms that liquidation is incomplete.

> "The stock has a very weak setup... whoever is holding the stock starts to liquidate the stock, or big mutual funds or big hedge funds are getting out of that stock. And every day, they come and keep selling." — *Part 5*

> "And then there is this small pause in momentum of less than three days. And then, there are no buyers. There are only sellers. And again, selling comes in." — *Part 5*

### 5.3 Quantitative Criteria

| Element | Requirement |
|---------|-------------|
| Waterfall decline | 3+ consecutive days of selling (all red bars) |
| Pause duration | 1–3 days |
| Bounce size | Less than 2.5% (the smaller, the better) |
| Best setups | 1-day pause only |
| Pre-decline drop | 25–36%+ (shows magnitude of liquidation) |

### 5.4 Examples

**AEHR:** Waterfall decline of 25–36%, pause of just one day with a 2.4% bounce, then resumed selling.

**IQV:** Similar pattern — selling, minor pause, continued selling.

> "The stock is down 25% here, or 36%. It just barely manages to bounce back 2.4%. And then here, this is very short." — *Part 5*

### 5.5 Why It Works Universally

Unlike SPY-breakdown shorts (which only work in bear markets) or parabolic shorts (which require infrastructure), weak structure shorts work in **all markets** because they reflect stock-specific institutional liquidation rather than market-directional stress.

> "Weak structure short is my favorite setup on the short side because it works in all categories, in all kind of market conditions." — *Part 5*

## 6. Entry and Management

While Pradeep does not cover position sizing or stop-placement mechanics for bearish setups in this guide, the logic follows from the bullish momentum burst framework:

1. **Entry:** On the first day of resumed selling after the pause
2. **Stop:** Above the pause bounce high (keeps risk contained)
3. **Time stop:** If no follow-through in 3–5 days, exit
4. **Profit target:** 10–20% for SPY-sync and weakStructure; parabolic targets larger but impractical

## Key Takeaways

1. **Bearish opportunities are rarer** because markets trend upward. Only 5 distinct short setups exist within the momentum burst framework.
2. **Pradeep trades only 2 of the 5 setups:** weak structure shorts (universal) and SPY-sync momentum bursts (bear-market only).
3. **Parabolic shorts are theoretically the most profitable** (80–90% drops) but operationally the most difficult (borrow costs, halts, wide stops).
4. **SPY-breakdown shorts exploit correlation:** when SPY drops 1.5%+, most stocks break down simultaneously — entry is the first day of the breakdown.
5. **Consolidation breakdowns require bearish breadth:** they only work when the Market Monitor primary indicator is red.
6. **Weak structure shorts have three elements:** waterfall decline (3+ red days), pause (1–3 days, <2.5% bounce), resumed selling. This is Pradeep's favorite because it works in any market.
7. **The pause quality matters:** single-day pauses are best; multi-day pauses are inferior.
8. **A minor bounce <2.5% is key:** larger bounces mean buyers are stepping in, reducing follow-through probability.

## Note on Terminology

- **"Shot" / "shoot" / "short"** — Pradeep uses these interchangeably to mean short selling (betting on price decline). The transcript spells "short" variously as "shot" or "shoot" when Pradeep's accent compresses the consonant.
- **"Structure"** — The underlying supply/demand balance in a stock. "Weak structure" means supply overwhelms demand.
- **"Waterfall decline"** — A series of consecutive red days with no green days in between, resembling a waterfall.
