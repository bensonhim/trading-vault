## Summary

Part 1 of the "How to Trade Breakouts Guide" demolishes three deeply held myths about breakout trading: (1) that breakouts must occur above resistance, (2) that volume is required for a valid breakout, and (3) that entering on day 2 or 3 is acceptable. Pradeep's core argument: **a breakout is simply a percentage move today that is greater than the percentage move of the last 2–5 days.** That's it. From this definition, everything else flows: entry must be on day 1, stops go at the low of the day (or half day's range), and realistic profit expectations are 8–20% over 2–5 days for continuation breakouts, 20–25% for bottom bounces, and 40–100%+ for consolidation breakouts (over ~40 days). The session is framed as a "belief change" exercise — because beliefs drive behavior, and most traders have the wrong beliefs.

### 1. The Definition of Breakout on StockBee

The single most important concept in this entire session:

> "When we use the term breakout, it means: percentage change today is greater than percentage change in last two to five days."

**In visual terms:** A stock makes a move, then has a minor pullback (2–5 days), then on the breakout day the candle/price move is **bigger than any of those pullback days.** That is the breakout.

**Three types of breakouts under this definition:**

| Type | What It Looks Like | Profit Potential | Hold Period |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Continuation breakout** | Uptrend → 2–5 day sideways → bigger move | 8–20% (typical) | 2–5 days |
| **Bottom bounce** | Downtrend → big range-expansion day up | 20–25% | 10–20 days |
| **Consolidation breakout** | Long sideways base (weeks/months) → breakout | 40–100%+ | ~40 days |

**Critical insight:** Pradeep explicitly does not care about the classical definitions (breakout above resistance, 52-week high, all-time high, range breakout).

> "I do not care about what is your definition of breakout. This is what we mean by breakout."

---

### 2. Resistance Is a Myth

The classical technical analyst's definition of breakout = breakout above resistance. Pradeep calls this "religion" — people treat it like a belief system.

> "To them, breakout means breakout above resistance. And this creates a lot of problems because this is not a breakout which is going to be a breakout above resistance."

**He shows a chart where a stock breaks out WITH tremendous resistance above it, makes a 4-day 5% move, then breaks out again. Was it a valid breakout? Yes. Did it work? Yes.**

> "The most of the best performing stocks will be stocks which had tremendous resistance. And they had this bottom bounce kind of a move, and then they started making a move and they just kept on moving. They kept on moving, giving a fat finger to this whole concept of resistance."

**The "Borat comes to America" analogy:**
- Before visiting America, you think it's full of Pamela Andersons and Tom Cruises (based on TV/movies)
- Then you visit real rural America and realize it's not like that at all
- Similarly, if you've only read books about breakouts, you think breakouts = above resistance
- The reality: most big moves start WITH resistance overhead

> "If you're extreme believer of resistance and support, it's very difficult. And that is also going to be a problem for trading range breakouts."

---

### 3. Volume Myth

Another deeply held belief: breakouts need high volume to be valid.

**Pradeep's answer: No, they don't.**

> "Breakouts don't need volume to work."

**Evidence:**
- Meta (Facebook) breakout: volume was "nothing to write home about" — below average. Result: +59% move
- Goldman Sachs breakout: volume not particularly high. Result: worked
- Small caps / sub-$10: volume DOES matter (liquidity concern)
- Mid+ cap continuation moves: volume just needs to be "slightly above yesterday's volume"

> "If you buy a breakout with volume many times, that breakout will just sit and do nothing. And you buy a breakout which doesn't have much of a volume, and it will go up."

**The volume trap:** Traders wait until end of day to "confirm volume." By then, the stock is up 20%. They're too late.

> "People wait till the end of the day for the volume to show up. And when the volume comes in, by that time the stock is up 20%. So then you're too late to enter."

---

### 4. The Most Important Rule — Enter on Day 1 ONLY

This is the single most important practical rule in the session.

> "The only valid entry for a breakout is first day."

> "If you cannot enter a breakout on the first day, find something else to do. Find some other setup."

**Why day 2/3/4 doesn't work:**
- By day 2, most of the move is already over
- Classic example: **Crocs**
  - People bought on Friday (day 2)
  - Got stopped out
  - Pradeep: "I know at least six or seven people bought it yesterday. Hey, E! I bought Crocs and it got stopped out. When did you buy it? Friday. Why did you buy it on Friday? Because I saw on the timeline it might go $10–$20. Stop fooling yourself."

> "If anybody tells you that you can make money by buying a breakout on second day, third day or fourth day, they are trying to sell you something."

> "Do whatever you need to do to get into a breakout on the breakout day. Before the close, you should be in a breakout on a breakout day."

---

### 5. Stop Placement

**The only valid stop: low of the day (or higher).**

> "The only valid stop for a breakout is low of the day or higher than low of the day."

Logic: If a breakout is genuine, it will not violate the low of the breakout day. If it does, the breakout hypothesis is invalidated.

**Two stop methods Pradeep uses:**

| Method | Calculation | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| **Low of day** | Intraday low on breakout day | More conservative, standard rule |
| **Half of day's range** | (Open + Close) / 2, or simply intraday range / 2 below entry | More aggressive, useful in bull markets |

> "If there is a genuine breakout, it's never going to come back to that half day's range."

**Example where half day's range saved him:**
- Axon breakout — entered early, stock moved up then faded
- Half-day stop got him out with a small loss
- Money was then put into another breakout that worked

### 6. Price-Based Risk Sizing

**High-priced stocks vs low-priced stocks require different risk tolerance:**

| Stock Type | Typical Breakout Size | Required Stop | Pradeep's Personal Target |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High-priced** ($50+) | 2–5% | 1–2.5% | 2.5% max risk per trade |
| **Low-priced** (<$10) | 10–20% | 8–10% | N/A — requires bigger risk |

**Why Pradeep focuses on high-priced stocks:**

> "If I'm putting my stop at half of the day, that means typically breakout of 5% or less. And as a result, most of the things which I do is high priced."

Example: Thermo Fisher — consolidation breakout at 2.38%. Low of day stop = 2.38% risk. Matches his 2.5% constraint perfectly.

> "If you decide that you want to risk less, then you have to go higher priced."

### 7. Three Types of Breakouts — Details

#### A. Continuation Breakouts (2-Leg)

- **Setup:** First leg up → 2–5 day pullback/sideways → continuation breakout
- **Profit potential:** 8–20% in next 3–5 days
- **Hold period:** 2–5 days (up to 10 for higher-priced)
- **Volume requirement:** Minimal — just slightly above prior day

#### B. Bottom Bounces

- **Setup:** Stock in downtrend → big up day that exceeds last 5–10 days' range
- **Works best on:** Established companies (Goldman Sachs, Thermo Fisher) — not biotechs
- **Profit potential:** 20–25%
- **Hold period:** 10–20 days (at best 40 days)
- **Important:** Most bottom bounces are 1-month trades. Holding longer = give back gains

#### C. Consolidation Breakouts

- **Setup:** Stock in long consolidation (weeks to months) → breaks out
- **Profit potential:** 40–100%+ (if it becomes a multi-leg move)
- **Hold period:** ~40 days
- **Volume requirement:** This is the ONE type where volume helps
- **Reality check:** Even "big moves of 40%+" happen in <40 days

---

### 8. The 2-to-40 Day Rule

**All explosive gains in breakouts happen between 2 and 40 days.**

> "Breakouts is a method which is ideally from two to forty days."

> "All the explosive gains in a breakout will happen between two to forty days."

Examples shown:
- Tesla: 61% move took 29 bars (~40 days)
- Random stock: 87% move took <2 months
- Tesla: 187% move took ~40 days
- Taser (TASR): 600% move took ~40 days — but Pradeep notes these are extremely rare (maybe <100 such moves in 40 years)

> "This dream of people have about holding stocks for long term... this is not the method which we are trading."

### 9. Realistic Expectations

| Setup Type | Realistic Target | "Lottery Ticket" (Rare) |
|---|---|---|
| Continuation breakout | 8–20% in 3–5 days | N/A |
| Bottom bounce | 20–25% in 10–20 days | N/A |
| Consolidation breakout | 20% typical, 48–100% if multi-leg | 300–500% in <40 days (extreme rarity) |

**If you want 40%+ moves, you need:**
- Stocks below $10
- Market cap below $1B (ideally below $500M)
- OR massive 9M+ volume breakouts on larger caps

> "A probability of a $100B stock making 40% is very low. It can happen. But if you are primarily looking for that..."

### 10. Belief Change Is Required

Pradeep frames this entire session as a belief-change exercise:

> "In order to make money from breakouts, you have to be willing to change beliefs because beliefs are hard coded and beliefs drive behavior."

**Three beliefs to change:**
1. `breakout = above resistance` → `breakout = bigger percentage move than last 2–5 days`
2. `breakout needs volume` → `volume helps on consolidation breakouts only; continuation moves just need slightly above-average volume`
3. `I can buy on day 2/3 when it's confirmed` → `first day only; before close`

> "So a lot of you have beliefs that if there is no volume, then the breakout is not good... I'll wait till the end of the day for the volume to show up. And when the volume comes in, by that time, the stock is up 20%. So then you're too late to enter."

## Key Takeaways

1. **Breakout definition:** Percentage move today > percentage move of last 2–5 days. Nothing else matters.

2. **Three types:** continuation (2-leg), bottom bounce, consolidation breakout. Each has different profit potential and hold period.

3. **Resistance is optional.** Most big moves start WITH resistance overhead. Don't let classical TA "religion" blind you.

4. **Volume is optional** for continuation breakouts. Only consolidation breakouts benefit from volume. Don't wait for end-of-day volume — you'll be too late.

5. **Enter on day 1 only.** The only valid entry is the breakout day, before close. Day 2/3 entries usually fail.

6. **Stops: low of day (or half day's range).** If the breakout is genuine, it won't violate the day's low. Half-day range is more aggressive but useful in bull markets.

7. **High-priced stocks = smaller risk.** If you want 2.5% risk, trade $50+ stocks. Sub-$10 stocks require 8–10% stops.

8. **Profit potential by type:**
   - Continuation: 8–20% (3–5 days)
   - Bottom bounce: 20–25% (10–20 days)
   - Consolidation: 20% typical, 40–100%+ if works (up to ~40 days)

9. **All explosive gains happen in 2–40 days.** Holding beyond 40 days is usually giving back gains.

10. **Beliefs drive behavior.** If you don't change your beliefs about resistance, volume, and entry timing, you cannot trade breakouts successfully.
