# How to Trade Breakouts Guide — Part 3

**Source:** https://stockbee-videos.b-cdn.net/2023-04-02-how-to-trade-breakouts/2023-04-02-how-to-trade-breakouts.mp4

## Summary

Part 3 is entirely about **process and implementation**. Pradeep's core thesis: knowing the setup is worthless unless you can implement it consistently. The session reverse-engineers the actual trading processes of two masters — **Dan Zanger** (consolidation/bottom bounce) and **Mark Minervini** (consolidation/VCP) — and shows how to build watchlists and workflows for all three breakout types. Key insight: **Dan Zanger holds positions for weeks not because of some secret sauce, but because he trades consolidation breakouts in high-priced, low-volatility stocks with tiny dollar stops.** Once you understand the link between setup type → holding period → stock selection criteria, you can replicate any trader's process.

---

### 1. The Three Types — With Practical Labels

| Setup | Key Visual | Typical Hold |
|---|---|---|
| **Bottom bounce** | Downtrend → violent reversal day that engulfs 5–10 prior days | 1–4 weeks |
| **Continuation breakout** | Uptrend → 2–5 day minor pullback (3–4%) → next leg up | 3–5 days |
| **Consolidation breakout** | Long base (weeks to months) → volume spike → breakout | 3–6 weeks |

Pradeep notes these are simplified; nuances exist (major vs minor bottom bounce, flat vs cup-shaped consolidation, etc.).

---

### 2. The "Template" Approach to Studying Traders

> "Once you have this framework, you can study traders and quickly try and figure out what they are doing."

**The method:**
1. Identify which of the three setups a trader primarily uses
2. Study their actual trades (not what they say, what they DO)
3. Reverse-engineer their process: filters → watchlist → entry rules → stop size → hold period
4. Replicate or adapt to your own constraints

---

### 3. Dan Zanger Deep Dive — What He Actually Does

**First, dispelling myths:**
- Most think Zanger holds for months. **False.**
- > "Anybody who thinks Dan Zanger holds it for months is sadly mistaken... Most of these trades are at best one month. Very few times it is more than a month."
- Average hold: **4–6 weeks** (not months, not years)

**Why he can hold that long:**
- He buys **consolidation breakouts** (at the START of a move, not leg 2/3)
- He selects **high-priced stocks** ($40+, often $300+ during 2020)
  - "Because he's selecting high-priced stocks, he can hold this for that time period. If he was trading $5 or $6 stock, do you think he will be able to hold it for this kind of duration?"
- He selects **lower-volatility stocks**
  - "You have to inherently select something which has lower volatility."
- His dollar stop is small ($1–2), which is a tiny % on a $400+ stock

**His actual process (as reverse-engineered by Pradeep):**

| Step | Action | Details |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Evening | Go through 570 stocks ($40+, +500K liquidity) | "Drink a beer, look at every one of these stocks" |
| 2. Find patterns | Look for flat bases, ascending triangles, channels | "His favorite pattern is flat-flat basis." |
| 3. Create watchlist | 40–60 stocks that meet criteria | "He operates with 40 to 60 stock watchlist" |
| 4. Morning | Monitor watchlist for $1+ price break | "As soon as it breaks out, he buys it" |
| 5. Volume confirm | Relative volume / "Zanger ratio" high | "If he sees there is relative volume is high" |
| 6. Entry | Buy 50K–100K shares | "He will buy anywhere between 50,000 to 100,000 shares" |
| 7. Stop | $1–2 initial stop | <1% on $400 stock |
| 8. Add | If goes in favor, pyramids | "If it keeps going by end of day, he will add to it." |
| 9. Hold vs exit | If works → hold 2–4 weeks. If stalls → dump in 2–3 days | "If it goes in his favor, he keeps it. If it doesn’t, he dumps." |

**His focus patterns (from his own website/materials):**
- Flat bases (favorite)
- Ascending triangles
- Channel formations (especially descending channel breakouts)
- **Not** cup-and-handle (unless earnings breakout)

### 4. Mark Minervini's Process — VCP Approach

**Primary filter:** Trend Template
- Filters universe down to ~392 stocks in a decent market
- In strong bull markets: 1,600–2,000 stocks meet criteria

**Secondary filter:** Look for VCP (Volatility Contraction Pattern)
- "Looking for a VCP pattern... one, two, three small handles forming"
- Longer-term consolidation setting up nicely
- Creates watchlist of 30–40 stocks

**Morning execution:**
- Watches only his buy list
- Buys whichever breaks out first
- "Whichever breaks out first is the way you keep buying."

---

### 5. The High-Price / Low-Volatility Requirement for Longer Holds

This is a crucial nuance many miss:

> "If you want to hold something for longer term... you have to inherently select something which has lower volatility."

| Want to Hold | Required Setup Type | Required Stock Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| 3–5 days | Continuation breakout | Any price, any volatility |
| 1–4 weeks | Bottom bounce | Index components (more stable) |
| 3–6 weeks | Consolidation breakout | High-priced ($40+), lower volatility, liquid |

**The logic:**
- A $400 stock with $2 stop = 0.5% risk → can weather noise
- A $10 stock with $2 stop = 20% risk → gets stopped by normal volatility
- Lower volatility stocks don't whip you out during normal market noise

### 6. Building Processes for Each Breakout Type

#### A. Bottom Bounce Process

**Universe:** NASDAQ 100 + S&P 500 (521 stocks)

**Sort:** By worst momentum (lowest YTD or lowest TI 65)
- > "Where are bottom bounces going to come from? Bottom bounces are going to come from things which are beaten down."

**Build watchlist:** 50–60 stocks near bottom, setting up for possible reversal
- Create list of beaten-down names
- Mark those near logical support / reversal zones
- Include stocks showing minor pullback setups too

**Execute:**
- Morning: watch only this list
- When one breaks out (big range expansion up) → buy
- Prioritize by whichever catches volume first

#### B. Continuation Breakout Process

**Universe:** Top 200–300 stocks by momentum (short-to-medium term)

**Sort:** By 1-month, 2-month, or 10-day gains — Pradeep's preference: 10-day
- > "If you're smart and you really understand this game, you want to sort it by 10 days. You'll find setup very quickly."

**Build watchlist:** 40–50 stocks with clear first leg + pullback
- Eyeball charts for uptrend + minor pullback (20% leg, 3–4% pullback)
- Reject 4th/5th leg setups unless exceptional
- Include some consolidation setups that might also qualify

**Execute:**
- Morning: watch only this list
- React to breakout in real-time (or use combination scan)
- Prioritize by volume, then price, then market cap (for small accounts)
- > "I just find it enormously easy to do all my trading during the day. I find these stocks as and when they're breaking out."

#### C. Consolidation Breakout Process (Dan Zanger Style)

**Filter 1:** Price > $40, Liquidity > 500K–1M shares
- Result: ~570 stocks

**Evening work (2–3 hours):**
- Go through each chart manually
- Look for: flat bases, ascending triangles, channel formations
- Mark stocks showing tight consolidation after a prior move

**Build watchlist:** 40–60 stocks

**Morning:**
- Monitor for $1+ breakout with above-average relative volume
- Entry on breakout
- Stop: $1–2 below entry
- Add if continues in your favor

**Alternative for smaller accounts:** Lower price threshold, lower liquidity

### 7. The Volume Ratio "Gimmick" — Not the Secret

Pradeep demonstrates how easy it is to create a "fancy" indicator:

- Create column: `v / (avg(v,10) / 6.5)` — call it "StockBee Volume Ratio (STKBVR)"
- This projects intraday volume relative to average
- > "I can create a volume buzz indicator very easily... just to show you that Dan Zanger's ratio is not the secret to what he does."

**The point:** The ratio is just relative volume. It's useful for prioritizing, but it's not why Zanger makes money. **The money comes from the process: finding the right setups BEFORE the breakout, then executing early.**

### 8. Implementation > Ideas

> "The real money in anything is if you can implement it. A lot of people know a lot of things about this thing. But it's the actual process and implementation which makes you money."

**The implementation challenge:**
- Knowing "buy consolidation breakouts" is useless if you don't have a nightly process to FIND them
- Knowing "enter on first day" is useless if you don't have orders ready
- The watchlist approach is the bridge between idea and execution

**Pradeep's comment on Stockbee Lab:**
- Software in development to warehouse 50–100 orders before market open
- Allows pre-planned entries on watchlist stocks without manual order entry
- > "You can keep orders ready... as and when you see that it is breaking out, you quickly press one button and the order goes."

### 10. Key Insight: Matching Setup to Lifestyle

> "If you are a working person, realistically, this is not where you should be. You should be doing consolidation breakout."

| Trader Profile | Best Setup Type | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Active / full-time | Continuation breakout | High frequency, short hold, day monitoring |
| Working / part-time | Consolidation breakout | Lower frequency, 30–60 min evening prep, hold weeks |
| Bottom-fisher / contrarian | Bottom bounce | Occasional, clusters at corrections, hold weeks |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Implementation beats knowledge.** Knowing the setup is 10%; having the process to find and trade it daily is 90%.

2. **Dan Zanger's secret is not a ratio — it's the process.** High-priced stocks ($40+) → lower volatility → $1–2 stops → can hold for weeks. His average hold: 4–6 weeks, not months.

3. **Flat bases are king.** Zanger's favorite pattern: flat-flat bases. Not cup-and-handle (unless earnings breakout).

4. **Want to hold longer? You MUST change what you trade.**
   - Continuation breakout → 3–5 days (by definition, it's mid-trend)
   - Bottom bounce → 1–4 weeks
   - Consolidation breakout → 3–6 weeks
   - You cannot force a continuation setup to become a multi-week hold.

5. **Build watchlists the night before.**
   - Bottom bounce: Sort by worst momentum → 50–60 names
   - Continuation: Sort by recent momentum → find pullbacks → 40–50 names
   - Consolidation: Eyeball 570 charts for flat bases → 40–60 names

6. **Morning execution is simple if prep is done.** Watch your list. Buy the breakout. Done.

7. **Volume ratios are useful but not magical.** STKBVR = just relative volume. Don't chase gimmicks.

8. **Order warehousing helps.** Having 50–100 orders ready before the open prevents missing entries.

9. **Working people should focus on consolidation breakout.** 30–60 min evening prep, hold weeks, don't need to monitor all day.

10. **If you know a process, you can replicate it.** Whether it's Zanger, Minervini, or Pradeep — study the actual steps, then copy.
