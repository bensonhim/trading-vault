# How to Get Started In Trading Guide – Part 2

## Summary

Part 2 focuses on one idea: **choose your trading frequency and time frame — it's the single most important decision you'll make.** Pradeep calls it the "most consequential decision" and says if you'd made it on day one, you'd save yourself 2–3 wasted years. Most beginners never make this decision and end up bouncing between day trading, swing trading, and position trading — learning none of them properly.

---

### 1. The Four Roads: Day Trading · Swing Trading · Position Trading · Investing

Once you pick a road, it closes all the other doors — and that's the point. Committing to one path is what frees your mind to build real expertise. Each path demands different tools, different brokers, different scanning software, different temperament.

| Road | Typical Hold | What You Need | What You Give Up |
|------|-------------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Day Trading** | Minutes to hours | $30K+ (US PDT rule), full screen time, high energy | Can't do it with a demanding job; can't do it with $25M+ (your own orders move the market) |
| **Swing Trading** (short, 3–5 days) | 3–5 days, 8–20% moves | Full-time attention, aggressive position sizing | Very high taxes (Pradeep meets his tax consultant monthly); must cut losers fast |
| **Swing Trading** (long, 2–4 weeks) | Weeks to a month | Less screen time but must survive volatility | Must drop position sizing dramatically; net returns shrink correspondingly |
| **Position Trading** | 3 months to 3+ years | Business analysis skill, patience for 30–40% drawdowns, diversification | Chunky returns (800% one year, 10% the next); 3-year learning curve; frog-kiss problem |
| **Investing** | 3–5+ years | Deep research upfront, then passive | Lowest energy but requires 3–5 year conviction; different skill set entirely |

**The critical trade-off:** The longer you hold, the more volatility you must absorb, and the smaller your position sizing must be. Smaller sizing = smaller returns. There is no free lunch.

---

### 2. Why Most Beginners Waste 2–3 Years

Pradeep describes the exact cycle:
1. Try day trading for 3 months → give up
2. Someone says "position trading is where the real money is" → try it for 3 months → give up
3. Try swing trading for 5 months → give up
4. Conclude: "Nothing works, everybody is a fraud"

This is the most common outcome. Not because the methods don't work, but because **no expertise was built in any single area.**

**His MBA analogy:** On day one of MBA school, Pradeep committed to marketing. Never looked at finance, supply chain, or HR. Result: he became good at marketing. Meanwhile, classmates who couldn't decide were still "undecided" in their third semester — expert at nothing.

**His own trading journey:** He admits he spent years chasing only "big moves" (EP trades). He wishes he had committed to swing trading from day one. The only reason he survived is a series of lucky breaks ("fortuitous events") that found him the right path early.

---

### 3. The Four Factors That Should Decide Your Style

Pradeep is explicit: **not everyone should be a swing trader.** Every guru says "do it my way" — but your path depends on:

1. **Personality** — Can you project a business 3 years forward and be patient? Or do you need quick resolution? Some people are wired for daily feedback; others think in years.
2. **Lifestyle** — If you're a trauma surgeon who can't look at a screen for 9 hours, day trading and swing trading are simply impossible. It's not about desire; it's about physical availability.
3. **Age and energy** — Day trading demands staring at 4–5 screens all day. Realistic at 25; probably not at 60.
4. **Capital** — This cuts both ways. Small capital ($5K–$10K) limits you to day/swing. Large capital ($25M+) prevents you from actively trading because your own breakout orders move the market. (He cites the investing championship trick: winners withdraw profits and reset to $100K because it's easier to compound on a smaller base.)

**The capital-size trap:** Trading competition winners don't keep trading with $2M. They withdraw to $100K and start over. Growing from $100K to $200K is easier than from $1M to $2M. Pradeep himself can actively trade ~$35M but cannot actively trade $25M at the day-trading frequency — the market impact is too large.

---

### 4. What Nobody Tells You About Each Style

#### Day Trading
- Can people make money? **Yes.** $1,000/day day trading is achievable.
- Successful day traders have **low drawdowns** and make money irrespective of market conditions.
- Learning curve is **shorter** because the feedback loop is fast — you know within months if your method works.
- But: you need $30K+ (US pattern day trade rule), full-time availability, and intense focus.

#### Swing Trading (3–5 days, 8–20% moves)
- This is Pradeep's bread and butter.
- Requires full-time commitment — "it's a full-time job, no escaping the work."
- The secret weapon: **extreme position sizing**. He runs 50–150% of account per swing trade, holds for 3–5 days, then cuts. This drives huge returns but creates enormous tax bills ("I cry every quarter paying the IRS").
- **Critical point:** If you extend the holding period to 3–4 weeks, you **must** drop your position sizing to ~10% of account. You can tolerate more volatility at 50% sizing for 3 days than at 50% sizing for 4 weeks. So longer swing = smaller position = smaller absolute return. There is no way around this math.

#### Position Trading
- Pradeep gives the most critical analysis here because many gurus push position trading as "the way."
- **Returns are chunky:** One year you make 800%, the next 3 years you make 10–20% or even go negative.
- **The frog problem:** For every stock you think is a "princess" (fixed star), you kiss dozens of "frogs" (meteors) that turn out to be toads. Under Armour went down 70% and will never recover to the price position traders bought it. Same with Teladoc (57% drawdown) and Zoom.
- **Diversification destroys returns:** Because so many picks are duds, position traders are forced to diversify. Diversification + drawdowns = lower annualized returns than swing trading.
- **Learning curve is brutally long:** You don't know if your decision was right for 2–3 years. Day/swing traders get hundreds of feedback loops per month; position traders get one data point per year per pick.
- **Can position traders make money?** Yes. **Do most people have the personality to sit through 3 years of suboptimal returns followed by one big year?** No.

#### Investing
- 3–5 year horizon. Passive after initial research.
- Tax efficient.
- Completely different tools (MarketSmith, research databases, annual reports).
- Not discussed in depth — it's a different world.

---

### 5. Volatility Risk — The Elephant in the Room

This is the single most important concept in the entire talk. Pradeep says **nobody talks about this at the beginning.**

- Day traders and short-term swing traders are, at their core, practicing **volatility avoidance.** They get in and out quickly to avoid sitting through drawdowns.
- Position traders must **embrace** volatility. A 30–40% drawdown is normal. Jim Ruppel's service, for example, shows 30% gains followed by giving it all back.
- The longer you hold, the more volatility risk you eat. To survive it, you must size down. To size down is to shrink your returns. **This is why position trading produces lower annualized returns than swing trading for most practitioners**, despite the "hold longer = make more" myth.
- The gurus selling position trading never show you their 57% drawdowns or their 1,000 dead meteors for every Amazon.

---

### 6. Pradeep's Personal Position and Temptation

He's honest about his own limitations:
- He **can** position trade. He runs a "Working People Portfolio" as a service to his community, and some of his capital is in longer-term positions.
- But a PYPL position trade **"drives me nuts"** with its volatility. After 20+ years, his brain is wired for 8–20% in 3–5 days. He cannot sit through a stock swinging 40%.
- Position trading requires different tools (MarketSmith, databases, research reports) — he has those resources now, but didn't when starting out.
- **Bread and butter** = swing trading with massive position sizing. Everything else is supplementary.
- He meets his tax consultant monthly because of the volume of short-term gains.

---

### 7. Can You Combine Styles?

Yes — but only **after** you've mastered one. Pradeep does a small amount of position trading now, but:
- "If you don't have expertise in both, you'll do a very bad job of both."
- Focus on learning one thing first, then add another. Think of it like compound skills — you master A, then layer B on top.
- The people who fail are the ones doing 1–2 EP trades per year, a handful of swing trades, and looking at position trading "because some guru said so." They have no personality match for anything.

---

### 8. Walk Like an Egyptian

The talk closes with Pradeep playing the 1986 Bangles song and drawing the analogy:

> Decide how you want to walk. Walk like a day trader, walk like a swing trader, or walk like a position trader. Pick one. Invest in that style until it becomes effortless. Don't drastically change your hairstyle halfway through.

People who are already married to a style (and have invested time in it) should **not** switch. The grass always looks greener. Monish, Gallic, Larry, Chris Kahl — they all succeeded by committing deeply to one method and not looking sideways.

If you're still undecided: take 3–4 days to honestly evaluate your personality, lifestyle, age, and capital. Make a decision. You'll thank Pradeep 10 years from now. If you can't decide, you'll spend the next 3–5 years frustrated and going nowhere.
