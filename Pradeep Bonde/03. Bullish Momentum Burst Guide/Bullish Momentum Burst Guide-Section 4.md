## Summary

Section 4 covers what happens after you've found a Two Lynch setup: when to enter, where to place stops, how to size positions, and how to manage the trade day-by-day. The core message is that **entry timing is critical** because momentum bursts are 3–5 day phenomena, and **stop placement is a progression** — you don't use one static stop. The section also covers position sizing using TC2000's built-in calculator and introduces the advanced concept of "peeling" (80/20 profit-taking).

### 1. Youngness: Why It Matters More Than Anything

> "The more the stock has lots of people excited about it, and it's a fourth/fifth kind of a leg of the move — more likely it is going to fail."

| Leg | Failure Probability | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **First leg** | Lowest | Fresh, unknown, no crowd |
| **Second leg** | Low | Still early |
| **Third leg** | Moderate | Becoming visible |
| **Fourth/Fifth leg** | High | Obvious, attracts profit-taking |

**Setups near lows after market bottoms** are among the highest probability:
- The stock just put in a bottom
- First leg is fresh buying
- Very orderly consolidation (buyers are still there)
- Nobody expects the continuation

> "Every time you see a setup like this, when you're running 4% scan, that's when you should get excited."

### 2. Entry Timing: The Earlier, The Better

| Entry Time | Risk-Reward | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **9:30–10:00 AM** | **Best** | Tightest stop, highest follow-through probability |
| **10:00–11:00 AM** | Good | Still early enough |
| **11:00 AM–2:00 PM** | Acceptable | Wider stop, but still viable |
| **2:00–4:00 PM** | Declining | Much wider stop, less day left |
| **After hours** | **Avoid** | Illiquid, wide spreads, partial fills |
| **Next day** | **Do NOT** | Good stocks gap up; late entry = tiny profit window |

**Why early entry matters:**
- A stock that's up 4% at 9:45 AM may be up 15% by end of day
- Your stop at 9:45 might be 2.5%; by 3:30 PM it's 8%
- The earlier you enter, the tighter your stop, and the more capital you can deploy

> "That is why it becomes extremely critical to try and find breakouts early and enter them as soon as you find."

### 3. Stop Placement: A Progression

#### Stage 1: Conservative (Beginners) — Low of the Day

```
Stop = Low of breakout day
Example: Stock entry at $30, low of day = $27.57 → Stop at $27.57
Risk: 8% (for lower-priced stocks) to 3-5% (for higher-priced stocks)
```

#### Stage 2: Aggressive (Experienced) — Half of Day's Range

```
If stock opens at $30, goes to $34 (+$4)
Half range = $2
Stop = $34 - $2 = $32  (or entry minus half-range)
```

> "If this stock ends up giving up half of today's gain, that breakout is no more valid. There is no more momentum burst."

**Pradeep's personal evolution:**
1. **Started:** 3-day low as stop (very wide)
2. **Later:** Low of the day (moderate)
3. **Now:** Half of day's range, or slightly above half (tightest)

**Why half-day-range is conceptually sound:** The entire premise of momentum burst is that the stock should continue in the direction of the burst. If it gives back half of Day 1's gain, the burst thesis is invalidated.

### 4. Hard Stops vs. Mental Stops

| | Hard Stop | Mental Stop |
|---|---|---|
| **Type** | Real order in the system | "I'll sell when it hits X" |
| **Recommendation** | **Use this** | Dangerous — rationalization kicks in |
| **After hours?** | No — only during market hours | N/A |
| **Pre-market?** | No — too illiquid, partial fills |

> "I am not a fan of mental stop. I use hard stops. My stops are only active during the trading hours."

**Why no after-hours stops:** Very little liquidity in pre-market and after-hours. Your stop might get hit at a terrible price with partial fills.

### 5. TC2000 Stop Setup on Chart

Add two items to your chart toolbar:

1. **Bar Low** → Rename prefix to "Stop:" → Shows the low of the day
2. **Custom PCF formula for half-day stop:**
```
O + (C - O) / 2
```
Shows approximately where the half-range stop sits without manual calculation.

**Also add as columns** in your scan view: "Stop" (bar low) and "Stop half" (custom formula).

> "Organize like crazy around your setup. When you see a breakout, immediately you know where to put a stop."

### 6. Position Sizing

Using TC2000's built-in tool:

| Input | Example |
|---|---|
| Account size | $100,000 |
| Risk per trade | 1% ($1,000) |
| Entry price | $29.61 |
| Stop price | $27.57 (low of day) |
| **Result** | 490 shares (~$14,500) |

| Input | Example |
|---|---|
| Account size | $100,000 |
| Risk per trade | 1% ($1,000) |
| Entry price | $29.61 |
| Stop price | $28.63 (half range) |
| **Result** | ~980 shares (~$29,000) |

**Key insight:** The tighter your stop, the more shares you can buy with the same risk. This is why dollar breakout (with 1–2% stops) allows 30–50% position sizes vs. 4% breakout's 10–15%.

**Number of positions:** 3–4 maximum at any time.

> "It's the same as number of kids you have. If you have three kids, it's manageable. If you have two, it's much better."

### 7. Trade Management: Day-by-Day

#### Day 1 (Entry Day)
- Enter as early as possible
- Set initial stop: half of day's range (or low of day for beginners)

#### Day 2 (If Stock Follows Through)
- **Move stop up** to slightly above half of Day 2's range
- Not half of Day 2 (that's still too much risk on a winning trade)
- **Worst case:** You lock in a small profit

#### Day 3 (If Still Working)
- **Aggressive stop management:** Move stop to just below Day 3's close (1–2% below)
- By Day 3, you should have significant profit
- On a $10 stock, you might have 14%+ profit

#### Day 4 (Critical Day)
- **Shift to trailing stop** — 2–3% below the current price
- If stock keeps going up, trail up with it
- If it reverses, you get stopped out with a good profit
- There is NO reason to stay in a momentum burst beyond Day 4–5 for 4% breakout setups

> "There is no reason to stay in a momentum burst, especially a 4% kind, beyond fourth or fifth day."

#### Time Stop: If No Follow-Through by Day 3

If by midday Day 3, the stock has not moved in your direction:

> "By third day, if the stock has not moved, by midday around midday of third day, I'm going to kill that trade for whatever profit I get or loss I make."

The trade is over. Don't hope.

### 8. Advanced: Peeling (80/20 Profit-Taking)

For experienced traders only (not beginners):

- Take a **larger position** than normal (60–80% of account)
- On Day 2 or 3, sell **80% of the position** to lock in profit
- Keep the **remaining 20%** as a "runner" with a trailing stop

> "Peeling a position, doing 80/20, is a very advanced trading technique. I would recommend that in the beginning, you should first get good at doing the conventional thing, and then add those kind of techniques."

**Why not for beginners:**
- Takes focus away from setup quality
- Decisions about when to peel add complexity
- Risk of peeling too early or at wrong time
- Can sub-optimize the trade

---

### Key Takeaways

1. **Enter early** — first 30–60 minutes is the sweet spot; next-day entry is strongly discouraged
2. **Stop progression: Low of day → Half range → Slightly above half range → Trailing**
3. **Hard stops during market hours only** — no mental stops, no after-hours exposure
4. **Position sizing determines everything** — use the calculator; 3–4 positions maximum
5. **Day 3 time stop is mandatory** — if no follow-through by midday Day 3, kill the trade
6. **Day 4 shift to trailing stop** — protect profits, let winners extend slightly
7. **80/20 peeling is advanced** — don't attempt until your win rate is established
