### 1. The Ideal Two Lynch Template

Every example in this section is checked against the Two Lynch checklist. Here's what the ideal setup looks like:

| Criterion | What It Looks Like on Chart |
|---|---|
| **Not up two days in a row** | Day before breakout: flat, slightly down, or at most 0.5% up |
| **Linearity** | First leg: strong, persistent buying; candle bodies progressively higher; no big selling candles |
| **Young** | First leg only, or first leg + second leg. NOT fourth/fifth leg. |
| **Narrow/negative day** | 0% to -2% day before the breakout candle |
| **Consolidation quality** | Compact, 3–5 days of sideways/low-volume pause; no 4% breakdowns; volume declining during consolidation |
| **Close near high** | Breakout day: stock closes within 70%+ of its intraday range |

---

### 2. Example 1: Clean First-Leg Setup (4-Day Consolidation)

**Chart walkthrough:**
- **First leg:** Stock went up with strong, persistent buying — clear linearity
- **Consolidation:** 4 days of compact, sideways movement; no 4% breakdowns; volume was low during pause
- **Narrow day prior:** Day before breakout was slightly negative or 0%
- **Breakout day:** Clean 6%+ range expansion; stock closed near high
- **Not up two days in a row:** Confirmed
- **Young:** First leg only

> "If you really focus on these kind of setups only, then what you'll see is that this kind of setups work very well."

**Result:** This is the template. Memorize it.

---

### 3. Example 2: Second-Leg Setup with Tight Consolidation

**Chart walkthrough:**
- **First leg:** Very strong, aggressive buying
- **Consolidation:** Short, orderly; no 4% breakdown; very low volume
- **Young:** Second leg starting (first leg was established, this is the continuation)
- **Narrow day prior:** Confirmed — 2–3 day pullback before breakout
- **Close near high:** Confirmed

> "So two Lynch: young, narrow or negative day prior to consolidation. Consolidation quality, orderly. There is no 4% breakdown. Stock is breaking out, closing near the high. All boxes it ticks."

---

### 4. Example 3: Minor Up Day Tolerance (Up 0.52%)

**Important nuance:** The stock was "up two days" — but one day was only **0.52%**. Pradeep explicitly says this is acceptable:

> "Some of the setups I'm showing like this because people say, 'Oh, this is up two days in a row.' This was not really up two days in a row because here, the stock is up 0.26%. It is up 0.38%. It's just going sideways before the breakout day."

**Key rule:** An uptick of 0.5% or less does NOT count as "up two days in a row." The stock is effectively going sideways.

---

### 5. Example 4: Longer Consolidation (Stock Went Sideways for a Month)

**Chart walkthrough:**
- **First leg:** Strong buying
- **Consolidation:** Extended sideways period, but still no 4% breakdowns, still orderly
- **Young:** This is a FIRST leg — the stock went sideways for a month, then re-established
- **3-day narrow period** before the breakout
- **Close near high:** Clean breakout

> "Sometimes the consolidation is going to be just three days. I like at least three days of consolidation. Sometimes the consolidation will be longer. This is the example of a longer consolidation."

**Takeaway:** Longer consolidations are acceptable as long as the quality criteria are met: no 4% breakdowns, no high-volume selling, orderly sideways movement.

---

### 6. Variations: Breakouts After Market Bottoms

When the overall market puts in a bottom and starts turning, the setups look **different** from conventional Two Lynch:

- Stocks may not have a clear "first leg" because the market was in a downtrend
- Setups may appear messier but are actually high quality because they're FIRST legs emerging from a bottom
- Biotech stocks during market turns often produce these variations

> "Studying variation of Two Lynch will help you in understanding that in different phases of market, if the market is under correction, the setups look slightly different."

**Practical advice for beginners:** Start by focusing only on A-quality setups. As you gain experience and pattern recognition, add variations to your arsenal.

---

### 7. What to Avoid: The Youngness Criterion

**Critical example:** A stock that looked like a good setup, but:

- **First leg** → already moved up
- **Second leg** → already moved up
- **Third leg** → already moved up
- **Fourth leg** → this is where the breakout appears

> "These kind of setups where the move becomes so apparent and everybody's excited about — these are the setups which most of the time tend to fail."

The same chart pattern that looks attractive on the fourth/fifth leg is the same one that looked attractive on the first leg — but the failure rate is dramatically higher because:
1. Too many people see it now
2. Profit-taking pressure increases
3. The "apparent" setup attracts competition

---

### 8. The Pullback Variation

Another valid variation: the first leg was established, then the stock had a **minor pullback** (not a sideways consolidation, but a brief dip):

- During the pullback: **no volume increase** (no selling pressure)
- Stock then resumes with a 4% breakout
- This is a valid setup because the pullback was low-volume (sellers absent)

> "Understanding of nuances is very important. There are small nuances which you need to notice in a setup."

---

### 9. Building Pattern Recognition

Pradeep's prescribed study method:

1. **First deep dive:** Just understand the 3-day phenomenon — see it on different charts across different time periods
2. **Second deep dive:** Focus on Two Lynch quality — categorize setups as A, B, C quality
3. **Third deep dive:** Study variations — near-low setups, pullback setups, market-turn setups
4. **Ongoing:** Every day, review your scan results and classify them. Over time, you'll instantly recognize quality setups.

> "The more examples of Two Lynch you study, the better you become in this game."

> "If you make a mistake in selecting setup and enter wrong choppy setups, then what is going to happen is your results are going to be choppy."

---

### Key Takeaways

1. **The ideal Two Lynch setup** has: not up 2 days, linearity, young (1st/2nd leg), narrow/negative day prior, compact consolidation (no 4% breakdowns), close near high
2. **Minor upticks (≤0.5%) before breakout are acceptable** — the stock is effectively going sideways
3. **Longer consolidations are fine** — as long as no 4% breakdowns and volume is low
4. **Young = first or second leg.** Fourth/fifth legs have dramatically higher failure rates
5. **Pullback setups work** when the pullback has no volume increase (sellers absent)
6. **Market-turn variations look different** — study them separately
7. **Pattern recognition > theory** — study 300–500+ examples minimum before trading
