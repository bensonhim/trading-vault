### 1. The 366-Day Portfolio Strategy

For working people who want market exposure but cannot actively trade, Pradeep proposes building a portfolio of stocks to hold for **more than one year** — primarily for tax advantages (long-term capital gains rates vs short-term).

**His actual 2020 portfolio:**
- 15 positions from Nasdaq 100 (mostly)
- Only one non-Nasdaq 100 stock (TDG)
- Result: **+27% overall**, with most positions up 39% to 100%+
- **The disaster:** Zoom (became a meteor)
- **The laggard:** Amazon (didn't do much)

---

### 2. Why Nasdaq 100 / Major Indices?

> "They're all part of Nasdaq 100. Do you think these are bucket zero, bucket one? No. These are all Nasdaq 100 stocks."

The logic: start with established, large companies that have already survived过滤器. Not trying to find the next Tesla from IPO — that requires active research (Part 7). Instead, pick from the existing leaders.

**But there's a filter:** Not all Nasdaq 100 stocks are equal.

---

### 3. The Consumer vs Enterprise Distinction

This is a critical framework for selecting long-term holds:

**Consumer companies (durable, understand, hold):**
- Facebook
- Netflix
- Apple
- Amazon
- JD.com
- Marriott
- Disney
- Monster Beverage
- Nike

**Enterprise tech (substitution risk, be cautious):**
- Okta (identity management — "What is the moat for Okta? If Microsoft or Amazon bundles identity into their services, what happens to Okta?")
- Cisco ("Dead company")
- Splunk
- Cognizant

> "Netflix — do you think people are not going to sit on their sofa and watch Netflix till they die? In fact, if he's going to be dying, he will say my last wish is to binge watch some program before I die."

> "Okta and all, their threat of substitution is very high."

The rule: **If you can't explain the moat, don't hold it long-term.**

---

### 4. Avoid Momentum Stocks That Already Ran 800–900%

> "Something which has gone up crazily in last two years or three years, that's more than likely to become a meteor."

**Momentum mean reversion for portfolio building:**
| Stock | Status | Reason to Avoid |
|-------|--------|----------------|
| Tesla | Avoid | Up 1000% from bottom. "What is more likely to happen to a stock which is up a thousand percent?" |
| Zoom | Avoid | Already ran a lot in 2020, became a meteor (confirmed in hindsight) |
| NVIDIA | Avoid | "Already made a big move. Unlikely to make a big move again." |
| AMD | Avoid | Same logic |

> "All these stocks have not participated. So money will flow into Netflix."

The approach: **Buy what hasn't participated yet**, not what already made its move.

### 6. The QQQ Question — Index Investing

What about just buying QQQ (Nasdaq 100 ETF)?

**Problem 1: Drawdowns**
- QQQ has had: 30%, 20%, 37%, 72% drawdowns
- Can you sit through a 72% drawdown?

**Problem 2: Long flat periods**
- QQQ spent **15 years going nowhere** after the 2000 dot-com bubble
- "If you are 50, consider — 14 years or 15 years it did nothing. Your retirement dream will be over. You'll be carrying parts for AutoZone."

**When QQQ makes sense:**
- Very young investors (Pradeep's daughter in her 20s)
- 40–50 year time horizon
- Dollar-cost averaging (buying every month)
- Roth IRA only — tax-advantaged compounding

> "TQQQ TQQQ — remember that. TQQQ is not your solution."

(Note: Pradeep mentions TQQQ, the 3x leveraged Nasdaq ETF, for his daughter's very long-term account, but warns it's not for everyone.)

---

### 7. The Cap Risk in QQQ

QQQ is capitalization-weighted. Current top holdings: Apple, Amazon, Facebook, Google, Tesla.

> "At some stage, what is going to happen to them is the same thing which happens to any market leader. They're going to collapse. They will be replaced."

Example: **JCPenney** was once "the Amazon of the retail industry."

> "When it happens, what do you think is going to happen to QQQ? It is going to spend 15 years going nowhere."

This is why picking individual stocks (especially unloved/underperforming ones from the index) can outperform QQQ — if you have the skill.

---

### 8. Kids / Youth Products = Dangerous for Long-Term Holds

> "Look at all kids related products, kids and teenagers — you should never bet based on that."

**Examples:**
- Roblox — "70% probability it's a meteor. Kids are extremely fickle. If something tomorrow comes in which is better than Roblox, they are going to dump Roblox like a hot potato."
- Remember "Willys" — everyone wanted it, then it disappeared

> "What is the probability that next year they'll be in Roblox? There is no probability."

> "While Phillips 66 will still be around next year. Guaranteed."

**The contrast:**
- **Phillips 66** (energy/refining) — boring, but guaranteed to exist in 20 years
- **Roblox** — exciting, but may not exist in 20 years

For a 366+ day hold, boring and durable beats exciting and fragile.

---

### 9. The Three Things You Need to Understand

For any long-term investment decision:

> "You need to understand brands, you need to understand margins, and you need to understand moats."

**Moat question:** What prevents competitors from taking this company's lunch?
- **Nike** — brand moat (substitutable? No, because brand)
- **Wine** — substitutable product (no moat)
- **Coinbase** — commodity business (lower-cost players coming: IBKR, Fidelity, etc.)
- **Okta** — bundling threat from Microsoft and Amazon

---

### 10. Market Cap at IPO Matters for 10–20x Returns

> "What was the capitalization of Amazon when Amazon IPO? What was the capitalization of Tesla when Tesla IPO? Less than $1 billion. Less than $10 billion."

**The pattern:** The biggest long-term winners IPO'd at small market caps ($1B or less), then grew.

| Company | IPO Market Cap | Current Status |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| Amazon | ~$0.5B? | Mega-cap, multi-thousand % return |
| Tesla | ~$1.7B | Mega-cap, multi-thousand % return |
| Coinbase | $60–65B | Already huge at IPO — limited runway |
| Roblox | ~$40–45B | Already huge at IPO — meteor risk |

> "This kind of stock will get cut into 90%."

The implication: **the easy 10–20x is gone once a company is already $50B+ at IPO.** You're no longer catching the early growth phase.

### 11. Hedge Fund Consensus Lists as Idea Sources

For those building serious long-term portfolios for wealthy clients, Pradeep monitors:

**Hedge fund consensus lists:**
- "50 stocks that matter most"
- "Top consensus stock picks"
- These represent what the smartest money is researching

> "These people have the ability to do due diligence. So then I look at this. And from here also, you can build a portfolio."

**Caveat:** Always look **under** the hood. Don't just copy the list. Hedge funds may hold 100 positions, so their 50th pick may only be 0.5% of their portfolio.

**The filter:**
- Avoid stocks that made 800–900% in the last 1.5 years
- "More than likely they will underperform for next one year or they will go down."
- Focus on stable, established, consumer-facing businesses

---

### 12. Bitcoin Is a Trading Instrument, Not an Investment

> "Bitcoin looks like a mania. It has the looks and feel of mania."

> "All the things based on history of stock market, which I traded and everything — to me, Bitcoin looks like a mania."

Pradeep views Bitcoin as a **trading instrument** (short-term holds) rather than a portfolio-building asset for 366+ day holds. This aligns with his general skepticism of things with "no moat" and pure speculation.

## Key Takeaways

1. **366+ day holds are for tax reasons.** Build a portfolio, hold past the long-term threshold, pay lower capital gains.

2. **Start with major indices (Nasdaq 100, S&P 500).** Don't try to find IPOs if you're busy. Pick from the established names.

3. **Consumer > Enterprise for long-term holds.** Consumer brands (Netflix, Apple, Disney, Nike) have staying power. Enterprise tech (Okta, Cisco, Cognizant) faces substitution risk.

4. **Avoid momentum stocks that ran 800–900%.** They're likely to mean-revert or become meteors.

5. **Kids/teen products are dangerous.** Roblox, youth fads — extremely fickle. Adults stick with brands; kids abandon them overnight.

6. **Understand brands, margins, and moats.** If you can't articulate the moat, you can't hold it long-term.

7. **Big market cap at IPO = limited upside.** The 10–20x returns come from companies IPO'ing at <$1B market cap.

8. **QQQ is for the very young with 40+ year horizons.** Everyone else needs to understand drawdowns and flat periods.

9. **Hedge fund consensus lists are useful idea sources.** But always add your own filter — avoid the recent high-fliers.

10. **Bitcoin = trading instrument, not long-term hold.** View it as speculation, not portfolio construction.
