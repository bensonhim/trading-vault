# Guide 22 - Reversals

## Overview

Reversals are **exhaustion setups**. Pradeep teaches that when selling exhausts itself, a very little buying can turn a stock around because the last seller has sold. This creates a structural edge where you can enter with a tiny stop and capture an immediate reaction — what he calls a **"fallen chick"** setup.

The setup is situational: it thrives when the market itself is oversold or range-bound, and it is almost always a **short-term swing trade** (3–5 days) that transitions into a momentum burst. Pradeep explicitly calls this a **mean-reversion setup with a clear signal** — unlike generic bottom-fishing, it requires a visible exhaustion candle (hammer or doji at the end of a decline).

## Core Concepts

### What Is a Reversal Setup?

A reversal setup occurs after **3 to 5 days (or more) of selling**, where the stock opens low and closes near the high of the day (or prints a doji/hammer), indicating that selling has exhausted itself. Because there are no more sellers, very little buying can push the stock up sharply.

> "Reversal is a selling exhaustion or a buying exhaustion setup... When selling exhausts, the last seller has sold. What happens is a very little buying can turn the stock around."

### Two Types of Exhaustion

| Type | Description | Frequency | Best Use |
|------|-------------|-----------|----------|
| **Minor exhaustion** | Small pullback in an uptrend, 3–5 days of mild selling | Common | Primary focus in current market regime |
| **Major exhaustion** | Deep waterfall decline, often 40%+ from a peak, multi-week selling | Rare | Higher risk/reward, can yield two-leg moves |

Pradeep emphasizes that **major exhaustion was common in old bear markets**, but in modern markets where money rushes in quickly, minor exhaustions are more tradable.

### Situational Awareness

Reversal bullish works best under specific market conditions:

- Market or sector is **short-term or long-term oversold**
- The **market itself has printed a reversal bar** (e.g., SPY reversal)
- Sector has been **deeply oversold** (e.g., semiconductor selloff)
- The stock is **institutional quality** with natural support

> "In setups also, there are seasons, and that way you have to understand."  
> "And that is the largest skill, which is a situational awareness."

## Key Principles

1. **Exhaustion is prerequisite.** The setup is not a pullback; it is exhaustion at the end of a clear decline. The candle close must be near the high or have a long lower tail.

2. **Avoid gap-down exhaustion.** A gap-down open with close near high is a different setup. Pradeep prefers a **waterfall decline with steady selling** followed by a hammer/doji.

3. **Trade only in the last 5–15 minutes.** Run the scan at 3:00–3:30 PM, but enter at 3:50–3:58. Waiting ensures the candle shape is confirmed.

4. **If it doesn't work the next day, move on.** Do not overstay. These are momentum burst trades, not long-term holds.

5. **Use a tiny stop.** Pradeep's core philosophy: always aim for the smallest possible risk. On reversals, stop is **2.5% or less**.

6. **Next-day stop placement.** Put the stop the morning after entry — not on the entry day. If it opens positive, move stop to breakeven.

7. **Institutional quality only.** Focus on stocks owned by at least **1,000 funds**, ideally 2,500. They have support from fund buyers on dips.

8. **No penny stocks / biotech junk.** Do not trade reversals on $3–$5 stocks or speculative biotech. The probability of a dead-cat bounce from uninformed buyers is too high.

9. **Process timing.** 3:30–4:00 PM is the ideal window because morning hours are for SIPs, EPs, and breakouts. Reversals fill the otherwise dead afternoon time.

10. **Setup clarity above all.** Build a **300-example booklet** of reversal charts so you know exactly what "perfect" looks like. Without setup mind clarity you will dilute your criteria and lose.

> "If I apply my total understanding and my talent, will I be able to find setups where with the wider stop we can make money? Yes. But does it appeal to me? The answer is no."  
> "I'm only interested in setup ideas where I can get in with less than 2.5% stop."

## Scan Formulas

### Reversal Bullish (Primary Scan)

The scan is an **anticipation method**: it finds stocks where selling has exhausted and the candle structure suggests immediate reversal potential.

- **Run at:** 3:00–3:30 PM; **enter** in last 5 minutes (ideally 3:50–3:58)
- **Sort by:** `% change` so positive candidates appear at top
- **Filter by:** Price > $5 (or $10 preferred), institutional ownership (1,000+ funds ideally) — these are Pradeep's discretionary refinements, not necessarily hard-coded

Pradeep also suggests creating a **"Reversal Bullish New"** variation and a **"Reversal Bearish"** scan, but he does not prioritize the bearish version because most of his capital is long.

#### Alternative: Watch-List Method

Instead of relying solely on the scan, go through:
- **Stocks owned by 2,500 funds**
- **Bucket zero / momentum leader list**

Each night, manually scan ~2,500 stocks for exhaustion bars. Pradeep says a combination of scan + watch-list elbow grease is best because "the scan doesn't always do a good job of finding every one of these."

## Entry / Stop / Sizing Rules

| Parameter | Rule |
|-----------|------|
| **Entry timing** | 3:50–3:58 PM (last 5–15 minutes) |
| **Entry signal** | Confirmed hammer / doji closing near high after 3–5+ days of selling |
| **Stop on entry day** | No day-of stop; next morning is when stop is placed |
| **Initial stop** | 2.5% or less from entry |
| **Next-day management** | If opens positive → move stop to breakeven or breakeven + small cushion |
| **If it gaps up** | Let it run; that's "free money" |
| **If it gap down** | Close immediately at open; consider gap-down as cost of doing business |
| **Hold time** | 3–5 days (momentum burst horizon) |
| **Profit target** | 8–20% move, or sell into strength with 80/20 rule |
| **Position sizing** | Flat share sizing (e.g., 5k, 10k, or 20k shares) adjusted by market awareness; risk 0.5%–1% of account |
| **Close stop requirement** | Always; Pradeep: "I'm always looking for setup ideas where I can put a stop of less than 2.5% or 1%" |

> "If it makes 10% in one day, make sure that you take eighty twenty. And do it again, and do it again, and do it again."

## Bottom Line

Reversal bullish is a **low-risk, high-probability mean-reversion swing setup**. It is not about catching falling knives indiscriminately; it is about waiting for a **confirmed exhaustion pattern** (hammer/doji at the end of 3–5 days of selling) on **institutional-quality stocks**, and entering with a **sub-2.5% stop** in the **final minutes of the day**.

The edge is structural: when selling exhausts, only a small amount of buying is needed to create a violent snap-back. The trade is often a free-money gap up the next morning. Pradeep's core advice is to **build a 300+ example playbook** so that perfect setups "slap you in the face" and you never dilute your criteria. Combine scan-based discovery with manual watch-list review for the highest win rate.
