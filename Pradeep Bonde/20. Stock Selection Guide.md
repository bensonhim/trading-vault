## Overview

Stock selection is the *second half of the trading equation* — the force-multiplier that turns a good setup into a great trade. Once you have a setup idea (breakout, momentum burst, EP, reversal, etc.), the specific stock you apply it to determines whether you make small money, big money, or no money at all. Pradeep calls this the **"holy trinity": setup + stock selection + essay (execution)**. No amount of setup mastery can compensate for trading the wrong vehicle. This guide breaks down every major stock-selection methodology Pradeep uses — from growth buckets and fund-ownership tiers to factor-based frameworks and theme-driven vehicle selection — and teaches you how to match your selection criteria to your capital, time horizon, and risk tolerance.

---

## Core Concept

### What Is Stock Selection?

Stock selection is **not** a setup. It is the *pre-filter* you run before you ever look at a chart pattern or decide where to buy.

> *"Setup selection is the tools you select. Stock selection is the next part of the equation, which is very important because once you have a setup idea, what stock you're going to be using that on is going to determine whether you make big money or whether you make small money."*

If setup selection answers *"Where do I buy and sell?"*, stock selection answers *"What do I buy?"*. The same breakout on a random $15B slow-moving industrial will produce a 3% pop; the same breakout on a neglected sub-$1B story stock with 9M volume can produce a 50–200% move.

Pradeep emphasizes that **capital and time commitment dictate your selection strategy**:
- Day traders need stocks in play and aggressive intraday velocity.
- Swing traders need themes, catalysts, and 9M breakouts.
- Working people who cannot watch screens need persistent growers or high-fund-ownership names they can hold with wide stops.
- Large accounts need institutional-quality liquidity to deploy size safely.

## Key Principles

### 1. The Holy Trinity: Setup + Stock Selection + Essay

Most traders stop at setup selection and spend years fiddling with indicators. Pradeep argues that once your setup is decided (ideally **once for all**), all additional energy should go to stock selection and execution discipline.

> *"If you have a stock selection strategy and you're buying those stocks on the third or fourth day, it is not going to work. Setup is the foundation. Then stock selection. Then essay specific to that setup and that stock selection. When you get all three together, that is when you have a reliable way to make money. That's the holy trinity."*

Setup answers *how*. Stock selection answers *what*. Essay answers *how much, where, and when to exit*.

### 2. Sales Growth Is the #1 Long-Term Driver

For multi-quarter to multi-year moves, **sales growth** — not profitability — is the engine. Pradeep is blunt:

> *"Sales growth is the number one driver of stock prices over a longer period of time. Short term, it doesn't matter. Short term is story. But if you're looking for something which has made a move for two years, three years, you'll always find that behind the scene, that stock will be having sales growth as the driver."*

He repeatedly cites examples:
- **ENPH**: 26,000%+ move driven by 39%+ sales growth for 26–27 quarters in a row.
- **SHOP**: ~6,700% move in ~5 years, every quarter 39%+ sales growth.
- **TSLA**: From $14 to ~$140 during its real run, every quarter 39%+ sales growth. Now it's sideways because sales growth stopped.
- **NFLX (referenced as NFVES/NFES)**: 32,000% move with back-to-back 39%+ sales growth.

For tech stocks specifically, anything below 39% growth is considered *useless* for explosive moves by Pradeep's standards. Starbucks, CNG, or mature restaurants won't hit that threshold.

### 3. Bucket Method: Catch Young Growth Early

Pradeep runs daily "bucket" scans to isolate young growth stocks *before* the broader market piles in. The key is **finding acceleration in the first or second quarter**, not chasing after seven or eight quarters when the stock is already up 500%.

**Bucket Zero (highest priority)**
- Average sales change last 2 quarters ≥ 39%
- Last reported quarter sales change ≥ 39%
- Annual sales > $25M (to avoid meaningless growth on tiny bases)
- Market cap < $11B (ideally < $10B)
- 50-day average volume > 100,000 shares
- Current price > $1
- IPO / incorporation date within last ~10–11 years (e.g., 2015 to current year)

**Bucket One**
- Same sales-growth criteria as Bucket Zero
- Removes the IPO/age restriction and cap restriction
- Older, larger companies that still have accelerating sales

**Bucket Two**
- EPS % last reported quarter ≥ 39%
- EPS % one quarter ago ≥ 39%
- Average sales change last 2 quarters ≥ 20%
- Profit margin and pretax margin filters optional

These buckets are **mutually exclusive**: Bucket Zero stocks are removed from Bucket One, and both are removed from Bucket Two. The output is a ranked watchlist, not a setup. You still trade your primary setup (SOS, continuation, reversal, etc.) on stocks that appear in these buckets.

### 4. Fund Ownership: The Institutional Quality Filter

Borrowed from the late Kevin Marder, this method divides stocks into two categories: **liquid glamour** (500+ fund ownership) and **speculative glamour** (everything else). Pradeep expanded this into tiers because, as accounts grow, liquidity and stability become non-negotiable.

**Tiers:**
- **5,000+ funds**: "Must own" stocks. Currently ~10–12 names (MSFT, AAPL, AMZN, NVDA, GOOGL, META, LLY, V, MA, JPM, UNH, AVGO). These act as a **market-health gauge**: when they break down, the market is unhealthy; when they start making new highs, the turn is real.
- **2,500+ funds**: ~166 names. Primary list for large-size swing trades. Clean, lower-volatility swings where 8–10% moves with large capital are achievable.
- **1,000+ funds**: ~600 names. Breadth list for idea generation, especially newer IPOs and spin-offs (Reddit, CELH, Astra Labs, etc.).
- **500+ funds**: ~1,200 names. Marder's original universe; too broad for daily review but usable for weekend scans.

> *"If I see something which is owned by 5,000 plus funds, can I go 70% of my account on that idea? With a close stop? I can put all my capital into Nvidia on a clean setup. Will I put 100% of my account on Oklo? Tell me."*

**Smart-fund tracking:** Beyond raw counts, Pradeep tracks high-conviction managers:
- Fidelity Growth Company (FDGRX)
- Fidelity Contrafund
- Morgan Stanley Growth
- T. Rowe Price New Horizons (PRNHX)
- Odyssey Aggressive Growth
- WellRock Capital (Tiger Cub)
- Baker Brothers (biotech)

A **cluster of smart funds opening new positions simultaneously** is an extremely powerful signal (e.g., ELF and LULU in recent years).

> *"When you get a cluster of funds buying an idea at the same time, that is a very powerful signal."*

### 5. Factor-Based Investing: The Quant Playbook

Institutional stock selection is not mystical; it is **factor analysis**. Pradeep breaks factors into two buckets: investment factors (months to years) and trading factors (minutes to days).

**Investment / Position-Trading Factors:**

| Factor | Definition | Retail Application |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Value** | Low P/E, P/B, P/S, EV/EBITDA, positive accruals (cash flow > earnings) | Low-risk basket for bear markets or deep-value plays |
| **Momentum** | Price momentum or earnings momentum; use *offset* rankings (e.g., 63-day momentum offset by 25 days) to strip short-term mean reversion | Don't buy raw short-term leaders; buy the stock that *still* ranks high after the last month is excluded |
| **Quality** | Earning *stability* and *persistence*, low debt, high ROE/ROA | Best for multi-year holds (HEICO, Cintas, Ecolab, AutoZone) |
| **Low Volatility** | Low beta | Reduces drawdowns in long-term portfolios; note: traders should do the *opposite* and chase high volatility |
| **Yield** | Dividends + buybacks | AutoZone bought back 46% of its shares over 10 years; Apple $600B+ buyback |
| **Crowding** | Velocity of fund ownership increase | Rising fund counts on a young stock = institutional validation |
| **Growth** | EPS %, sales %, cash-flow growth | The entire IBD / CANSLIM framework is a multi-factor model |
| **Sentiment** | Analyst ratings, social media sentiment (Goldman Sachs retail basket) | Retail-sentiment factor has outperformed all other factors in recent years |
| **Insider** | Cluster insider buying vs. selling | 76 insiders selling TSLA in a year = warning; cluster buying = validation |
| **Size** | Small-cap effect | Below $1B or below $10B makes the biggest moves |

**Short-Term Trading Factors:**

The quant funds (Citadel, Jane Street, Renaissance, Two Sigma) trade entirely different factors for intraday to 3-day holds:

| Factor | Mechanism | Implication for Your Trading |
|--------|-----------|------------------------------|
| **Mean Reversion** | The #1 short-term factor. Stocks up big in 1–5 days tend to fade; losers tend to bounce. | Don't chase extended stocks; fade spikes after liquidity evaporates |
| **Order Flow / Volume** | Aggressive buying imbalance detected algorithms pile on; when flow peters out, they exit. | Volume is the lifeblood of short-term moves; 40 new highs at the open = follow-through likely |
| **Overnight Gap Fade** | Gaps tend to move in direction of gap for minutes/hours, then fade intraday. | Don't buy the open blindly; wait for the fade or buy only on post-fade continuation |
| **PEITS** | Post-earnings intraday/swing surprise | Surprise moves fast but reverts quickly now because everything prices in within hours |
| **Social Sentiment** | Retail sentiment on Reddit/X | Can create self-fulfilling squeezes on story stocks |

> *"This is why when you hear 'let your winners run' and you get a winner that makes 20% or 30% in one week, what is the probability that that is going to continue? It's low."*

### 6. Select Your Primary Setup — Then Marry It to a Stock Selection Filter

The biggest mistake traders make is trying to master everything at once. Pradeep's primary setup is crystal clear:

> *"Primary setup: EP 9M + momentum burst bullish. Everything else is secondary. Reversal bullish? Nice to have. Anticipation? Nice to have. Sugar Babies? That's a supporting actress, not the main actress."*

Within a catalyst-based framework, his hierarchy is:
1. **Revenue growth / real catalyst** (highest conviction)
2. **Themes / story catalyst**
3. **SIPs** (secondary)

If you are a pattern-based trader, decide: *"My primary setup is momentum burst SOS, buy on the first day."* Then run that setup **only** on stocks selected by your chosen filters (e.g., 2,500+ funds for size, or sub-$1B sugar babies for velocity).

> *"If you are shooting a movie, do you decide the main character first or the side characters first? These are all side characters. Can I make a living just doing anticipation? No. Can I make a living doing reversal bullish? No."*

### 7. Vehicle Selection Must Match Market Phase

The *dominant* stock-selection strategy changes with market cycles:

| Market Phase | Best Vehicle Selection Strategy |
|--------------|--------------------------------|
| **Bubble / Post-COVID** | Themes / story stocks / sugar babies (9M breakout) |
| **Post-crash / Year 1** | Turnarounds with real sales/profit |
| **Recovery / Year 2–5** | Real growth stocks (bucket zero / bucket one) |
| **Year 6+ / Late cycle** | Back to stories |

Currently (as of the latest videos), Pradeep states that **themes have been the best vehicle-selection strategy** and it is not even close:

> *"Currently, the best is themes. Just story stocks. The more bullshit the story is, the more likely it works."*

The top performers have near-zero sales but a compelling narrative (quantum, nuclear, crypto treasury, AI). Paradoxically, traditional growth screens like IBD 250 have underperformed because they *exclude* these story stocks.

### 8. Liquidity vs. Fund Ownership vs. Theme — Three Separate Lenses

Pradeep is explicit that these are **not the same thing**:
- **Liquidity** = how many shares trade daily. Oklo is extremely liquid but speculative. You can do size on liquid names, but that doesn't make them safe.
- **Fund ownership** = institutional validation. AutoZone is owned by 2,500+ funds but trades only ~165K shares/day. It is institutionally validated but not liquid enough for huge size.
- **Theme** = what the crowd cares about today.

You must match the lens to your goal: 
- For rapid small-account growth → themes + small cap + low float + high short interest.
- For stable large-account compounding → 2,500+/5,000+ fund names + close stops + larger size.

### 9. Persistence and Buybacks for Long-Term Investors

If you are a working person or want to hold for years, look at **yearly persistency** and **aggressive buybacks**:
- **AutoZone**: 33 years, only 4 negative years. Bought back so much stock that float dropped from ~150M to 15M shares.
- **O'Reilly**: Similar auto-parts model, persistent growth.
- **Cintas**: Boring uniform business, almost every year positive.
- **Ecolab**: 35 of 40 years positive.
- **HEICO**: 24 of ~30 years positive.

The common factor: **consistent earnings stability, low debt, persistent sales growth, and aggressive buybacks.**

## Scan Formulas

### Growth Buckets (for TC2000 / MarketSmith-style platforms)

**Bucket Zero — Young High-Growth Sales**
- Sales % last reported quarter ≥ 39%
- Average sales % change last 2 quarters ≥ 39%
- Annual sales > $25,000,000
- Market cap < $11,000,000,000
- 50-day average volume > 100,000
- Current price > $1
- IPO / incorporation date within last ~10–11 years (e.g., 2015 to current)

> *Note: Pradeep runs two near-identical scans — one on IPO date, one on incorporation date — because MarketSmith's database can mismatch these fields. The combination eliminates false negatives.*

**Bucket One — Broad High-Growth Sales**
- Same sales-growth criteria as Bucket Zero
- Remove the IPO/age restriction
- Remove the strict cap ceiling

**Bucket Two — Earnings-Validated Growth**
- EPS % last reported quarter ≥ 39%
- EPS % one quarter ago ≥ 39%
- Average sales % change last 2 quarters ≥ 20%
- Optional: pretax margin ≥ threshold; after-tax margin ≥ threshold

**Mutual Exclusivity Rule:** Remove Bucket Zero names from Bucket One; remove Bucket Zero + One names from Bucket Two. This produces three non-overlapping watchlists.

### Fund-Ownership Tiers

- **5,000+ funds**: ~10–12 mega-cap names; acts as a market-health gauge
- **2,500+ funds**: ~166 names; primary large-size swing universe
- **1,000+ funds**: ~600 names; secondary watchlist for new IPOs and spin-offs
- **500+ funds**: ~1,200 names; Marder's original "liquid glamour" universe

Check new additions/deletions monthly. A new name entering the 2,500+ list from below = institutional accumulation. A name dropping from the 5,000+ list = potential distribution.

### RSI / Momentum Persistency Filter

For relative-strength stock selection within a beaten-down market:
- Universe: US common stocks + ADRs
- Liquidity: 100,000+ shares/day
- Price % of 52-week high > 85 (i.e., within 15% of its high)
- Sort by **TI65** or 6-month momentum ranking to find what is *still* strong

### 9M EP / Theme Discovery

> *"The best way to find a theme is by stock characteristic: which stocks show up in 9M breakout."*

- Run **9M breakout scan** daily
- Filter for: sub-$1B or sub-$10B market cap, low float, high short interest, neglected story
- Cross-check with **Sugar Babies** list
- The earlier you catch the theme via SOS/9M, the higher the edge

### Offset Momentum Rank (Anti-Mean-Reversion)

Used by quant funds to avoid buying the recent 1-month spike:
- Calculate 3-month (63-day) momentum
- Offset by ~1 month (25 trading days)
- Rank stocks by that lagged momentum
- The stocks that *still* rank highest are the true momentum plays, not the ones about to snap back

## Working People Adaptation

Working people cannot watch screens all day. Their stock selection must be **low-maintenance and high-conviction**.

1. **Select persistent growers**: Use yearly persistency data. Target stocks with 25+ positive years out of 30–40 (AutoZone, O'Reilly, Cintas, Ecolab, HEICO).
2. **Use the 2,500+ / 5,000+ fund lists**: These stocks don't require babysitting. Reversal bullish on JPM or VISA can be entered with a close stop and checked once a day.
3. **Hold growth via buckets**: Bucket zero / one stocks with real sales growth can be held for 3–6 months if the sales-growth trend remains intact. Exit when the growth decelerates or future quarters guide down.
4. **Avoid day-trading small caps**: Do not try to trade story stocks or sub-$5 names if you cannot watch the market. The risk of a 30% overnight gap is too high without active management.
5. **Weekly = not stock selection**: Using weekly charts is a *time-frame* decision, not a selection strategy. You still need the right stock underneath.

> *"If you don't want to trade too much and you want to buy something and hold for one year or two years, using a weekly chart is not going to help you. What is going to help you is you have to find a stock which is likely to go up for three to six months. Stock selection is the crux."*

## Bottom Line

**Treat stock selection as a separate skill from setup selection.** Build a systematic filter (growth buckets, fund-ownership tiers, factor checklists, or theme/sugar-baby tracking) and apply your primary setup *only* to stocks that pass that filter. The best stock-selection strategy changes with market phase — currently, themes and sugar babies dominate, but real sales-growth stocks will dominate again in the recovery years. Match your selection criteria to your capital size: small accounts should chase velocity (small cap, low float, themes, high short interest), while large accounts need liquidity and institutional validation (2,500+/5,000+ fund names). Above all, **stop searching for the perfect scan and start marrying your setup to the right stock.**
