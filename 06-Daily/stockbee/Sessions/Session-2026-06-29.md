# Cautiously Bullish Monday — Rebalancing Fades, NNBR Catalyst, and the Trade Factory Delivers

Pradeep opened Monday **cautiously bullish** but immediately set the test: Friday's buying had to prove it was real, not just Russell rebalancing. By mid-morning the verdict was in — no true range expansion, morning breakouts fading, and we were back in the chop pit. The AM session drilled the difference between buying a stock and buying a setup, using [[NNBR]] (AI data-center liquid-cooling award) as the day's clearest catalyst example and [[APP]] (Applovin) as the continuation of a textbook reversal-bullish trade. A long sidebar on William O'Neil, IBD, Darvas, and position sizing reinforced that big money requires big size on high-conviction setups and that selling into 20–25% strength is the historical norm. The PM session flipped the tone: the weekend watch list worked — [[Dell]], [[Intel]], [[AMD]], [[Google]], and [[SPCX]] were breaking out — proving once again that doing the work the night before is what creates actionable mornings.

## AM Meeting (9:15 AM)

### Cautiously Bullish, But Test Friday's Buying

> "My situational awareness: cautiously bullish. There are some buying that happened Friday. Let's see if it was real buying or it was something which was just the index rebalancing."

Pradeep's framework question: was Friday a clear range expansion? His answer: no.

> "If this is range expansion, the person on the screen should look like the most handsome person rated among the top 25 handsome persons of all time. His name is Brad Pitt. I don't see that as a range expansion at all."

Without range expansion, the market remains in a choppy, range-bound regime. The playbook: only trade stocks with a clear, articulable reason to go up or down — EP, DEP, EP 9M.

> "When you have a trending market, pattern-based setups work. Here, there is no continuous buying coming in. So anticipation kind of setups don't work. You have to be very careful in what you are going to be focusing on. Focus on something which has a clear catalyst, reason to go."

### Don't Buy a Stock, Buy a Setup

> "You need to buy a setup and not a stock."

> "If you buy random stock, then they don't work. Look at APP or look at Snow. And now look at BLZ. And then those things worked. Why? They were setups."

### William O'Neil, Darvas, and the Fantasy of Position Trading

A long AM sidebar debunked the idea that William O'Neil was a pure position trader:

> "William O'Neil himself said that charts only come in last. He would not buy a stock just based on chart. It has to have institutional sponsorship. It has to have growth characteristic. It has to have six quarters of profitability."

> "He says himself: selling into strength. When you sell like this, you won't be caught in a hard trending 20 to 40% correction that can hit market leaders. Typically, growth stocks tend to advance 20 to 25% after breaking out of a proper base, then decline and set up a new base."

> "Take 8% risk, put an 8% stop, and sell for 20 to 25%. That is what the thing is. But in most cases, it was not even 20 to 25%. Most of the sales were 8 to 12 or 12 to 15%."

On Darvas:

> "People just draw Darvas boxes on random charts. But if you read his later books, he had a checklist — cards he got printed. Is there linearity? Is it up two days in a row? Is it tight? He had eight qualifying criteria."

### Position Sizing and Big Money

> "Is anybody telling you that they make big money and they didn't do big size? I'm telling you they are lying. They are lying."

Pradeep told a story of a famous trader who publicly advises 6–12% risk but privately put 150–170% of his account into his best idea to make his first million.

> "You have no idea what NNBR is doing also. I'm not saying put your account on NNBR. I'm just telling you the reality of what this trading industry is like."

### Reversal Bullish vs Catalyst-Based EP: Different Stop Rules

> "Stop is a function of what the setup is. There is no universal answer to where to put a stop. If this is a catalyst-based EP, you don't put it at low of the day because if it comes to half of the day, the setup is not even valid. If it is a pattern-based reversal bullish like APP, you can have a very close stop."

### Psychology: Repetition and Human Behavior

> "It takes 32 repetition, at least minimum, before an idea is even considered by human mind. And then after that, it takes another 32 repetition for a human being to understand that this is the second day of a swing. So it takes 64 explanations for a person to understand why should have been here, not here. This is why I repeat things so many times because I know how human mind works."

### The Idiot Phase

> "In your early trading career, you take trades with 22% stop. Because when you're in your idiot phase, you're idiot."

## Key Takeaways

1. **Cautiously bullish requires proof** — Friday's buying needed to show real range expansion on SPY/QQQ. It didn't; morning breakouts faded, confirming rebalancing-driven chop.
2. **Only trade catalyst-based setups in chop** — EP, DEP, EP 9M with a reason to go up or down. Pattern-based setups need a trending market.
3. **NNBR was the day's clearest catalyst** — AI data-center liquid-cooling award, capacity sold out, small cap. Sizable potential, but sized small due to Chinese-company uncertainty.
4. **APP validated reversal bullish** — Up ~11% in two days, textbook follow-through after buying exhaustion before the gap.
5. **Buy setups, not stocks** — Random tickers fail. APP, SNOW, BLZ worked because they were setups with catalysts and context.
6. **William O'Neil sold into strength** — Historical O'Neil data shows most trades sold at 8–15%, some at 20–25%, with rare triple-digit holds. Position-trading fantasy contradicts the source texts.
7. **Big money requires big size on conviction** — The public advice of 6–12% risk is contradicted by real stories of traders putting 150%+ of account on their best idea.
8. **Stop is a function of setup** — Catalyst-based EP/DEP: give room, stop near low of day. Momentum burst: half-day stop. Reversal bullish: tight stop.
9. **The weekend watch list paid off** — Dell, Intel, AMD, Google, SPCX broke out Monday. Doing the work the night before is what creates calm execution.
10. **Prioritize 1–3 ideas** — Trade Factory produces endless setups; you can't take them all. Predefine priority, stops, and entries before the open.
11. **Anticipation is for trending markets** — In choppy/range-bound conditions, anticipation setups have lower probability.
12. **Repetition is required for learning** — It takes ~32 repetitions for the mind to even notice an idea and another 32 to understand it. That is why the same lessons are repeated daily.
