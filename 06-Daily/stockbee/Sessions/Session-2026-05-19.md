# Situational Awareness: The Pilot's Framework for Traders

The market is in a choppy distribution phase — breakouts are failing, selling pressure dominates, and the **#1 reason traders blow up is losing situational awareness**. Pradeep draws on the US Air Force's OODA loop framework to explain why: observe the market's breadth signals, orient to what they mean for your setups, decide not to trade when conditions don't align, and act accordingly. In this environment, the right action is often **doing nothing**.

---

## AM Session: Situational Awareness Deep Dive

### 1. The Origin of Situational Awareness

Pradeep traces situational awareness (SA) to the **Vietnam War era**, when the US Air Force was losing planes because pilots were bored, distracted, and not paying attention to formation. The Air Force created a formal SA training program — now mandatory even in commercial aviation.

> "I participated in many of those [SA] meetings… the whole idea of situational awareness is to make sure that you take into consideration the situation and accordingly adjust your plan."

He draws on personal experience working for a **FedEx cargo franchise in India**, where pre-flight SA meetings covered fog, landing conditions, cargo type (live chicks, horses, gold), and emergency planning. SA is **specific to your operation** — cargo pilots care about different risks than passenger pilots, just as EP traders need different SA than day traders.

### 2. The OODA Loop Framework

The US Air Force's decision framework:

| Phase | Meaning | Trading Application |
|-------|---------|---------------------|
| **Observe** | Watch what's happening | Breadth, 20% study, EP 9M scans, watchlist |
| **Orient** | Interpret the observation | "Breakouts are failing" → don't trade breakouts |
| **Decide** | Choose your action | Sit on hands, or trade only specific setups |
| **Act** | Execute | Small size on select setups, or do nothing |

> "Once you act, again, you just do a flywheel… observe, orient, decide, act is a continuous iterative decision-making framework."

The key insight: **it's a circle, not a one-time event**. You constantly re-observe and re-orient.

### 4. Death by a Thousand Cuts

The central warning of the session:

> "Death by a thousand cuts means you will keep buying breakouts, and they will fail. And as a result, you'll have small, small losses and multiple of those losses. And by the time you realize, you'll bleed."

This happens when traders **lose situational awareness** — they keep doing what worked in the uptrend (buying breakouts) even after the market regime has changed. Pradeep compares it to pilots who stopped paying attention:

> "The biggest reason why traders have drawdowns is they lose situational awareness."

> "When this rally started, the narrative was Iran problem is over, and AI became the market narrative. Now? Iran is a quagmire. Bonds have become inflation. That's gonna become a thing."

### 6. Chop Is More Dangerous Than Downtrend

> "Chop is more dangerous than a downtrending move. At least in a downtrending move, you know what is happening, and you can short. Chop is going to tempt you every day to buy or short something, and the next day it's going to hurt."

He expects the market to **range for a few weeks** based on past parallels — after a big move, sideways consolidation for 2–3 weeks is the typical pattern. He references [[SNDK]] which took 3–4 weeks to set up for a next leg.

### 7. Speed of Moves Has Accelerated

> "What used to happen in eighteen months now happens in one and a half months."

This makes SA **even more critical** — you must observe and act faster than before.

### 8. Writing Builds Expertise

Pradeep cites Norwegian neuroscience research showing **handwriting** builds knowledge retention better than typing:

> "There is a Norwegian scientist… if you type, it doesn't really register. But if you handwrite, somehow the brain… you remember much better if you write."

He uses large Post-it notes and Japanese uni-ball pens as his physical SA tools:

> "Tricks of the trade — how to be more creative and how to gain knowledge by writing."

## Key Takeaways

1. **Situational awareness is the #1 skill** — without it, traders die by a thousand cuts in choppy markets
2. **OODA loop applies to trading** — observe (breadth, scans), orient (what does it mean?), decide (trade or sit?), act (execute or walk away)
3. **Choppy distribution = breakouts fail** — both breakouts and breakdowns struggle; the right move is often doing nothing
4. **Death by a thousand cuts** — repeated small losses from forcing trades in the wrong regime
5. **EP 9M near 52-week lows = low quality** — beaten-down stocks appearing in scans don't have follow-through
6. **Market narrative shifted** — from "Iran resolved + AI" to "Iran quagmire + bonds/inflation"
7. **Speed has accelerated** — what took 18 months now takes 1.5 months; SA must be faster
8. **Handwriting > typing for retention** — write your SA daily to build expertise
9. **SA is specific to your setup** — EP traders need different SA than day traders
10. **In chop: sell into strength, peel positions, use tight stops** — don't let winners turn into losers
