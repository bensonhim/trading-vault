# Choppy Transition: Think in Setups, Not Stocks

The market entered a high-volatility choppy period after Friday's momentum burst to the downside; the 20% study at 17 signals short-term bullish potential, but most setups are wide and volatile — only EP 9M and DEP setups with genuine catalysts deserve risk, and even a perfect setup like [[SNOW]] can fail in this environment.

---

## AM Session — 9:15 AM

### Market Context: Cautious, High Volatility

Friday's stronger-than-expected May employment report triggered a 900+ selling day with margin deterioration across all indicators and secondary indicators turning red. [[VIX]] surged. Semiconductor weakness led the sell-off on renewed rate hike fears and AI valuation concerns. Small caps ([[TNA]], [[IWM]]), Dow, NASDAQ, and S&P all closed red with negative range expansion. Crypto slid as Bitcoin dropped below $70,000.

The 20% study landed at **17** — a low reading that historically signals short-term bullish potential. But Pradeep immediately qualified it:

> "So low ratings are bullish. And the high readings are bearish. So you had enough inclination or enough warning that something like this was going to happen."

> "The probability that it will bounce back is high when there is a low reading, but doesn't mean that it is gonna lead to a start of a new bull move. It might transition into a choppy period."

### The Gap-Up Problem

The ideal scenario was a gap-down open — that would have set up a clean bounce play. Instead, the market gapped up, which "killed it." Pradeep warned of three possible outcomes: **gap and fade**, **gap and crap** (high established in first minutes, nothing after), or the gap sticking. Most of the morning's gap-ups started fading within minutes.

> "Just because it is gapping up, doesn't mean anything. So many times you can have a gap and fade."

### Focus: EP 9M and DEP Only

Pradeep's plan of action was explicit:

> "Slow and deliberate risk taking on A plus quality, EP 9M, and delayed reaction setups, selling into strength and peeling to account for anticipated volatility from headline risk."

> "Most setups are now wide and volatile. It will take time to repair."

> "Focus is EP 9M, DEP, DEP 9M bearish, or stocks with three plus EP 9M in one month."

For breakdowns, he stressed the **three EP 9M rule**: after two or three EP 9M volume days to the downside, the short setup works. Going in early doesn't work — you must wait for the stock to start breaking down properly. Three EP 9M bars confirm institutional selling.

> "What does three EP 9M tell you? That institutions are selling. And if institutions are selling, and then it starts working."

### The Core Message: Think in Setups, Not Stocks

> "The moment you start thinking in stocks, you are dead. What should you be thinking? Setup."

> "Setup selection and setups is the key. Otherwise, we are just going to go."

> "My one single best advice to all of you would be to think in setups."

When a setup appears on a stock — like [[SNOW]] as a DEP — that is the time to enter. Not before, not on hope, not on narrative. The setup defines the trade.

Snowflake was Pradeep's top idea for the day: a **Delayed Reaction EP** with a clear catalyst, institutional quality, and a post-earnings pullback that set up the entry.

> "I was watching like a hawk. I am watching Octa and Snow as the number one idea, Snow and the number two Octa today."

By mid-morning, [[SNOW]] was already at breakeven. But the stock struggled to follow through — the choppy market environment was the headwind. By end of AM session, [[SNOW]] was fading.

### The "Organized Like Crazy" Process

> "It is a classic example of what is organized like crazy. How come you people find out this now?"

Pradeep emphasized that finding these setups requires pre-work — scanning, building watchlists, understanding catalysts — done *before* the market opens. He had [[SNOW]] researched over the weekend.

> "Today, all the work on something like this Snow was done before — on the weekend itself, I had the stock researched. I know what is the catalyst."

### Analogies & Color

**Korean Glass Skin** — Used as a metaphor for extreme discipline and process. Koreans work "like crazy" with their 10-step beauty routine. Trading requires the same relentless process orientation.

> "Just need to find now one Korean facial place close to me and go and get my 10 step Korean glass facial done."

**Silicon Valley Escort Story** — A Forbes article about escorts charging $3K-5K/hour to Silicon Valley tech workers was cited as a classic excess signal, comparable to pole dancers buying houses in 2008:

> "Where do you see stories like this? Do you see these stories when the market is bottoming?"

This is the **shoeshine boy** indicator for the AI bubble era — extreme prosperity stories at the top.

## PM Session — 3:00 PM

> ⚠️ **Short session (~14 minutes).** Mostly a reiteration of morning themes with closing assessment.

### Dead Cat Bounces

Pradeep characterized the day's bounce as a **dead cat bounce** — short covering, not genuine buying:

> "It's a choppy period. Today's bounce is just people covering their shorts. It's a dead cat bounce. There has to be follow-through."

> "After running a marathon, can the market run another marathon back to back? Anything is possible. But most likely, this is what we end up into."

### [[SNOW]] Outcome

Snow was stopped out. Pradeep accepted it without regret:

> "Snow was my number one idea. It was a very good setup as a delayed reaction — it ticked all my boxes. It had a catalyst. Everything was right. Volume was there. But it didn't really start."

The setup was Category A. The environment was the problem, not the selection.

### [[DELL]] — Why Not

> "Dell has rallied 221% into this catalyst. So Dell is not my A category setup."

> "Snow is coming out of a huge neglect. That's the difference."

Neglect = explosiveness. Extended stocks don't have the same upside geometry.

### Reversal Bullish Candidates

Only a handful made the list: [[HPQ]], [[DELL]] (more anticipation than reversal), [[TTWO]] (Take-Two), [[SATS]] (EcoStar). Minimal conviction.

### 20% Study Clarification

A member asked if the low 20% study should prompt swing buying. Pradeep's answer:

> "The 20% study being low just tells you that the market is likely to bounce. Beyond that, there has to be a setup which is really good."

> "If you feel you can take larger risk and don't mind getting chopped, then go and do that thing."

The study is a **context indicator**, not a trade signal by itself.

### Closing

> "I don't see any reason to get really super aggressive here. You are going to get churned on the volatility for a couple of days or weeks from here. So be ready."

> "It's summer. It's swimming time, and the pool is waiting."

## Key Takeaways

1. **Think in setups, not stocks.** The moment you think in stocks, "you are dead." Setups define entries, stops, and exits.
2. **20% study at 17 = short-term bullish context**, but does NOT guarantee a new bull move. It may just lead to a choppy transition period.
3. **EP 9M and DEP are the only setups to trade in high-volatility environments.** Regular breakouts have too low a probability.
4. **Three EP 9M rule for shorts.** Wait for 2-3 EP 9M volume days confirming institutional selling before shorting breakdowns. Going early doesn't work.
5. **Even a perfect Category A setup can fail.** [[SNOW]] had catalyst, volume, DEP structure — and still got stopped out. The environment was the headwind, not the selection.
6. **Neglect = explosiveness.** [[SNOW]] coming out of neglect has much better upside geometry than [[DELL]] which already rallied 221%.
7. **Dead cat bounces are not follow-through.** After a marathon sell-off, the first bounce is usually short covering, not institutional buying.
8. **"Organized Like Crazy" = pre-work before market open.** All the research on [[SNOW]] was done over the weekend. The entry was mechanical once the setup triggered.
9. **Gap-ups in choppy markets are traps.** The ideal was a gap-down; the gap-up killed the bounce setup. Three outcomes: gap and fade, gap and crap, or stick — and most fade.
10. **Process over outcome.** A stopped-out trade on a valid setup is not a failure. Catastrophic drawdowns (30-40%) are what you must avoid.
