# Rally Fades at Day Three — Semis Break Down, No Fresh Setups, and the Guide Is the Answer

Pradeep opened July 1 expecting the three-day-old rally to be tested. The 20% study was already in the caution zone, the easy breakouts had happened on Friday and Monday, and the room was filling with "should I buy TQQQ now?" emails. By mid-morning semiconductors were breaking down, Pradeep's ARM and Intel positions were stopped out, and the lesson became clear: this was a range-bound fade, not a trending market. The PM session reinforced that institutions were using breakout liquidity to distribute semiconductor holdings, that there was almost nothing actionable anywhere in the trade factory, and that the only real fix for most members was to go through the StockBee guide.

---

## AM Meeting (9:15 AM)

### Situational Awareness: Third Day of the Swing, Caution

Pradeep framed the day as the make-or-break test for the Friday–Tuesday bounce.

> "Most of the rallies in a range bound market tend to be two to three days early, and we'll see if today we are starting week."

> "The 20% study... it's greater than 100. That's like the... we already are in that territory of caution. So we are already extended."

The "email indicator" confirmed it: members were asking about buying TQQQ and SOXL three days into the move.

> "By email indicator, it's full of people now asking, hey EJ, I missed TQQQ. Is it time to buy TQQQ or SOXS? Should I sell my kids and wife and buy it? ... That's almost invariably third day of a swing."

### Nothing Fresh to Buy

Pradeep had already scanned delayed-reaction EP, EP 9M, DEP, and momentum burst.

> "I went through every single thing or delayed reaction EP. ... Now if I see, I don't even see one good delayed reaction EP setup."

> "There are no DPs to focus on."

The playbook was therefore: manage existing positions, move stops, trim winners, and wait.

### Semiconductors Lead the Fade

Early in the session the semis started giving up ground.

> "A lot of downside alerts. ... It's the semis getting hit again."

> "If breakouts start failing very quickly, that means we are going to go back into the range."

Pradeep got stopped out of ARM and Intel, leaving only Dell, Snow, and APP still working.

### Biotech Warning: Don't Chase the Gap

A member asked about Cure and biotech breakouts. Pradeep explained the sector's specific behavior:

> "Biotechnology stocks have one day big move. And after that, they just go sideways and nothing happens. ... Most of the time, trying to trade biotechnology as an anticipation setup, you will probably end up getting a disease which will need you to get a biotech drug, because you're going to just chop yourself."

> "If you want to trade biotech, better to play it non gap. A breakout in biotech is more likely to work than when they gap up."

### DEP Requires Fund Quality

Pradeep clarified again that DEP is not a generic breakout.

> "The first requirement for DEP is fund quality. Thousand plus what? ... So if it is not thousand plus part, it's just normal."

### Trim the Bushes

The central AM action plan was to harvest what the weekend watch list had produced.

> "Always keep trimming the bushes. That's the best of idea."

> "I sold my ARM. I took a quick profit because I said this ARM, that is good rate of profit for the kind of stress setup it was."

### Stop Discipline

After Intel stopped him out, Pradeep stressed respecting the stop and walking away.

> "If a girl says no to you, do you go on a second date or a third date? If it'd had to go, it would have gone by now. ... Once you got stopped out, there is nothing to do. Look for the next setup."

### Do the Guide

Pradeep repeatedly returned to the low guide-completion rate in the room.

> "First, do the guide. By first, I think everybody should do the guide. Only 37% of the people do guide, and everybody who's profitable has done guide multiple times, so that tells you everything."

> "Can you imagine 37% only go through the guide? It's like a one type, and the remaining are so smart."

## PM Meeting (3 PM)

### Institutions Are Distributing Semiconductors

Pradeep's main PM observation: every time semis tried to break out in recent months, sellers appeared.

> "The institutions are using liquidity, the breakout liquidity, to upload their position. And they have done this repeatedly in the last couple of months. Every time these stocks try to break out, it's the same thing which has happening."

He pointed to repeated breakout-and-failure patterns in SOXX, Intel, MU, and SNDK.

> "Every time it tries to break out, there is a seller. Here, breakout and seller. Three times. Third time."

This is important contextual information: the sector has no follow-through because institutions are selling into strength.

### Nothing Actionable in the Trade Factory

Pradeep ran EP 9M, DEP, delayed-reaction EP, anticipation, and reversal bullish. The result:

> "I already went through everything, and I didn't see anything which is like actionable. ... There is just no qualified setup which matches anything which looks like a good setup."

> "When you have to struggle hard to find one good thing to buy, that means the market is telling you something."

### Reversal Bullish: Popularity Filter

He again drilled the social-arbitrage logic of reversal bullish. Today the examples were weak.

> "Who's going to buy it? Show me one person on Twitter who's excited, who runs a newsletter service or a Discord. ... They're talking about Target, and they are dying to get into Target tomorrow?"

> "Good setup is not enough for reversal bullish to work most of the time. You need a buzz. You need a line of buyers who are waiting for somebody to tell them, hey, this is a reversal bullish."

A good reversal bullish looks obvious:

> "You want to see clean, like, which looks like an egg — completely like you don't need to ask anybody that is it a reversal bullish kind of a thing. Then it works. Otherwise it doesn't work."

## Key Takeaways

1. **Third-day swings are dangerous** — The Friday–Monday–Tuesday rally hit the typical 3-day momentum-burst window. By Wednesday the risk/reward flips against fresh entries.
2. **20% study above 100 is a caution flag** — Pradeep uses breadth readings to calibrate aggressiveness; extended readings mean trim, not chase.
3. **Semiconductors are distributing** — Repeated breakout-and-failure in SOXX, Intel, MU, and SNDK shows institutions are selling into liquidity. That is actionable information: avoid the sector for new longs.
4. **There was nothing fresh to buy** — EP 9M, DEP, delayed-reaction EP, anticipation, and reversal bullish all came up empty. The market was saying "wait."
5. **Manage what you have** — ARM and Intel stopped out; APP and Snow were trimmed or managed. The weekend watch list produced the only winners.
6. **Stop discipline is non-negotiable** — Once a stop is hit, walk away. Don't negotiate with a stock that has rejected you.
7. **Reversal bullish requires buzz** — The setup only works on popular stocks that newsletters and Discords will recommend tomorrow. Target and obscure names fail the popularity filter.
8. **Biotech is event-driven chop** — Most biotech catalysts produce one-day gaps, then sideways. Non-gap breakouts are more tradable than gap-up EPs.
9. **DEP requires fund quality** — "Thousand plus" names only; otherwise it's just a normal breakout.
10. **Go through the guide** — The strongest correlate Pradeep sees between profitability and behavior is completing the guide multiple times. Process beats random scanning.
11. **When there is nothing to do, do nothing** — Swimming pool > forcing a trade.
