# Yesterday Was the Day — Slim Pickings After a Broad Breakout

The day after a massive breadth-driven breakout is **not** the day to buy — that is the session's radical claim. Pradeep drove this point home relentlessly: everything that had to break out broke out on June 11th, and if something didn't break out then, it wasn't worth breaking out. The result was a session of slim pickings, repeated warnings against chasing second-day moves, and Pradeep's emphatic return to his core directive: **trade setups, not stories**. The SpaceX IPO dominated Wall Street's attention, but Pradeep dismissed the entire space theme as "fugazi" and "fantasy" — the real money was in semiconductors and real catalysts from yesterday.

---

## AM Meeting (9:15 AM)

### Market Conditions at Open

- **Breadth**: Lack of FOMO at the open — a positive sign. No gap-up chasing.
- **Indices**: Subdued; the market appeared to be "taking a weekend off" ahead of the World Series and SpaceX IPO
- **Assessment**: Choppy environment. Pure continuation setups unlikely to work. Focus on EP 9M and DEP only.

> "The fact that the market is not gapping up and there is a lack of FOMO is actually good. That's what you want to see. You don't want to be seeing FOMO — that makes entries difficult."

> "Let this yesterday's bounce get digested. The previous bounces like this, you'll see that they don't really take off immediately because there is a lot of damage already done."

### Yesterday Was the Day

This was the dominant theme of the morning — Pradeep repeated it at least five times:

> "All the stocks which had to break out, they broke out yesterday. If something didn't break out yesterday, that also is really telling."

> "Yesterday was the day. Yesterday was the day."

> "Everything which had to break out broke out yesterday, and that's really the thing to remember."

> "You're not finding many setups today, and the reason why is because yesterday, whatever had to break out broke out. If something didn't break out yesterday, it wasn't really worth breaking out."

The implication was clear: if you missed the June 11th breakout day, **do not force entries on June 12th**. Wait for the next round of EP 9M setups to develop.

### Focus on EP 9M and DEP

> "EP 9M, DEP, and StockBee business is where we are going to be focusing on. A pure continuation setup may or may not work."

> "We focus on EP nine million, and just go from there. We are not trading indexes."

- **Primary focus**: EP 9M — stocks with 9M+ volume on the catalyst day
- **Secondary**: Reversal bullish
- **Avoid**: Pure continuation setups in this choppy environment
- **Key rule**: Buy setups that have tightness — "If you buy chop, you're gonna get chopped"

### Google Trade: Reversal Bullish in Action

Pradeep walked through his Google trade as a textbook example of a reversal bullish setup:

- **Entry**: ~$158 (bought on yesterday's reversal signal)
- **Exit 1**: Sold ~80% at $162.81 (pre-market / early gap-up)
- **Exit 2**: Sold remaining at $162.29
- **Stop**: $161 (remaining position)
- **Result**: ~83% of position sold at profit; "83% lighter"

> "The whole idea of a setup like reversal bullish, it's just a single strategy. You buy something at $158, and you sell next day morning if it gaps up."

### Biotechnology: The Anti-Continuation Category

> "Biotechnology stocks are the best category of stocks to avoid as a continuation setup. They are just one shot and done. They don't really have any follow-through."

> "Biotechnology, you buy them on the day of catalyst right at the open, and they make all their move in one day. And after that, nothing happens."

> "I've seen very little advantage to doing DEP on biotechnology based on thousands of examples which I have studied."

### No Edge in Index Inclusion, M&A, or Buyouts

Pradeep was emphatic that retail traders have **zero edge** in certain categories:

> "Inclusion in an index is not a catalyst which works very well because every single one of these big funds — they are not fools. They know their models. They know what criteria the index is going to use."

> "Goldman Sachs has a whole business of warehousing stocks likely to be added to the index and selling them to mutual funds for half a percent profit. You need to understand what is your size and find an edge that fits your size."

> "There is no edge in index addition, buyouts, merger, acquisition. You have zero edge in them. What you have is an edge on a stock which has some catalyst, EP 9M."

The Victoria's Secret model analogy returned:

> "If you're five feet four inches, average looking, permanently tanned, and you try and go for a Victoria's Secret model — do you have any edge? Unless you have billions of dollars in your bank account. Same way — there is no edge in index addition, buyouts, M&A."

### The Second-Day Rule

> "This is the second day of a swing. Buy on the first day of a swing. That's an unbreakable rule as far as I am concerned."

Pradeep emphasized this when asked about [[MU]] and other semiconductor names that broke out yesterday — they are **not** for entry today. Wait two to three more days for a potential DEP setup, or look for fresh EP 9M setups.

### The SpaceX IPO Frenzy

The SpaceX IPO dominated Wall Street's attention, but Pradeep was dismissive:

> "I don't care about SpaceX. I'm going to go and do my six packs."

> "I am sick and tired of hearing about SpaceX because every single person I know in the local community — from people who came to repair something in my house to the painter who painted my house — everybody is on that bandwagon."

He compared the frenzy to two other moments of mass-market obsession in his lifetime:
1. **SpaceX IPO** — random people tracking him down to ask about it
2. **GME** — the meme stock mania

> "When random people — literally people who had come to repair something in my house — are tracking me down and asking me about SpaceX IPO, that tells you something."

### Secular Bull Market Intact

> "This market has a couple more years ahead of it. We are in a secular bull market driven by liquidity, and this is not the end of a bear market."

- **IPO indicator**: Pradeep has an IPO indicator — 800+ IPOs in a year signals bearishness. Current levels are nowhere near that.
- The market is not in danger of a secular top.

### The "Click" — Mind Clarity Over Hard Work

> "It's not the amount of hard work which you put in. I've seen so many traders who put in a lot of hard work, but they don't make it. It's mind clarity — that moment where you get it."

> "Some people are quick learners. Some people can learn in weeks. Some people will take six months. Some people will never get it."

- Mastery of a setup is a **click moment** — not a function of hours logged
- Members who do well typically attend **2–3 boot camps** and rewrite concepts in their own words
- The "but" is always the problem — "the moment they start asking 'but,' my 'but' starts hurting"

### Digressions: London Etiquette, History of Medicine, Contraceptives

Pradeep's signature digressions enriched the session:

**London calling** — The "London" metaphor for the current market: subdued, restrained, no FOMO. Also, London etiquette lessons:
- Don't eat peas like a savage with your fork
- Don't eat a club sandwich with your fingers — cut it from the bottom with a fork

**History of medicine** — "The most fascinating subject in the world":
- The first surgeons were **French barbers**, not doctors
- The red-and-white barber pole = bloodletting (white = bandage, red = blood)
- The white coat doctors wear? Inherited from barbers who wanted to distinguish themselves from hair-cutters

**History of contraceptives** — Learned during his pharmacy studies:
- Romans used pig intestine and copper sticks
- Arabs discovered that inserting stones/sticks into female camels prevented pregnancy during caravan treks
- "That's how contraceptives were invented — when the camel used to take off, if it got pregnant, they had a problem"

### Space Economy Skepticism

Pradeep expanded on his space-stock skepticism in the PM session:

> "We can't get data centers running on Earth, and we are going to have space data centers? Something goes wrong in the plumbing — you're going to send a plumber from here to repair it? Come on. This is fantasy."

> "If we can't get a robotaxi out in ten years, and you're talking about things hanging in air somewhere. Colony on Mars? How are you going to provide organic, sustainably grown food to them?"

> "You go to stories when you have nothing else to do. You had real breakouts happening yesterday. [[SNDK]], [[MU]], [[Marvel]], [[DELL]] breaking out — which is a real catalyst. And here, this fugazi."

> "For foreseeable future, this is the top in the space economy."

### Trade Setups, Not Opinions

The PM session closed with Pradeep's core directive, stated more forcefully than ever:

> "I don't think. I trade setups. Don't think. Trade setups."

> "I trade setups. I don't trade individual stocks. I do setups."

> "If you cannot answer in one word, right away — the very fact that Okta and Snow didn't break out yesterday — there is just no reason to get involved in them."

> "Especially in this choppy market, have a reason to buy anything. Just don't go and do crazy things."

### Can You Articulate Your Reason?

> "If you're going to be buying something, you should be able to articulate clearly a reason why this should work, especially in a market which is going to be choppy."

This is the test: if you can't state in one sentence why a stock should go up based on your setup definition, **don't buy it**.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Yesterday was the day** — After a broad breakout day, the next day offers slim pickings. If you missed the entry, don't chase second-day moves. Wait for fresh EP 9M setups.
2. **Buy on the first day of a swing, not the second** — This is an unbreakable rule. Second-day entries in momentum stocks have poor risk/reward.
3. **EP 9M and DEP work independent of the market** — These are the go-to setups in choppy conditions. Pure continuation setups are unreliable.
4. **No edge in index inclusion, M&A, or buyouts** — Retail traders have zero edge competing against Goldman Sachs and institutional models. Find edges that fit your size — small-cap stocks below $1B market cap.
5. **Biotechnology is anti-continuation** — Buy on catalyst day at the open, sell same day. No DEP, no follow-through. Based on thousands of examples studied.
6. **If something didn't break out when everything else did, that's information** — Snow and Okta failing to break out on June 11th is the market telling you the catalyst is priced in or the stock lacks conviction.
7. **Space stocks are "fugazi"** — Story stocks driven by SpaceX IPO hype, not real catalysts. The space economy narrative is fantasy at current technology levels. Real money is in semiconductors with real catalysts.
8. **Lack of FOMO at the open is good** — A subdued, no-gap open is preferable to a FOMO-driven gap-up that forces chasing.
9. **Mind clarity over hard work** — Trading mastery is a "click" moment, not hours logged. Some get it in weeks; some never get it. Rewriting concepts in your own words accelerates the click.
10. **Can you articulate your reason?** — If you can't state in one word why a stock should work based on your setup definition, don't buy it. Especially in a choppy market.
