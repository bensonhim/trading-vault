# Context Is Everything: 90% of 20% Moves Start with 4% Breakouts

> **90% of stocks in the 20% study started with a 4% breakout.** If you buy a 4% breakout at the start of a swing — with no 4% breakout in the prior 2-3 days — context is good enough. Pradeep shorted ELF for near-zero risk (entered $163.02, $1 stop = 0.6% risk) and closed 80% of the position at $158. Bought Reddit and Axon as anticipation setups near market close. **Context is everything** — same setup in wrong context = loss. Context determines probability. Market up 8-9 days; select breakouts still working but stalling likely. Morning meetings may be cancelled; switching to one 3 PM meeting daily.

Market bounced V-shaped, almost recovered losses. Question: will it stall and pull back or continue? Select breakouts still working (20% study at 61 stocks). But 8-9 days up = digestion/choppy period likely.

### Key Teaching: 90% of 20% Moves Start with 4% Breakouts

> "Out of these, 7 out of 61 didn't start with 4% breakout. 90% started with 4% breakout."

Studied the 20% list methodically: PSNL, CVGO, Calamar, SkyWater, Geo, Victoria's Secret — nearly all started with 4% breakout. The few that didn't had volume conditions unmet. **If you focus on 4% breakout at the start of a swing**, with context (no 4% breakout in prior 2-3 days), you can capture most 20%+ moves.

### Key Teaching: Context Determines Whether a Breakout Works

> "The qualifying criteria determines whether the breakout is likely to work or not."

Same pattern, different context = different outcome. Examples:
- 4% breakout at start of swing with no prior expansion = good context, likely to work
- 4% breakout on day 4-5 of an ongoing move = extended, likely to fail
- Beginner traders are like moths attracted to light — they buy stocks already up 3-4 days

### Key Teaching: Victoria's Secret as Reversal Bearish Example

Showed VSCO as a reversal bearish example — stock up 4 days, 14%, showing clear exhaustion. This was the context for shorting. Context: stock exhausted, reversal bearish scan, near-zero-risk entry possible.

## 10 AM Meeting

### Trade Factory: Very Few Actionable Setups

Only 16-17 bullish setups early morning = context signal that most things aren't breaking out. Not ideal day for breakout side.

### Key Teaching: "What Are Your Thoughts?" = No Setup (Reinforced)

Same theme as yesterday. If you need to ask for thoughts, you don't have a setup.

### Key Teaching: Anticipation Is Sophisticated

> "Anticipation is not a setup for everybody. It's more for people who have had some success, thought through, and know what they are doing."

Anticipation = buying before breakout. Requires deep understanding. Like Japanese tea ceremony vs British tea — sophisticated, deliberate, requires practice.

### Key Teaching: Stops Must Be Logical

- Half-day stops only work if entry is excellent. Start with low-of-day stops.
- For anticipation: <1% stop, ideally <0.5%. Eli Lilly would have needed only 0.03% stop.
- $1 stop on a $960 stock makes no sense. Need $10+ stop minimum.

### Key Teaching: Range Expansion Must Be Qualified

Every breakout is not a buy. Qualifying criteria + context = probability of working. Buying on day 3+ of a move = extended context = high stop-out risk.

### Key Teaching: Nobody Paid Attention to This Morning's Lesson

> "I showed you Victoria's Secret as a reversal bearish example this morning. Not one person was paying attention."

Same setup Pradeep traded for free money (ELF short). Nobody remembered it when asked.

### Key Teaching: Shorts Need More Context

Range expansions on long side have better follow-through than short side. 65 stocks bullish vs 7 bearish in 20% study. Shorts need: catalyst, close stop, end-of-day entry preferred.

5. Each layer of context increases probability

### Key Teaching: Stacking Probabilities for Small Accounts

> "If you have a smaller account, you have to work harder. People with large accounts can put 5,000 shares and walk away with $25K."

Small accounts = need highest probability setups. Can't afford random trades. Must add context layers:
- Pattern (range expansion) → Catalyst (earnings/news) → Fundamentals (bucket 0/1 vs bucket 3) → Sector confirmation → Technical levels (support/resistance)
- Each layer stacks probability higher

### Key Teaching: ADR Is Backward-Looking

> "ADR is backward-looking. The best setups are neglected, low ADR stocks that suddenly have a high range expansion."

Don't filter for high ADR. Start-of-swing stocks often have low ADR before the breakout. ADR takes care of itself after the move works.

### Key Teaching: Most Big Money Was Made on Breakouts

> "Tell me one USIC champion who became a champion not trading breakouts."

Breakouts work. 90% of USIC champions trade breakouts. People say breakouts don't work, but that's a setup/process/trader problem, not a method problem.

### Key Teaching: Anticipation Entry Logic (Axon & Reddit)

Bought Axon and Reddit near market close as anticipation setups:
- Axon: perfect setup, high-priced, IBD 50 watchlist candidate → others will front-run tomorrow
- Reddit: reversal bullish, found in morning scan, entered near close
- Logic: if newsletter writers put Axon on watchlists tonight, it gaps up tomorrow → sell to willing buyers

> "Always try to understand what other traders are doing. That's a superpower."

### Key Teaching: Front-Running Popular Patterns

When IBD made cup-with-handle popular, sophisticated traders bought in the cup and sold when IBD readers bought the breakout. Anything popular gets front-run. Algorithms now front-run known patterns.

### Key Teaching: Commodity Futures — Not Recommended for Small Accounts

Small account → go to commodities for no PDT? Wrong logic. Commodities have few liquid contracts, limited trades per year, and extreme leverage risk. Better to trade stocks with high-conviction setups.
