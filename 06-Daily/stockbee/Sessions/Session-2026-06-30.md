# Process Beats Markets — The Trade Factory, Social Arbitrage, and Why Nothing Was Worth Buying on June 30

Pradeep opened June 30 still managing the weekend watch-list breakouts — ARM, APP, Dell, Intel, AMD, Google — and repeatedly told the room to move stops and take the free money. But by the PM session the verdict was categorical: there was almost nothing actionable. Every breakout worth taking had already happened on Friday or Monday; anyone looking for fresh setups on Tuesday was late, desperate, or both. The day became a clinic in the "Trade Factory" framework (EP/DEP/EP 9M/sugar babies for home runs; momentum burst for singles; anticipation and reversal bullish after 3 PM), the importance of process over random scanning, and the social-arbitrage logic behind reversal bullish: you are not trading a chart pattern, you are front-running the newsletters and Discord crowds that will recommend the same popular names to their followers the next morning.

---

## AM Meeting (9:15 AM)

### Context: A Stubborn Range, Tight Stops, and the Weekend Watch List

Pradeep's opening call: the market is range-bound and choppy. Friday's buying may have been real, or it may have been index rebalancing. Monday gave some follow-through. Today is the test.

> "If some of these breakouts fade or struggle to get traction, that will be a sign of stubborn range. And this is why I put a very tight stops on most of the things because, if this doesn't follow through today, that tells you that this is just a range bound action."

He was already positioned from the weekend watch list: APP, ARM, Dell, Intel, AMD, Google. The plan was not to chase new names but to manage those positions, move stops aggressively, and let winners run.

> "Now all that you have to do is to make sure that you move your stop to breakeven or thing like that, and let it run if it runs in your favor."

### The Trade Factory: Two Conveyor Belts

Pradeep laid out the daily process in detail:

| Conveyor Belt | Purpose | Typical Setups | Stop Style |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Home runs / mini home runs** | Move account 10–100% in one trade | EP, EP 9M, DEP, sugar babies | Wider stop, often low of day, sometimes 8–10% |
| **Singles** | Move account 0.5–5% per trade | Momentum burst, 3–5 day swings | Tight stop, often <5% |

> "Home runs and mini home runs: EP, EP 9M, DEP, sugar babies. Singles: momentum burst. And a combination of the two is what I trade based on market filter. In addition to this, complementary to momentum burst is anticipation setup and reversal bullish."

### EP 9M Is a Method, Not a Buy Signal

A recurring AM theme: the scan is not the setup.

> "Every stock which shows up is not worth getting into. It is just ... yeah. It is more to it than just running a scan. Context qualifier."

For EP 9M the qualifier is the cat/dog/liquid-lava classification, and the context is whether there is a clear reason for the stock to go up.

> "If you apply any of that, is there any reason to get excited about Nokia? Except for if you're a Swedish guy, then you might get excited about this thing."

### Stop Discipline and Taking the Free Money

Pradeep peeled 80% off ARM and left 20% as a runner with a trailing stop. He explained that once a reversal-bullish move has paid, the goal is to protect the gain, not maximize it.

> "I took my 80% off. 20% is with a stop. If it gives you more money, that's welcome. If not, not a problem."

> "Stop is a function of what the setup is. There is no universal answer to where to put a stop."

### On AI Note-Taking and Process Laziness

A pointed AM rant against members who spend time making AI-generated visuals but cannot explain a DEP or SOS:

> "There are people here who spend so much time taking notes using AI and creating some fancy pictures and images. And they have a shit understanding of what a DEP is, what's SOS is. ... That AI is like something last thing you should be focusing on if you can't even trade."

And later:

> "Cancel all your AI subscription and write it with hand, then you'll understand."

### Specialization Advice

Pradeep told newer traders to master one setup, not many.

> "Anybody who's new here or struggling, specialize in one setup. It takes a lot of time to master one setup. ... Most of the successful initial success I have seen in recent years: they are wearing only one setup, DEP on fund quality names."

### The Board Outside His Office

Pradeep pointed to a sign on his office door:

> "There is a board outside my office. What does it say? ... 'Process trumps markets.' And this I wrote down."

## PM Meeting (3 PM)

### Nothing Worth Buying

By 3 PM the conclusion was clear:

> "I already went through everything, and I didn't see anything which is worth... everything happened on Friday or Monday. If you missed that, tough luck."

> "There is just no qualified setup which matches anything which looks like a good setup. ... When you have to struggle hard to find one good thing to buy, that means the market is telling you something."

The best setups had already broken out. Tuesday was the third day of the swing — historically the wrong entry point for a momentum burst.

> "It's probably third day of most of those momentum bursts, and that is it. ... Why can't we accept that there is no good setup? It's a fear of missing out."

### The Two Ways to Make Money

Pradeep reprised the trade-factory framing:

| Way | Target | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Home runs / mini home runs** | 10–100% account move | EP, EP 9M, DEP, sugar babies |
| **Singles** | 0.5–5% account move | Momentum burst, 3–5 day swing |

> "Singles will take your account up half a percent overall in one trade to up to 5% in one trade."

### EP 9M: Cat, Dog, Liquid Lava

The qualifier for EP 9M must be applied every time:

> "Just because the stock shows up in EP 9M is not a buy. You have to qualify it. Qualification is whether it is a cat, whether it is a dog, whether it is a liquid lava."

### Reversal Bullish Is Social Arbitrage

The PM's most important lesson: reversal bullish is not about the chart pattern, it is about front-running popularity.

> "Whenever you're doing a reversal bullish, it is not the chart pattern. It is the context and qualifier which is important. ... How many people are going to be excited about Procter and Gamble, and how many newsletters are going to put Procter and Gamble as their pick tonight? ... Zero. It has to be hot. Hot. Hot."

> "I recommended App. App is a very hot stock. It has earnings. It has sales. It's AI. I can tell you 300 newsletters which will be excited about this next day."

> "You are selling to somebody who buys next day. ... It's a Ponzi scheme. ... You are front-running the next day buyers."

The one-question filter:

> "Ask yourself before buying any stock: who are the fools who are going to buy it tomorrow? You have to have enough of them."

### Position Sizing: Size Matters

Pradeep emphasized that small positions do not move accounts.

> "Position sizing depends on your risk. If your risk is tiny, you put 25 to 30%. ... The whole idea of position sizing is to take enough to make a difference. If you're just putting small, tiny positions, you're not going to make much money."

But he also warned that high size is for personal money, not for recommendations:

> "I don't want to do anything where I cannot put 20 to 25% of my account. ... That is my personal restaurant. Will I recommend that? No."

### No Anticipation, No Bottom-Fishing

Pradeep said this market is not right for anticipation setups and that bottom-bouncing will get you hurt.

> "This is not a market where I'm willing to do anticipation. ... Picking bottoms only works under right condition. All those people who are picking bottoms in Microsoft, how well did that work out?"

### Weekend Work Paid Off

Despite the lack of new setups, the weekend preparation delivered:

> "Whatever work I did on weekend paid off. ... Dell, Intel, AMD, Google, SPCX, NVIS, FDMT — those were the ones. If you did anything with it on Monday, you should be very happy."

### Reading the Timeline and Learning from Others

Pradeep highlighted a community member's morning post as a "lesson of a lifetime" on how to analyze news catalysts:

> "That guy gave you a lesson of your lifetime as to how to analyze news catalyst. ... He's giving it to you on a platter. ... People are not even using that information."

This reinforced the broader point: valuable knowledge is in the timeline and the process posts, not in ticker-only posts.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Process trumps markets** — Pradeep's office sign is the core philosophy. The same sequence of scans, the same classification, the same stop rules, executed daily, is what produces edge.
2. **The trade factory has two conveyor belts** — Home-run setups (EP, EP 9M, DEP, sugar babies) and singles (momentum burst). Complementary late-day setups are anticipation and reversal bullish.
3. **Scan ≠ setup** — EP 9M only tells you a stock has volume. You must classify it (cat, dog, liquid lava) and confirm it has a clear reason to go up.
4. **Context and qualifier are non-negotiable** — SOS, reversal bullish, continuation, and EP all require specific conditions. Running a scan without context is "running naked."
5. **Tuesday was the third day of the swing** — Most momentum bursts last 3–5 days. Entering on day three is historically poor timing; the easy money was Friday and Monday.
6. **Reversal bullish is social arbitrage** — You are front-running the newsletter/Twitter/Discord crowd. Buy names that will be popular tonight, not obscure charts.
7. **Position sizing must be meaningful** — To move an account, you need size on conviction. But scale to your own risk and whether you are trading your own money.
8. **Sell into strength and move stops** — Once a reversal-bullish or continuation move pays, peel 80%, move stop to breakeven or better, and let 20% run.
9. **Weekend preparation creates calm execution** — The watch list from Sunday night (Dell, Intel, AMD, Google, Marvel) produced the only actionable ideas.
10. **Specialize** — Newer traders should master one setup, ideally DEP on fund-quality names, before expanding.
11. **No setup? Do nothing** — Desperately trying to find something to buy is FOMO. Sometimes the correct action is to reduce exposure and wait.
12. **Don't outsource thinking to AI** — Hand-written notes and deep study of basics (DEP, SOS, continuation) beat fancy AI-generated visuals if you cannot explain the setup.
