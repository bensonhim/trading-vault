# Pullback Day 2 — Oversold Bounces Win, Extended Stocks Get Hit, 80/20 Saves Profits

Day 2 of the new year and Pradeep's central teaching crystallized: when the market bounces, it's the beaten-down stocks that make the biggest moves — not the ones near 52-week highs. This isn't opinion; it's empirical fact that plays out every cycle. Meanwhile, extended stocks like JPMorgan and Mara were getting hit, and the 80/20 exit method proved its worth again on Cigna and ABBV trades. Very few new actionable setups — this was a day to be selective and nimble.

---

## AM Meeting (9 AM & 11 AM)

### The Oversold Bounce Principle

> "Stocks which are down 60%, 70%, 80%, 90% are the ones which make the biggest move. And stocks which are holding up near high don't even make a move. And this happens every single fucking time."

> "The first leg of the move was 80%. The first leg is the most explosive leg in many cases."

Pradeep's key teaching: after a market bottom, the stocks that rally hardest are the most oversold — not the relative-strength leaders near 52-week highs. This contradicts conventional CAN SLIM wisdom, but the data is unambiguous. The first 6–10 weeks of a bull market favor bottom-fishing setups; consolidation breakouts only start working after that.

## PM Meeting (2 PM)

### Selectivity Is Everything in Pullback

> "If my situational awareness is we are in pullback mode, I'm more cautious and more selective. I'm not going to take any and every setup here."

> "Just because setups are showing up in the scan doesn't mean you go all out."

The PM scan yielded almost nothing actionable. This was by design — pullback mode demands extreme selectivity.

### 80/20 Deep Dive

> "When I do eighty twenty, what am I doing? I'm oversizing the position. When I took out 80%, I'm left with the original swing position I would have put in."

This was the key teaching of the session. 80/20 is not just an exit method — it's a position-sizing strategy:

1. **Oversize the entry** — JPMorgan: 5,000 shares in a $1M account ≈ 85% of account
2. **Take 80% off quickly** — Leaves a normal-sized swing position
3. **The 80% profit is your real trade** — The remaining 20% is a free ride
4. **Risk is tiny** — JPMorgan: 27¢ stop = 0.13% of account risk
5. **ABBV example** — $156 × 5,000 shares, 80% off = reasonable exposure remaining

> "You don't need balls to do FFFM. You need skills."

> "Taking 80% off keeps you in the trade with proper sizing."

### Cigna: Why 80/20 Matters

Cigna looked like a good breakout yesterday. Today it gave up all gains. But because Pradeep took 80% off early, the remaining 20% stop-out was trivial. Without 80/20, this would have been a losing trade.

## Key Takeaways

1. **Oversold stocks make the biggest moves** — Not stocks near 52-week highs; this is empirically true every cycle
2. **First phase = bottoming setups, not breakouts** — Consolidation breakouts only work after 6–10 weeks of bull market
3. **Extended stocks are getting hit** — JPMorgan, Mara, and other extended names were the primary short candidates
4. **80/20 is a position-sizing strategy, not just an exit** — Oversize entry, take 80% quickly, ride the remaining 20% for free
5. **Day 3 of sell-off = diminishing short opportunities** — Easy entries on shorts were days ago; by day 3, not much new to do
6. **Selectivity over activity in pullback** — "Nothing I see here either in short side, long side, or anticipation which I'm excited about"
7. **Cigna proved 80/20 saves profits** — Good breakout that gave up all gains; 80/20 made it profitable anyway
8. **FFFM requires skill, not courage** — Finding free money situations requires setups, sizing, and discipline
9. **Situational awareness is non-negotiable** — Pradeep frustrated with attendees wanting to buy in a pullback after extensive explanation
10. **Breakouts fail in pullback** — Most breakout attempts were falling; don't force longs in this environment
11. **Bull market undergoing correction, not a bear market** — Anticipation shorts not warranted; this is still a bull market
12. **Linearity as proxy for buying pressure** — Whether long or short, look for the most linear setups
