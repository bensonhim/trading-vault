# Range-Bound Discipline — FOMO on Micron, the DEP Advantage on Mega-Caps, and the Day Nothing Worked

Pradeep opened Thursday with a clear warning: the market is range-bound, Micron's blowout earnings were no surprise (everybody and their mother-in-law knew), and chasing the gap is exactly what the trade factory is designed to prevent — a 1.2-trillion-dollar cap like [[MU]] will almost certainly set up as a [[DEP]] (Delayed Reaction EP) with lower risk. By the PM session, the verdict was decisive: nothing followed through, [[BB]] was already up 209%, [[AAPL]] had no volume, the best action was to sell into strength on [[BLZ]] and sit on hands. The session became a masterclass in the surprise-and-neglect requirement, why gap-ups on widely-expected news are traps, and why the end goal is printing money — not being right about a stock's three-year prospects.

---

## AM Meeting (9:15 AM)

### Situational Awareness: Range-Bound, FOMO Is the Enemy

> "We are in a range bound kind of situation which has been established. If it does happen, you will see that lot of these stocks, which are probably setting up nicely, will be the one which will break out also."

The market remains range-bound. The trade factory — setups, scans, rules, context, qualifiers, defined entry/exit parameters — is the antidote to emotional reaction. Pradeep drew the emergency-room analogy: when a patient is freaking out, the doctor does not freak out; the doctor follows methodology.

> "You go to an emergency room, and you have some heart problem or something, and you are freaking out. And if the doctor also starts freaking out, oh my god, he's got a heart attack, what do I do — if he starts doing that, what is going to happen? You don't do that. You're a professional."

The FOMO trigger of the day: random non-traders on Instagram and Twitter asking about [[MU]] after its blowout earnings. Pradeep's advice to those who cannot control themselves:

> "If you cannot avoid a FOMO, buy 10 shares of MU, buy 20 shares of MU. That's what I told all the people who are emailing me. But in the long run, the antidote to FOMO is having a setup and process and following it."

### The DEP Advantage on Mega-Cap Earnings

A $1.2T stock like [[MU]] is highly likely to set up as a delayed reaction EP rather than a chaseable gap:

> "This size stock, the probability — 95% probability, or I would say probably 97% probability — is that it will create a delayed reaction EP with a low risk. You will be able to still make an entry. But you have to trust."

Gap-ups are frequently emotional overreactions. [[NVDA]] had a very good earnings and "can do the same thing what it did last time, and it might be lower risk than chasing a gap." Gaps can become KLPs (gap that fills and fails). [[AVGO]] was the recent cautionary tale — massive buildup, gapped up, then "just booked."

> "Gaps can be traps, and you never know. The gap can end up becoming a KLP also."

### Surprise and Neglect: Why Earnings Growth Alone Doesn't Move Stocks

A member named Nitin asked about buying [[MU]] in premarket on 4x revenue/EPS. Pradeep dismantled the logic:

> "Stock can have 195% growth, but it is not that which drives the stock. If it was happening for the first time and the market didn't expect, then the market will care about it. When it has widely expected and everybody and their mother-in-law knows about it, it is not a surprise. Market moves based on surprise. Market doesn't move based on earnings growth or sales growth."

The qualifier checklist for an EP:
1. **Momentum burst** — was there a prior leg?
2. **Neglect** — was the stock ignored before the catalyst?
3. **Surprise** — surprise happens only where there is neglect
4. **Setup** — does it meet entry conditions?

> "Where was the neglect? Tell me. That's the most important. If you don't really want to use that to make money, you're just going to buy a random thing."

### Google: Narrative Changing Again

> "Today, Google is going to be the play, be it whatever direction. It's going to go hard."

The narrative on [[GOOG]] is shifting — lost two key people, too bureaucratic, Sundar is not a risk taker. Pradeep's characterisation:

> "Sundar is like a typical middle class guy who looks at right side of the road, left side of the road, sees there is no car coming, and then he goes quickly. He's not the kind of guy who's got audacity."

But if [[GOOG]] is going to go up, it will show up in EP 9M scans — react then, don't predict. The Anthropic founder naming [[Tempus AI]] and [[LLY]] (Eli Lilly) as the two AI beneficiaries was noted as a recurring narrative catalyst.

### Distribution, Not Buying

> "I don't get a good feeling here. Do you get a good feeling here? This is a classic — what is it called? Distribution. No one's buying today."

On good news, stocks are fading instead of breaking out. The market is using positive catalysts to offload inventory on retail buyers:

> "Instead of that news getting bought, the market is just taking that news to offload inventory on retail fools."

### The "Degenerate Gambler" Philosophy

> "We are degenerate gamblers who just focus on three to five day moves. I have no idea what is going to happen to this stock three years down the line. All that I see is EP 9M, as long as there is a catalyst. I just look at the setup and bought it. I don't even know whether it will work or not."

> "We are not PhD. We just know how to make money. That's it."

People outside trading think StockBee members have some secret. They don't. The trade factory is the secret — and it is repeatable.

### The Vedanta Analogy: Dismiss the Thought

A married member said he admires beautiful women "in the mind" but dismisses the thought. Pradeep connected this directly to trading discipline:

> "Here the thought came, let's buy a BlackBerry, but then you gently dismiss it. That market condition is not there. That's it. Very simple. You learn to observe your thoughts and gently dismiss. Don't react to it. This is pure Vedanta."

### Changing Belief: The Hungarian Doctor Story

Pradeep recounted the story of Ignaz Semmelweis, who proved doctors washing hands would save lives — but the medical establishment believed upper-class hands were inherently clean, so they committed him to an asylum where he was beaten to death. It took 20–30 years for the belief to change.

> "Beliefs are very hardcoded. In order to change belief — like this whole thing, stocks moving three to five days, momentum burst — now this is a belief. You have to go and see for yourself, and you see hundreds of examples, then your belief will change."

### Risk Discipline

> "The worst thing in trading is FOMO. When there is no setup and you FOMO, you are one trade away from disaster."

> "I'm keeping my wallet safe. Zipped."

> "I don't see setups. If there is no setup, you don't do anything."

## Key Takeaways

1. **Surprise requires neglect** — Earnings growth alone does not move stocks. [[MU]] grew 195% but everybody knew it was coming. Market moves on surprise, and surprise only exists where there was prior neglect. No neglect = no edge.
2. **Mega-caps favour DEP over gap-chasing** — A $1.2T stock like [[MU]] has ~97% probability of setting up as a delayed reaction EP with lower risk than chasing the premarket gap. Trust the process; wait for the DEP.
3. **Gaps can be traps (KLP)** — [[NVDA]], [[AVGO]] are cautionary tales of gap-ups that faded. A gap is frequently an emotional overreaction. Let the stock tell you via EP 9M.
4. **Context overrides setup** — Even valid EP 9M setups are not following through in this range-bound market. The trade factory requires situational awareness first, qualifier second, catalyst third. Skip any step and you're gambling.
5. **Protect profits ruthlessly** — [[BLZ]] went ~25% in 1–2 days. Pradeep sold 80%, kept 20%. When an EP 9M goes crazy, trim hard. Don't let a winner round-trip into a loser.
6. **Distribution on good news is a warning** — Stocks fading on positive catalysts (SNDK, MU after earnings) means institutions are using the news to offload on retail. This is classic distribution.
7. **The end goal is printing money** — Not being right about a stock's three-year outlook. Not having a PhD in a company. "We are degenerate gamblers who focus on three to five day moves." The trade factory is the only secret.
8. **Dismiss the thought, don't suppress it** — The Vedanta principle: when the thought "buy BB" arises, observe it and gently dismiss. Don't react. Market conditions are the filter.
9. **Beliefs change through evidence, not argument** — The Semmelweis story: new information changes belief, but only after you see hundreds of examples. Momentum burst is a belief you must verify for yourself.
10. **The best trade on June 25 was sitting out** — No compelling longs, no compelling shorts, no follow-through. Pradeep's wallet stayed zipped. The discipline of inaction in a range-bound market is the hardest and most profitable skill.
