# Data vs Information — Why Funds Sell, Procedural Memory, and the Tinder Date Breadth Thrust

July 23 was a teaching day. The market was the same range-bound chop, but Pradeep used it to deliver a masterclass on the difference between data and information, how institutional fund flows create market trends, and why procedural memory is the goal of every trader. The AM session was a deep dive into market breadth as a framework — how Fidelity ($7T), Vanguard ($11.9T) and other mega-funds leave tracks when they buy or sell, and how the market monitor is just measuring those tracks. The PM session was short (pool time) — nothing actionable in any scan, breakouts still squatting, shorts working better than longs, and a memorable lesson on reversal bullish: find 10-14 genuine trades per year, not every candle that looks like a pattern.

---

## AM Meeting (9:15 AM)

### The Framework: Funds Create Trends, We Participate

Pradeep opened with the big picture: the April-May rally was a historical move (bigger than a typical annual move in just 2.2 months), driven by Fidelity ($7T), Vanguard ($11.9T), and other mega-funds buying baskets of stocks. Now they're selling.

> "When continuous buying comes into the market, everything works. Our job is to participate when they are aggressive, and not be aggressive when they are not aggressive."

> "Funds work based on valuation. They operate on a two-quarter horizon. They have a different way of looking at it."

> "When they buy, they leave tracks. Like an 18-wheeler on your grass — you'll see the tracks."

### Data vs Information

> "This is data. What is the information? The deduction from this is that breakouts are unlikely to work."

> "Data is not information. Information is when you interpret data in a context and it becomes actionable."

> "If you get it right 90% of the time, that's more than enough in the market."

### 20% Study Is a Lagging Indicator

> "Before 20% study goes up, there'll be a buying binge. Buying binge, then 20% study. So 20% study is going to be a lagging indicator."

### Current Market: Range-Bound, Funds Distributing

> "In the last twelve days, the funds are selling. If the funds are selling, they are distributing."

> "Breakouts still happen. One day wonders happen. But they don't follow through. Follow-through happens when funds come in day after day after day."

> "This is a range-bound environment which entices you to buy setups because a breakout still looks like a breakout in a bad market. But it doesn't follow through — it goes into a long squat."

> "Squats are good if you do them in the gym, but squats are not good if it happens to your stocks."

### What to Do: SIPs or Day Trading

> "If breakouts are not working, then what do you do? You expand your universe to look at stocks in play, or something which has a catalyst relevant to this particular time."

> "If you cannot control that, you'll still trade, and then you'll blame momentum doesn't work, this doesn't work, this is a fraud, that is a fraud."

### Darvas Box: People Misapply It

> "Darvas never bought a stock which was down 95% from all-time high. His Starbucks box happened near high. It had to be within 15% of all-time high. I see people putting Darvas box on a stock which is down 99%."

> "He had eight conditions. People forget the seven things and just focus on the box."

### NVDA Long Entry — Procedural Memory in Action

Pradeep explained his NVDA long entry using a memorable metaphor:

> "I was watching the trailer of Odyssey. There is a goddess who gives you advice. That goddess told me: when you come back from gym, always run an EP 9M scan. Use the framework cat, dog, liquid lava. Look for bucket zero, bucket one, or 5000+ fund ownership. If it's start of swing, buy it."

> "I came from the gym. I saw it. I bought it. It took me 0.2 seconds to make the decision. Why? Because it's based on procedural memory."

> "NVDA is a bucket one stock, owned by 5,000+ funds, has very good earnings. Probability of 3-4 days of movement."

### Google Short Entry — Same Process

> "I was eating an ice cream. I ran an EP 9M scan. I saw a 5,000-fund ownership stock with a news catalyst. If a 5,000-fund stock shows EP 9M to the downside, it means an institution is selling."

### Deep Buying After Gap Down = Information

> "Deep buying happens because people who sold short, they cover. Their first reaction is to buy back."

> "Stocks gap down, so they make new highs intraday. This is information — deep buyers came in after the gap down."

## Key Takeaways

1. **Data is not information** — Market monitor numbers are raw data. Information is the deduction: "breakouts unlikely to work." Actionable insight comes from interpreting data in context.
2. **Funds create trends** — Fidelity ($7T), Vanguard ($11.9T) buy baskets, not individual stocks. We participate when they're aggressive, step back when they're selling.
3. **20% study is a lagging indicator** — Buying binge happens first, then 20% study goes up. Don't wait for 20% study to confirm — it's already late.
4. **Funds are distributing** — Last 12 days: selling pressure visible in market monitor. Breakouts happen but don't follow through because funds aren't buying day after day.
5. **Squats are bad** — Stocks that break out then sit sideways ("squat") in a range-bound market are the norm. Good for gym, bad for portfolio.
6. **Procedural memory is the goal** — Pradeep bought NVDA in 0.2 seconds. Not analysis — internalized process. Cat/dog/liquid lava + bucket tier + fund ownership → automatic decision.
7. **Shorts working better than longs** — Both Google and PLTR shorts had follow-through. Microsoft would have been better. Longs are squatting.
8. **Reversal bullish = 10-14 trades per year** — Not every candle. Find stocks with catalyst + popularity (APP example). Regional banks = half a percent. Not worth it.
9. **Anticipation = 25% win rate** — "Guaranteed chop in this market." People are enamored with anticipation but it's the worst setup here.
10. **EP 9M is the main character** — Reversal bullish and weak structure shot are cameos. Prioritize EP 9M → momentum burst → reversal bullish → weak structure shot.
11. **Research everything in EP 9M** — If you research a stock when it shows up, next time it sets up you don't need to research. Build the database.
12. **Max 7 party over** — Magnificent 7 going into debt, negative cash flows, no more buybacks. "Data centers will be pickleball courts in 5 years."
