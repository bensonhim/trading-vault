# Thursday Was the Day — Protect Profits, Not Chase Gaps

The market gapped up on Iran-ceasefire news — the third day of a move — and Pradeep's instant reaction was to sell, not buy. "Today is pay time," he declared, warning that every stock that gapped up was fading by mid-morning. The session became a masterclass in **situational awareness over pattern-chasing**: when the market has run three days and the catalyst was known three days ago, your job is to protect open profits, not chase gaps. Both AM and PM meetings yielded essentially zero actionable setups — "There is no setup, end of story." The only candidates were [[QBTS]] (a sugar baby with government catalyst), [[DASH]] (DoorDash, marginal breakout), and a token tracking position in [[SNOW]]. Pradeep devoted the bulk of both sessions to teaching the deeper principles: trade what is popular (not obscure), qualify every idea (like choosing a partner), and understand that **Thursday was the day** — everything that had to break out broke out on Thursday, and Monday was for selling.

## AM Meeting (9:15 AM)

### Situational Awareness: Third Day of a Move

The session opened with Pradeep's core philosophy — setups do not work in a vacuum. Momentum-based setups are "very, very vulnerable to market condition." When conditions favor you, everything works; when they don't, records fail.

The immediate context: the market gapped up on Iran news that was already known on Thursday. This was the **third day of the move**. The professional response was not excitement — it was profit protection.

> "Most likely, what is likely to happen is the people who bought on Thursday, what are they going to do? Bam, bam, bam. Thank you, madam. Goodbye."

> "Move started on Thursday. This is the third day of a move. So your number one priority should be to protect open profits."

> "Today is pay time."

### Everything Is Fading

Within the first 20 minutes, every stock that gapped up was fading:

> "Whatever opened with a gap, every single stock which I am watching here, which opened with a gap, is fading."

- [[DELL]] — gapped up $14-15, Pradeep sold 90% of his position into strength
- [[INTC]] — gapped then crapped
- [[SNOW]] / [[OKTA]] — "should have already moved, they are not moving"

> "This is where situational awareness comes in. Like, on a day like this, I don't want to be getting in early into anything because you're likely that you're going to be chasing things."

### "Whom Can I Sell To?" — The Popularity Principle

This was the dominant teaching of the AM session. Before buying any stock, ask yourself: **who else is going to buy after me?**

> "Every time you are tempted to buy, think — who can I sell this to? Who else is gonna buy this?"

> "If there is a breakout on Snow, are there a lot of people who are going to be excited about? The answer is yes."

> "If I buy something like Winnebago or Restoration Hardware — yeah, it might work. But tell me, how many people who have thousand-plus subscribers on Twitter are going to be excited about THO?"

**Popular stocks with broad appeal** (institutional quality, widely followed): [[SNOW]], [[OKTA]], [[MU]], [[DELL]], [[MRVL]], [[SNDK]]

**Obscure stocks nobody's watching** (avoid): [[THO]], Winnebago, Restoration Hardware, [[LYFT]], Microsoft (nobody's waiting to buy Microsoft at resistance)

> "Trade what is popular, not what is obscure."

> "If I bought Dell, I want to find somebody to sell to. If I bought Marvel, I want somebody else to come and buy after me."

### The Dating Analogy — Qualifying Like a Woman on a Dating App

> "Don't be like the men on dating sites. They swipe at every woman. A woman rarely swipes at a guy. Because women have qualifying criteria — he should be handsome, he should have this, he should not have a fish in his hand."

> "Learn to qualify. Not everything is a buy."

### The Catalyst Test

Pradeep walked through the COVID analogy to explain why catalyst-driven stocks move independently:

- [[MRNA]] (Moderna) — went up during COVID while everything else crashed

- [[TMO]] (Thermo Fisher) — "all the COVID vaccines are refrigerated, they're distributed refrigerated, so Thermo Fisher benefited"

> "Always when you're looking at EP 9,000,000 or EP, always think like that. You have to understand. You can't do it blindly. A scan is just to find the idea. After that, you qualify using your analytical skill."

## Key Takeaways

1. **Thursday was the day** — everything that had to break out broke out on Thursday (June 11). Monday (June 15) was for selling into strength, not buying.
2. **Protect profits on gap-ups** — when the market has moved three days and the catalyst was known three days ago, gaps are for selling, not buying. "Today is pay time."
3. **Trade what is popular** — before buying, ask "who else is going to buy after me?" Obscure stocks have no second buyer. [[SNOW]], [[OKTA]], [[MU]] have thousands of eyes on them; [[THO]], [[LYFT]], Restoration Hardware do not.
4. **Situational awareness over pattern** — pattern-based setups only work when the market is in continuous buying mode. In choppy, third-day markets, patterns fail.
5. **EP 9M is the king setup** — all the stocks that worked on Thursday were EP 9M. No EP 9M setups visible on Monday = no trades.
6. **Qualify like a woman on a dating app** — don't swipe right on everything. Have criteria. Catalyst? Volume? Institutional interest? If it fails three qualifiers, skip it.
7. **The Ponzi principle** — "We make money if somebody else buys after us." Be first, be early, be in the popular names.
8. **Duct tape your hands** — when there's nothing to do, doing nothing IS the trade. Pradeep went to the gym rather than sit and be tempted.
9. **Narrow rally = caution** — the rally is not broadening. Only Thursday's names are participating. Fresh breakouts are not appearing.
10. **Context and qualifiers matter** — reversal bullish requires selling exhaustion. A reversal bullish on [[MSFT]] three days into a move is not reversal bullish. Context invalidates the pattern.
