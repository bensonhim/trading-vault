*Question: fmp adjusted close? how can i get the unadjusted? seems like it affects me on calculation of indicator for market analysis*

**Yes**, FMP’s “adjusted close” (the `adjClose` field in the standard endpoint) adjusts historical prices for both **stock splits** and **dividends**. This smoothing can distort technical indicators in market analysis, which is why many traders and quants specifically want the raw **unadjusted** close (the actual traded closing price on that day, with no retroactive changes).

### Quick Breakdown of FMP Price Types

FMP offers several variants so you can choose the right one:

| Type | What It Adjusts For | Typical Field Name | Best For | Drawbacks for TA Indicators |
|------|---------------------|--------------------|----------|-----------------------------|
| **Fully unadjusted (raw)** | Nothing | `close` (or `adjClose` in newer endpoints) | Execution simulation, price-action patterns, actual historical levels | Discontinuities at splits |
| **Split-adjusted only** | Stock splits (not dividends) | `close` in standard `/full` endpoint | Most charting platforms, continuous price series | Still has small dividend gaps |
| **Fully adjusted** (`adjClose`) | Splits + dividends | `adjClose` | Total return calculations, long-term performance | Can alter indicator behavior around corporate actions |
| **Dividend-adjusted** | Dividends (splits handled separately) | Specific endpoint | Income-focused analysis | Less common for pure price TA |

In the **standard** historical endpoint (`/historical-price-eod/full` or the older `/v3/historical-price-full`), FMP explicitly states:
- `close` = adjusted **only for stock splits**
- `adjClose` = adjusted for **splits + dividends**

This is why your indicators are being affected — the adjustments create a smoother but sometimes artificial price history.

### How to Get the True Unadjusted Close from FMP

Use the dedicated **Unadjusted Stock Price API** (non-split-adjusted endpoint). This returns the actual traded prices without retroactive split adjustments (and without dividend adjustments).

**Endpoint:**
```
https://financialmodelingprep.com/stable/historical-price-eod/non-split-adjusted
```

**Example call (with date range):**
```
https://financialmodelingprep.com/stable/historical-price-eod/non-split-adjusted?symbol=AAPL&from=2020-01-01&to=2025-06-04&apikey=YOUR_API_KEY
```

**Supported parameters** (confirmed on docs):
- `symbol` (required)
- `from` (YYYY-MM-DD)
- `to` (YYYY-MM-DD)
- `apikey` (required)

The response contains the raw OHLCV data. Field names are typically `open`/`high`/`low`/`close`/`volume` (some responses or articles show `adj*` prefixes for consistency across endpoints — the values in this specific endpoint are the **unadjusted** ones).

You can also fetch the full history without date limits (subject to API plan limits, often capped at ~5,000 rows per call).

### Python Example to Fetch Unadjusted Data

```python
import requests
import pandas as pd

API_KEY = "YOUR_API_KEY_HERE"
symbol = "AAPL"

url = "https://financialmodelingprep.com/stable/historical-price-eod/non-split-adjusted"
params = {
    "symbol": symbol,
    "from": "2015-01-01",
    "to": "2025-06-04",
    "apikey": API_KEY
}

response = requests.get(url, params=params)
data = response.json()

# The historical data is usually under a key or directly as list
df = pd.DataFrame(data.get("historical", data))   # adjust key if needed
df['date'] = pd.to_datetime(df['date'])
df = df.sort_values('date')

print(df[[date, open, high, low, close, volume]].tail())
```

You now have the true unadjusted close in the `close` column.

### Why Adjusted Prices Affect Technical Indicators (and When It Matters)

This is the core issue you’re running into. Here’s a detailed breakdown:

**Problems created by adjusted data for TA:**
- **Splits** cause artificial price jumps/drops (e.g., a 4-for-1 split instantly quarters the price). Adjusted series back-adjusts everything, so moving averages, Bollinger Bands, channels, and support/resistance levels become continuous but no longer reflect what traders actually saw.
- Indicators like RSI, MACD, Stochastic, or ATR can behave differently because the volatility and price scale are altered retroactively.
- Around split dates you can get weird “spikes” or smoothed values that never existed in real trading.
- Some strategies (especially price-action, breakout, or level-based systems) rely on actual nominal prices.

**When adjusted data is actually better:**
- Long-term trend following or performance measurement (avoids huge discontinuities).
- Volatility or correlation studies over many years.
- Most public charting platforms (TradingView, etc.) default to split-adjusted for clean charts.

**When unadjusted (raw) is preferable for your use case:**
- Short-to-medium term indicators and signals.
- Backtesting realistic execution (what price could you actually have traded at?).
- Strategies sensitive to specific price levels or patterns that occurred in real time.
- Detecting or reacting to corporate actions themselves.

**Edge cases to watch:**
- Stocks with frequent/reverse splits (NVDA, TSLA, AAPL historically) — the difference is dramatic.
- High-dividend stocks — cumulative dividend adjustments can shift older prices noticeably.
- Very old data — adjustments compound over decades.
- Intraday vs daily — unadjusted matters more on daily for most TA.

### Practical Recommendations for Your Workflow

1. **Fetch both versions** and compare them side-by-side for your symbols and indicator set. Plot raw vs adjusted close and overlay your indicators — you’ll immediately see where they diverge.
2. **Test signal quality** — Run your indicator logic on unadjusted data and measure win rate, drawdowns, or whatever metrics matter. Many people discover that unadjusted produces cleaner or more actionable signals for their specific style.
3. **Hybrid approach** (common among serious quants):
   - Use **unadjusted** prices for signal generation and entry/exit logic.
   - Use **adjusted** (or split-adjusted) for position sizing, risk management, and PnL calculations.
4. **Verify around known corporate actions** — Check a few split/dividend dates manually. The divergence between unadjusted `close` and standard `adjClose` will show you exactly what FMP adjusted.

### Other Notes

- Free tier has rate limits — for heavy historical work you may need a paid plan.
- Data quality around corporate actions is usually excellent with FMP, but always sanity-check a few known events.
